Chapter 07: Lenses

Comprehensive Study Guide | SSC Science 1

Question 01

Define a Lens and its types.

[Image of convex and concave lenses]
A lens is a transparent material bound by two surfaces, out of which at least one is spherical.
A convex lens is thicker in the middle than at the edges and is also called a converging lens.
A concave lens is thinner in the middle than at the edges and is also called a diverging lens.
Lenses work on the principle of refraction of light as it passes through the transparent medium.
They are commonly made of glass or high-quality plastic with specific refractive indices.
Lenses are used in spectacles, cameras, telescopes, and microscopes to manipulate light.
Question 02

Define Center of Curvature and Radius of Curvature.

The center of the sphere whose parts form the surfaces of the lens is the center of curvature (C).
Since a lens has two spherical surfaces, it has two centers of curvature, $C_1$ and $C_2$.
The radius of the sphere whose parts form the lens surfaces is the radius of curvature (R).
Similar to the center, there are two radii of curvature, $R_1$ and $R_2$.
These points determine the curvature and the focal length of the lens.
The distance from the center of the sphere to the surface is the radius of curvature.
Question 03

Define Principal Axis and Optical Center.

The imaginary line passing through both centers of curvature is called the principal axis.
The optical center (O) is a point inside the lens on the principal axis.
A ray of light passing through the optical center does not suffer any deviation.
It is considered the origin for all distance measurements in the Cartesian sign convention.
For a symmetric lens, the optical center is the exact geometric center of the lens.
All incident rays passing through this point emerge parallel to their original path.
Question 04

Define Principal Focus and Focal Length.

[Image showing principal focus for convex and concave lenses]
For a convex lens, light rays parallel to the principal axis converge to a point called the focus (F).
For a concave lens, parallel rays appear to diverge from a point on the principal axis called the focus (F).
Every lens has two foci, $F_1$ and $F_2$, located on either side of the lens.
The distance between the optical center and the principal focus is the focal length (f).
The focal length of a convex lens is taken as positive in sign conventions.
The focal length of a concave lens is taken as negative in sign conventions.
Question 05

State the rules for drawing ray diagrams for a convex lens.

[Image showing rules for ray diagrams of convex lenses]
If the incident ray is parallel to the principal axis, the refracted ray passes through the focus.
If the incident ray passes through the principal focus, the refracted ray is parallel to the principal axis.
If the incident ray passes through the optical center, it travels without changing its direction.
At least two rays from these rules are needed to locate the image of an object.
The point where the refracted rays intersect (or appear to intersect) is the image position.
These rules are based on the fundamental laws of refraction and the geometry of the lens.
Question 06

Describe the image formed by a convex lens when the object is at infinity.

The incident rays coming from an object at infinity are parallel to the principal axis.
After refraction, these rays converge at the principal focus ($F_2$) on the other side.
The image formed is real, meaning it can be captured on a screen.
The image is inverted (upside down) relative to the object.
The size of the image is highly diminished, appearing as a point.
This setup is used in simple burning glasses and astronomical telescopes.
Question 07

Describe the image when the object is beyond 2F1 in a convex lens.

The object is placed at a finite distance further than twice the focal length.
The image is formed between $F_2$ and $2F_2$ on the opposite side of the lens.
The nature of the image is real and inverted.
The size of the image is diminished compared to the actual object.
This position is commonly used in camera lenses to capture large scenes on a small sensor.
As the object moves closer to $2F_1$, the image moves further away from $F_2$.
Question 08

Describe the image when the object is at 2F1 in a convex lens.

The object is placed exactly at twice the focal length from the optical center.
The refracted rays meet exactly at $2F_2$ on the other side of the lens.
The image formed is real and inverted in nature.
The size of the image is exactly the same as the size of the object.
The magnification produced in this specific case is exactly -1.
This position is used in terrestrial telescopes to invert images without changing size.
Question 09

Describe the image when the object is between F1 and 2F1.

The object is situated between the focus and twice the focal length.
The image is formed beyond $2F_2$ on the other side of the lens.
The nature of the image is real and inverted.
The size of the image is magnified (larger than the object).
This principle is utilized in film projectors to show large images on a screen.
Moving the object closer to $F_1$ increases the magnification and image distance.
Question 10

Describe the image when the object is at F1 in a convex lens.

The object is placed exactly at the principal focus of the lens.
The refracted rays emerge parallel to each other.
Theoretically, these parallel rays meet at infinity.
The image formed is real, inverted, and highly magnified.
This position is used in searchlights and spotlights to produce a parallel beam of light.
The image cannot be clearly captured on a nearby screen due to its distance.
Question 11

Describe the image when the object is between O and F1.

This is the only case where a convex lens produces a virtual image.
The object is placed very close to the lens, within its focal length.
The refracted rays diverge, so they appear to meet on the same side as the object.
The image formed is virtual, erect (upright), and magnified.
This is the principle behind the working of a simple microscope or magnifying glass.
The image can be seen through the lens but cannot be projected onto a screen.
Question 12

Explain the image formation by a concave lens.

[Image of image formation in concave lens]
A concave lens always produces a virtual, erect, and diminished image.
Regardless of the object's position, the image is formed on the same side as the object.
The image is always located between the optical center and the focus.
As the object moves closer to the lens, the image also moves closer to the lens.
The size of the image increases slightly as the object approaches but remains diminished.
Concave lenses are used to correct myopia (near-sightedness).
Question 13

State the Cartesian Sign Convention for lenses.

[Image showing Cartesian sign convention for lenses]
The optical center (O) is taken as the origin of the coordinate system.
The principal axis is taken as the X-axis of the system.
Distances measured in the direction of incident light (right) are positive.
Distances measured against the direction of incident light (left) are negative.
Heights measured perpendicular to and above the principal axis are positive.
Heights measured perpendicular to and below the principal axis are negative.
Question 14

State the Lens Formula and its significance.

The lens formula gives the relationship between object distance (u), image distance (v), and focal length (f).
The mathematical expression is: $(1/v) - (1/u) = (1/f)$.
This formula is applicable to both convex and concave lenses.
It is used to calculate the position and nature of the image formed.
The values of u, v, and f must be substituted with their proper signs.
It assumes that the lens is thin and the aperture is small.
Question 15

Define Magnification (M) of a lens.

Magnification is the ratio of the height of the image ($h_2$) to the height of the object ($h_1$).
The formula is $M = h_2 / h_1$.
It is also related to distances as $M = v / u$.
A positive magnification indicates a virtual and erect image.
A negative magnification indicates a real and inverted image.
If $|M| > 1$, the image is magnified; if $|M| < 1$, it is diminished.
Question 16

Define Power of a Lens and its unit.

Power (P) is the ability of a lens to converge or diverge light rays.
It is defined as the numerical reciprocal of the focal length (f) in meters.
The formula is $P = 1/f$ (where f is in meters).
The SI unit of power of a lens is Dioptre (D).
1 Dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 meter.
Power is positive for convex lenses and negative for concave lenses.
Question 17

Explain the combination of lenses.

When lenses are placed in contact, their total power is the algebraic sum of individual powers.
The formula is $P_{total} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \dots$
This helps in obtaining a desired magnification or sharpness.
The effective focal length is given by $(1/F) = (1/f_1) + (1/f_2) + \dots$
Lens combinations are used extensively in camera lenses and compound microscopes.
This method reduces optical aberrations and improves image quality.
Question 18

Describe the structure of the Human Eye.

[Image of the structure of human eye]
The eye is a spherical organ protected by a tough white layer called the sclera.
Light enters through a transparent front part called the cornea.
Behind the cornea is the iris, a dark muscular diaphragm that controls the pupil size.
The pupil is the small opening that regulates the amount of light entering the eye.
The crystalline lens is a double convex transparent layer that focuses light.
The retina is the light-sensitive inner screen where images are formed.
Question 19

What is the function of the Ciliary Muscles?

Ciliary muscles hold the eye lens in its position.
They help in changing the curvature of the eye lens to adjust focal length.
When the eye looks at distant objects, these muscles relax, making the lens thin.
When looking at nearby objects, the muscles contract, making the lens thick and rounded.
This process of adjusting the focal length is called accommodation.
The flexibility of these muscles decreases with age, leading to vision problems.
Question 20

Define Accommodation and the Near Point.

Accommodation is the ability of the eye lens to change its focal length.
This allows the eye to see both distant and near objects clearly on the retina.
The minimum distance at which an object is seen clearly without strain is the near point.
For a healthy young adult, the near point of the eye is $25 \text{ cm}$.
Objects placed closer than $25 \text{ cm}$ appear blurred to the normal eye.
The lens reaches its maximum possible thickness at the near point.
Question 21

Define the Far Point of the eye.

The farthest distance up to which an object can be seen clearly is the far point.
For a normal human eye, the far point is at infinity.
When looking at the far point, the ciliary muscles are in a fully relaxed state.
The eye lens is thinnest and has its maximum focal length at this stage.
In some defects like myopia, the far point shifts closer to the eye.
A clear far point is essential for tasks like driving and observing nature.
Question 22

Explain Myopia (Near-sightedness).

[Image showing myopia and its correction using a concave lens]
A person with myopia can see nearby objects clearly but distant objects appear blurred.
The far point of the eye is no longer at infinity but shifts closer.
The image of a distant object is formed in front of the retina instead of on it.
Causes include excessive curvature of the cornea or elongation of the eyeball.
This defect is corrected by using a concave lens of suitable focal length.
The concave lens diverges the rays so they can focus exactly on the retina.
Question 23

Explain Hypermetropia (Far-sightedness).

[Image showing hypermetropia and its correction using a convex lens]
A person can see distant objects clearly but nearby objects appear blurred.
The near point of the eye shifts further away from the standard $25 \text{ cm}$.
The image of a nearby object is formed behind the retina.
Causes include the eyeball becoming too small or the lens being too thin.
This defect is corrected by using a convex lens of suitable power.
The convex lens provides additional converging power to focus the image on the retina.
Question 24

Explain Presbyopia.

Presbyopia is a vision defect that occurs generally due to aging.
The ciliary muscles lose their flexibility and cannot change the lens shape easily.
People find it difficult to see nearby objects clearly (similar to hypermetropia).
Sometimes a person may suffer from both myopia and hypermetropia.
This is corrected using bifocal lenses, which have both concave and convex parts.
The upper part of a bifocal lens is concave, and the lower part is convex.
Question 25

Describe the use of a Simple Microscope.

A simple microscope consists of a single convex lens of small focal length.
The object is placed within the focal length (between O and $F_1$) of the lens.
It produces a virtual, erect, and highly magnified image of the object.
It can provide a magnification of up to 20 times the original size.
It is used by watchmakers, jewelers, and for reading small print.
The final image is formed at the least distance of distinct vision ($25 \text{ cm}$).
Question 26

Describe the construction of a Compound Microscope.

It consists of two convex lenses: the objective lens and the eye lens.
The objective lens has a small aperture and a small focal length.
The eye lens has a larger aperture and a larger focal length than the objective.
The objective lens forms a real, inverted, and magnified image of the tiny object.
This first image acts as an object for the eye lens, which magnifies it further.
The final image is virtual, inverted relative to the original object, and highly magnified.
Question 27

Describe the working of a Refracting Telescope.

A telescope is used to see distant objects like stars and planets clearly.
It uses two convex lenses: a large objective and a smaller eye lens.
The objective lens has a large focal length and aperture to collect more light.
It forms a real and inverted image of the distant object at its focus.
The eye lens then magnifies this image to produce a large virtual image.
Refracting telescopes are limited by the size and weight of the large glass lenses.
Question 28

What is Persistence of Vision?

The image of an object formed on the retina does not disappear immediately.
It remains on the retina for about $1/16$-th of a second after the object is removed.
If multiple images are shown faster than this rate, the eye perceives continuous motion.
This phenomenon is called the persistence of vision.
This principle is the basis for cinematography, movies, and television.
It allows our brain to bridge the gap between static frames.
Question 29

Explain the function of Rods and Cones in the retina.

[Image showing rod and cone cells in the retina]
The retina contains millions of light-sensitive cells called photoreceptors.
Rod cells are sensitive to the intensity of light (dim or bright light).
They help us see in low-light conditions but do not detect color.
Cone cells are sensitive to the frequency of light, which we perceive as color.
Different cones respond to red, green, and blue light respectively.
Absence or defect in specific cone cells leads to color blindness.
Question 30

What is Color Blindness?

Color blindness is a genetic condition where a person cannot distinguish between certain colors.
It occurs due to the lack of specific color-sensitive cone cells in the retina.
The most common type is red-green color blindness.
Apart from color perception, the vision of such individuals is otherwise normal.
There is no permanent cure for this condition as it is usually hereditary.
It can be a limiting factor in certain professions like pilot or electrical engineer.
