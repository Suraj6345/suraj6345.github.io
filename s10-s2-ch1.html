Chapter 01: Heredity and Evolution

Question 01

Define Heredity.

Heredity is the transfer of biological characters from one generation to another via genes.
It explains why offspring resemble their parents in physical and mental traits.
Johann Gregor Mendel is considered the pioneer of modern genetics.
It involves the transmission of DNA from the parents' gametes to the zygote.
Heredity is responsible for the maintenance of species characteristics over time.
Mechanism of heredity helps in understanding the cause of many genetic disorders.
Question 02

What is Transcription?

It is the process of synthesis of mRNA from the DNA strand.
The sequence of nucleotides in mRNA is complementary to the DNA strand used for synthesis.
Only one of the two strands of DNA is used in this process.
The enzyme RNA polymerase plays a vital role in the formation of mRNA.
This process occurs inside the nucleus of the eukaryotic cell.
The synthesized mRNA then leaves the nucleus and enters the cytoplasm.
Question 03

Define Translation.

It is the process where the code on mRNA is converted into a chain of amino acids.
tRNA molecules bring specific amino acids as per the codons on the mRNA.
Each tRNA has an 'anticodon' that matches the 'codon' on the mRNA.
The ribosome moves along the mRNA strand during this process.
Peptide bonds are formed between the amino acids brought by the tRNA.
This process continues until a 'stop codon' is reached on the mRNA.
Question 04

What is Translocation?

It is the movement of the ribosome from one end of mRNA to the other.
The ribosome shifts by the distance of one triplet codon at a time.
This movement is essential for the continuous reading of the genetic code.
It ensures that amino acids are added in the correct sequence to the protein chain.
Energy in the form of GTP is utilized during this ribosomal movement.
Translocation is a sub-step of the broader process of protein synthesis.
Question 05

Define Mutation.

A mutation is any sudden and permanent change in the nucleotide sequence of a gene.
It can involve the replacement, addition, or deletion of a single nucleotide.
Mutations can be minor or major enough to cause visible changes in traits.
Genetic disorders like Sickle Cell Anemia are caused by such mutations.
Mutations provide the raw material for variation and evolution.
They can occur naturally or be induced by environmental factors like radiation.
Question 06

Explain the concept of Central Dogma.

Central Dogma is the flow of genetic information from DNA to proteins.
It states that proteins are synthesized by DNA through the medium of RNA.
The process involves two main stages: Transcription and Translation.
DNA holds the master blueprint, while RNA acts as the messenger.
It is a universal process occurring in all living organisms.
Understanding this dogma is fundamental to the study of molecular biology.
Question 07

What is a Triplet Codon?

It is a sequence of three nucleotides on the mRNA that codes for a specific amino acid.
There are 64 possible triplet codons formed from the four nitrogen bases.
Dr. Har Gobind Khorana made significant contributions to discovering these codons.
Each codon is specific for one particular amino acid (Genetic Code).
AUG is a famous 'start codon' that also codes for Methionine.
Three specific codons act as 'stop codons' to terminate protein synthesis.
Question 08

Define Evolution.

Evolution is the gradual and continuous change occurring in living organisms over a long duration.
It leads to the formation of new species from pre-existing ones.
It is a multi-dimensional process involving physical, chemical, and biological changes.
Evolution explains the vast diversity of life present on Earth today.
It is a very slow process that takes millions of years to show significant results.
The theory suggests that all living organisms share a common ancestor.
Question 09

What are Morphological Evidences of evolution?

These involve similarities in the external features of different animals.
Examples include the structure of the mouth, position of eyes, and nostril shape.
Similarities in ear pinnae and hair distribution on the body are also considered.
These similarities suggest that different groups of animals share a common origin.
It is the most visible form of evidence used to classify organisms.
Morphological traits help in tracing the evolutionary path of a species.
Question 10

What are Anatomical Evidences of evolution?

These refer to similarities in the internal bone structure and arrangement of organs.
For example, the structure of a human hand, cat's foreleg, and whale's flipper.
Although their functions are different, the underlying bone arrangement is similar.
This suggests that these animals evolved from a common ancestor with a similar structure.
These organs are often called homologous organs.
Anatomical evidence provides a deeper understanding of evolutionary relationships.
Question 11

Define Vestigial Organs.

These are degenerated or underdeveloped organs that have no function in an organism.
They were functional in the ancestors but became useless due to environmental changes.
In humans, the appendix is a vestigial organ as we cannot digest cellulose.
The tailbone (coccyx) and wisdom teeth are other examples in humans.
Ear pinna muscles are useless to humans but functional in monkeys to move ears.
The presence of these organs proves that organisms undergo structural changes over time.
Question 12

Explain Paleontological Evidences.

Paleontology is the study of fossils preserved in the Earth's crust.
Fossils are the remnants or impressions of organisms that lived in the past.
They provide direct evidence of the types of life that existed in different eras.
The depth at which a fossil is found indicates the geological period it belongs to.
Carbon dating is used to determine the exact age of these fossils.
This evidence shows the transition from simple to complex organisms over time.
Question 13

What is Carbon Dating?

It is a method used to determine the age of biological samples like fossils.
It is based on the radioactive decay of the Carbon-14 ($C^{14}$) isotope.
When an organism dies, it stops consuming carbon, and $C^{14}$ begins to decay.
The ratio of $C^{14}$ to $C^{12}$ (stable isotope) changes continuously over time.
By measuring this ratio, the time since the death of the organism can be calculated.
This technique was developed by Willard Libby and is crucial for paleontology.
Question 14

Define Connecting Links.

Connecting links are organisms that show characteristics of two different groups.
They act as "evolutionary bridges" between two distinct classes of animals.
Peripatus is a connecting link between Annelida and Arthropoda.
Duck-billed Platypus is a connecting link between Reptiles and Mammals.
Lungfish is a connecting link between Fishes and Amphibians.
They provide strong evidence that one group of animals evolved from another.
Question 15

What are Embryological Evidences?

[Image comparing initial and final stages of vertebrate embryos]
This involves the comparative study of embryos of different vertebrates.
In the initial stages, embryos of fish, salamanders, tortoises, and humans look identical.
As the embryos develop, they begin to show the specific features of their species.
The similarity in early stages suggests a common ancestry for all vertebrates.
This is summarized by the phrase "Ontogeny recapitulates Phylogeny."
Embryology helps in understanding the developmental history of a species.
Question 16

State Darwin's Theory of Natural Selection.

Charles Darwin published his theory in the book "Origin of Species."
He stated that all organisms reproduce prolifically, leading to a struggle for existence.
Only those organisms that possess favorable variations survive (Survival of the Fittest).
The "unfit" organisms are eliminated by nature through various pressures.
Nature "selects" the organisms with the best traits to reproduce.
These selected traits are then passed on to the next generation.
Question 17

What are the objections to Darwin’s Theory?

Natural selection is not the only factor responsible for evolution.
Darwin did not explain the origin of useful and useless modifications.
The theory does not explain the mechanism of slow versus sudden changes.
It does not provide information about the source of genetic variations.
Many scientists felt that Darwin focused too much on competition and not cooperation.
Despite these objections, Darwin's work remains a milestone in biological science.
Question 18

Explain Lamarckism (Theory of Inheritance of Acquired Characters).

Jean-Baptiste Lamarck proposed that morphological changes are due to activities or laziness.
He called this the principle of 'use or disuse of organs.'
For example, a giraffe's neck became long due to stretching for leaves over generations.
The iron-smith has strong shoulders because of constant heavy hammering work.
These acquired characters are supposedly passed on to the next generation.
However, the idea that acquired traits can be inherited was later disproved.
Question 19

Why was Lamarckism rejected?

Science proved that changes occurring in the body (somatic cells) are not inherited.
Only those changes that occur in the germ cells (DNA) can be passed to offspring.
If a person develops strong muscles through exercise, their child is not born with them.
Experiments with mice (cutting tails for generations) showed offspring are still born with tails.
The theory failed to distinguish between genetic and environmental influences.
While the idea of 'use/disuse' was interesting, the inheritance part was incorrect.
Question 20

Define Speciation.

Speciation is the formation of new species from existing ones through evolution.
A species is a group of organisms that can produce fertile offspring through natural reproduction.
It can occur due to geographical isolation, such as mountains or oceans.
Reproductive isolation also leads to the development of new species over time.
Changes in food habits, habitat, and genetic makeup contribute to speciation.
It is the process that accounts for the immense biodiversity on our planet.
Question 21

Explain the evolution of Mankind.

Human evolution began roughly 7 crore years ago after the disappearance of dinosaurs.
Monkey-like animals evolved from ancient lemur-like ancestors.
The tails of these ancestors disappeared about 4 crore years ago.
"Ramapithecus" from East Africa was one of the first human-like apes.
The brain size increased, and they started walking upright (bipedal locomotion).
"Cro-Magnon man" evolved about 50,000 years ago, marking the start of modern humans.
Question 22

Who was the Neanderthal Man?

Neanderthal man is considered the first example of "Wise-man" (Homo sapiens).
They lived approximately 1,50,000 years ago in parts of Europe and Asia.
They had a larger brain capacity and used sophisticated stone tools.
They were the first to develop the practice of burying their dead.
Evidence suggests they used fire and lived in social groups or caves.
They eventually became extinct, possibly due to competition with modern humans.
Question 23

What is the importance of the Appendix in humans?

In humans, the appendix is a vestigial organ with no significant digestive function.
It is a small, finger-like projection located at the junction of the small and large intestines.
In ruminant animals (like cows), it is fully functional and helps digest cellulose.
In humans, it sometimes gets inflamed, leading to a condition called appendicitis.
Some recent theories suggest it might store beneficial gut bacteria.
Evolutionarily, it proves our ancestors had a diet much higher in raw plant matter.
Question 24

Explain the process of Protein Synthesis in brief.

The process begins with transcription where mRNA is formed from a DNA template.
mRNA carries the genetic code from the nucleus to the cytoplasm.
Ribosomes attach to the mRNA to begin the translation process.
tRNA molecules bring amino acids corresponding to the mRNA codons.
Amino acids are linked by peptide bonds to form a polypeptide chain.
The chain folds into a specific 3D shape to become a functional protein.
Question 25

Define a Gene.

A gene is a segment of DNA that contains information for a specific protein.
It is the basic physical and functional unit of heredity.
Genes are located in a linear fashion on the chromosomes.
They control all physical and physiological characteristics of an organism.
An organism inherits two copies of each gene, one from each parent.
Changes or mutations in genes lead to variations in the population.
Question 26

What is the role of tRNA?

tRNA stands for transfer RNA and acts as an adapter molecule.
Its main job is to carry specific amino acids to the ribosome.
One end of the tRNA has an anticodon that is complementary to the mRNA codon.
The other end of the tRNA binds to a specific amino acid.
It ensures that the correct amino acid is added to the growing protein chain.
There is at least one type of tRNA for each of the 20 amino acids.
Question 27

What is the significance of Fossils?

Fossils allow us to reconstruct the history of life on Earth.
They show the existence of extinct animals like dinosaurs and mammoths.
They help in determining the chronological order of evolutionary events.
Fossils provide evidence of the climate and environment of ancient times.
They reveal the "missing links" between different groups of organisms.
They are the most convincing evidence for the theory of evolution.
Question 28

Explain the Wiseman (Homo sapiens) stages.

The journey started with the Australopithecus, who had a small brain but walked upright.
Homo habilis was the "skilled man" who first used primitive tools.
Homo erectus walked perfectly upright and discovered the use of fire.
Neanderthal man showed increased brain size and social behaviors.
Cro-Magnon man was anatomically identical to modern humans.
Agricultural and cultural development started around 10,000 years ago.
Question 29

What is the contribution of the Wiseman to civilization?

Humans started practicing agriculture about 10,000 years ago.
They began domesticating animals for food, transport, and protection.
The development of language allowed for the sharing of knowledge across generations.
They created art, music, and complex social structures.
Scientific and technological advancements led to the modern industrial era.
Human intelligence has allowed us to manipulate the environment unlike any other species.
Question 30

Describe the structure of DNA briefly.

DNA is a double-stranded helical structure made of nucleotides.
Each nucleotide consists of a sugar, a phosphate, and a nitrogenous base.
The four nitrogenous bases are Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine, and Thymine.
Adenine always pairs with Thymine, and Guanine pairs with Cytosine.
The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between the bases.
It carries the genetic instructions for the development and functioning of all organisms.
Question 31

What is the role of RNA in heredity?

RNA acts as a messenger between DNA and the protein-making machinery.
mRNA (messenger RNA) carries the genetic code from the nucleus.
rRNA (ribosomal RNA) forms the structural part of the ribosome.
tRNA (transfer RNA) brings amino acids for protein synthesis.
In some viruses, RNA acts as the primary genetic material.
RNA is essential for expressing the characters stored in the DNA.
Question 32

Explain "Survival of the Fittest."

It is the core concept of Darwin's theory of natural selection.
It does not necessarily mean the strongest or fastest, but the best adapted.
Organisms with traits suited to their environment are more likely to survive.
These survivors reproduce and pass their "fit" genes to the next generation.
Organisms that cannot adapt to changes in the environment eventually die out.
This process leads to the gradual improvement and evolution of the species.
Question 33

What are the nitrogenous bases in RNA?

RNA contains four nitrogenous bases: Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine, and Uracil.
Unlike DNA, RNA contains Uracil instead of Thymine.
Adenine pairs with Uracil during the transcription process.
Guanine always pairs with Cytosine, similar to DNA.
These bases are part of the nucleotides that make up the single-stranded RNA.
The sequence of these bases determines the genetic information on the mRNA.
Question 34

Explain the significance of the tailbone in humans.

The coccyx or tailbone is a small triangular structure at the base of the spine.
In our primate ancestors, it was part of a functional tail used for balance.
As humans began walking upright, the need for a tail disappeared.
The tail shortened and fused into the modern vestigial coccyx.
It still serves as an attachment point for various muscles and ligaments.
Its presence is a clear anatomical evidence of our primate ancestry.
Question 35

What is the difference between Transcription and Translation?

Transcription happens in the nucleus; Translation happens in the cytoplasm.
Transcription produces mRNA; Translation produces a protein chain.
Transcription uses DNA as a template; Translation uses mRNA as a template.
The main enzyme in transcription is RNA polymerase; ribosomes are the "factories" for translation.
Transcription involves nitrogen base pairing; Translation involves codon-anticodon matching.
Transcription is the first step of gene expression; Translation is the final step.
Question 36

Explain the concept of 'Geographical Isolation'.

It occurs when a population is divided by a physical barrier like a river or mountain.
The two groups can no longer interbreed with each other.
Each group faces different environmental conditions and selection pressures.
Over time, genetic mutations and variations accumulate differently in both groups.
Eventually, the groups become so different that they cannot interbreed even if reunited.
This is a primary driver for the formation of new species (Speciation).
Question 37

What is the "Theory of Use and Disuse"?

This theory was proposed by Lamarck as the basis for evolution.
It states that if an organ is used constantly, it becomes stronger and more developed.
If an organ is not used, it gradually weakens and eventually disappears.
The example of the blacksmith's strong arms illustrates the "use" part.
The lack of legs in snakes (due to burrowing) illustrates the "disuse" part.
While logically appealing, it lacks a genetic mechanism for inheritance.
Question 38

Define a Species.

A species is the basic unit of biological classification.
It consists of a group of organisms that are physically similar.
The most important criterion is the ability to interbreed in nature.
They must produce fertile offspring (offspring that can also reproduce).
Members of a species share a common gene pool.
Different species are reproductively isolated from each other.
Question 39

What is the role of ribosomes in translocation?

Ribosomes act as the platform where protein synthesis takes place.
They hold the mRNA and the tRNA in the correct positions.
During translocation, the ribosome moves exactly one triplet codon forward.
This movement allows the next codon on the mRNA to be read.
It ensures the peptide chain is built one amino acid at a time without skips.
Without this coordinated movement, protein synthesis would stop or become faulty.
Question 40

Why is human evolution considered a continuous process?

Humans are still subjected to natural selection and environmental pressures.
Our diet, lifestyle, and environment continue to change, forcing adaptations.
Genetic mutations occur in every generation, introducing new variations.
Recent changes include the loss of wisdom teeth in many individuals.
Resistance to certain diseases is an example of ongoing genetic evolution.
As long as there is reproduction and environmental change, evolution continues.
Question 41

What is the function of the Iris and Pupil? (General knowledge related to evolution)

The iris is the colored part of the eye that regulates the size of the pupil.
The pupil is the opening that allows light to enter the eye.
This mechanism evolved to protect the retina from excessive light.
It also allows for better vision in low-light conditions.
Complex eyes evolved through millions of years from simple light-sensitive spots.
The similarity in eye structure across vertebrates points to a shared evolution.
Question 42

How does the position of nostrils provide morphological evidence?

The position of nostrils is similar in various groups of mammals.
This suggests that these animals are adapted to similar breathing mechanisms.
It indicates that they originated from a common land-dwelling ancestor.
In aquatic mammals like whales, the nostrils shifted to the top (blowholes) over time.
Comparative study of nostril placement helps scientists map the transition of species.
It is one of the many external traits used to establish evolutionary links.
Question 43

What is the importance of the Peripatus?

Peripatus is a unique animal known as a "walking worm."
It shows segmented body and thin cuticle, which are characters of Annelida.
It also shows tracheal respiration and open circulatory system, like Arthropoda.
It proves that Arthropods evolved from Annelids.
Because it bridges these two major phyla, it is a classic connecting link.
It provides a "snapshot" of evolution in progress.
Question 44

Explain the 'Survival of the Fittest' in the context of disease.

When a population is exposed to a new disease, some individuals may have natural resistance.
These individuals have a higher chance of surviving the outbreak.
The survivors then reproduce and pass the resistance genes to their children.
Over time, the entire population becomes resistant to that disease.
Those who were susceptible (unfit) were eliminated from the gene pool.
This is natural selection acting on a microscopic and genetic level.
Question 45

What is the role of Dr. Har Gobind Khorana?

He was an Indian-American biochemist who won the Nobel Prize.
He played a key role in deciphering the genetic code.
He helped identify which triplet codons correspond to which amino acids.
He also synthesized the first artificial gene in a laboratory.
His work confirmed that the genetic code is universal for all life.
His research laid the foundation for modern biotechnology and genetic engineering.
Question 46

How do wisdom teeth act as vestigial organs?

Wisdom teeth are the third set of molars that appear in late adolescence.
Our ancestors needed them to chew tough, raw plants and hard nuts.
As humans discovered fire and started cooking, food became softer.
The human jaw has gradually become smaller over thousands of years.
Now, there is often no space for these teeth, causing pain or misalignment.
They are no longer necessary for survival and are considered vestigial.
Question 47

What are the characters of the Apes that evolved into humans?

They lived in the forests of Africa about 2 crore years ago.
They had large brains compared to other animals of their time.
They used their hands for eating and performing simple tasks.
Due to thinning forests, they started living on the ground and walking upright.
Their lumbar bones developed to support a vertical posture.
This transition from forest-dwellers to ground-dwellers was the birth of the human line.
Question 48

Why are homologous organs significant?

Homologous organs have the same basic structure but different functions.
They provide evidence that different species are related to one another.
They show how a single ancestral structure can be modified for different needs.
For example, a bird's wing and a human's arm are used for flying and grasping.
Yet, both have the same bones (humerus, radius, ulna, etc.) in their structure.
This divergent evolution is a key evidence in anatomical studies.
Question 49

What is the role of the Environment in Evolution?

The environment acts as a selection pressure on all living organisms.
Changes in climate, food availability, or predators force species to adapt.
Only those with mutations that help them survive in the new environment will live.
Environment determines which traits are "favorable" and which are not.
Extreme environmental changes can lead to mass extinctions.
Evolution is essentially a response to the ever-changing conditions of the Earth.
Question 50

Summarize the importance of the study of Evolution.

It helps us understand our own origins and our place in the natural world.
It explains the biological basis for the similarities between all living things.
It is crucial for medicine, as it explains how bacteria and viruses evolve.
It aids in the conservation of endangered species by understanding their needs.
Evolution provides a scientific framework for the classification of life.
It allows us to predict how life might respond to future changes like global warming.
