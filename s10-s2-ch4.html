Chapter 04: Environmental Management

Question 1

What is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a self-sustaining unit formed by biotic and abiotic factors.
Biotic factors include all living organisms like plants, animals, and microbes.
Abiotic factors include non-living components like air, water, soil, and sunlight.
There is a continuous interaction and exchange of energy between these factors.
Every organism plays a specific role, such as producer, consumer, or decomposer.
The balance of an ecosystem is vital for the survival of all species within it.
Question 2

Define the role of Producers in an ecosystem.

Producers, mainly green plants, are the primary source of food in an ecosystem.
They convert solar energy into chemical energy through photosynthesis.
They provide nourishment to all levels of consumers in the food chain.
They release oxygen into the atmosphere as a byproduct of their metabolism.
Without producers, the flow of energy in the biological world would stop.
They form the base of the ecological pyramid in any stable environment.
Question 3

What are Herbivores?

Herbivores are primary consumers that feed directly on plants.
They include animals like deer, rabbits, elephants, and grasshoppers.
They convert plant energy into animal biomass for higher trophic levels.
They possess specialized digestive systems to break down complex cellulose.
They help in controlling the overgrowth of vegetation in a habitat.
Herbivores are essential links between producers and carnivorous predators.
Question 4

Define Carnivores.

Carnivores are secondary or tertiary consumers that feed on other animals.
They are adapted for hunting and eating meat with sharp teeth or claws.
They help maintain the population of herbivores, preventing overgrazing.
Predators like lions, tigers, and hawks are typical examples of carnivores.
They occupy the higher levels of the food chain or energy pyramid.
Their presence is an indicator of a healthy and balanced ecosystem.
Question 5

What is the role of Decomposers?

Decomposers include bacteria and fungi that break down dead organic matter.
They convert complex organic compounds into simple inorganic nutrients.
These nutrients are returned to the soil, making them available for plants.
They act as the "cleaners" of the environment by removing waste.
Without them, the Earth would be covered in layers of dead organisms.
They ensure the continuous cycling of matter through the biogeochemical cycles.
Question 6

Define a Food Chain.

[Image of a food chain showing producers, consumers, and decomposers]
A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms showing who eats whom.
It starts with a producer and ends with a top predator or decomposer.
Each step in the food chain is known as a "trophic level."
It demonstrates the unidirectional flow of energy in an ecosystem.
Typically, food chains are simple and contain 3 to 5 levels.
Any disruption in one link can affect all subsequent levels in the chain.
Question 7

What is a Food Web?

A food web is a complex network of interconnected food chains.
In nature, organisms rarely depend on a single food source.
It provides alternative pathways for energy flow if one species declines.
A food web represents the true complexity of nutritional relationships.
It increases the stability and resilience of an ecosystem.
All organisms in a habitat are part of a larger, global food web.
Question 8

Explain the 10% Law of energy flow.

This law states that only 10% of energy is transferred to the next trophic level.
The remaining 90% is used for life processes or lost as heat to the environment.
This is why the number of trophic levels is usually limited to four or five.
As we go higher in the pyramid, the total available energy decreases.
Top predators receive the least amount of energy from the original source.
This law was proposed by Raymond Lindeman in 1942.
Question 9

What is Environmental Conservation?

It is the practice of protecting the environment from degradation.
It involves the sustainable use of natural resources like water and forests.
Conservation aims to preserve biodiversity and prevent species extinction.
It requires a collective effort from governments, NGOs, and individuals.
Laws and regulations are enacted to control pollution and habitat loss.
Conservation is essential to ensure a healthy planet for future generations.
Question 10

Why is there a need for Environmental Conservation?

To maintain the ecological balance necessary for all life forms.
To protect the Earth from global warming and climate change.
To ensure a continuous supply of clean air, water, and fertile soil.
To preserve the aesthetic and medicinal value of various plant species.
To prevent natural disasters caused by deforestation and soil erosion.
To fulfill our moral responsibility toward the environment and future kids.
Question 11

Define Pollution.

Pollution is the undesirable change in the physical, chemical, or biological traits of the environment.
It is caused by various substances known as "pollutants."
Human activities like industrialization and urbanization are primary causes.
It affects the health of humans, animals, and plants significantly.
Pollution can occur in air, water, soil, and even through sound or light.
It leads to the depletion of natural resources and environmental degradation.
Question 12

What are Natural Pollutants?

These are harmful substances released by natural events.
Examples include volcanic ash, dust storms, and forest fire smoke.
Pollen grains and fungal spores are also considered natural air pollutants.
Methane gas from decaying organic matter is a natural greenhouse gas.
Most natural pollutants are managed by the Earth's self-cleaning cycles.
However, large-scale events like volcanic eruptions can cause global effects.
Question 13

What are Artificial (Man-made) Pollutants?

These are substances created and released due to human activities.
Industrial chemicals, plastic waste, and pesticides are major examples.
Exhaust gases from vehicles and smoke from factories are air pollutants.
Radioactive waste from nuclear power plants is extremely dangerous.
Sewage and agricultural runoff are primary sources of water pollution.
These pollutants are often difficult for nature to break down or recycle.
Question 14

Mention the effects of Air Pollution.

It causes respiratory diseases like asthma, bronchitis, and lung cancer.
It leads to the formation of acid rain, which damages crops and buildings.
Increased $CO_2$ levels contribute to the greenhouse effect and global warming.
Certain pollutants like CFCs cause the depletion of the ozone layer.
Smog reduces visibility and can cause accidents in urban areas.
Air pollution negatively impacts the photosynthesis process in plants.
Question 15

Mention the effects of Water Pollution.

It leads to waterborne diseases like cholera, typhoid, and dysentery.
Toxic chemicals in water can kill fish and other aquatic organisms.
It causes "Eutrophication," leading to oxygen depletion in water bodies.
Polluted water affects the growth and yield of agricultural crops.
Biomagnification of heavy metals in the food chain harms top predators.
It makes water sources unfit for drinking, washing, and industrial use.
Question 16

What is Soil Pollution?

It is the contamination of soil with harmful chemicals or waste.
Excessive use of chemical fertilizers and pesticides is a major cause.
Industrial waste and plastic dumped in soil reduce its fertility.
It kills beneficial soil microorganisms like earthworms and bacteria.
Pollutants from soil can enter the food chain through plants.
Soil pollution leads to erosion and prevents the growth of vegetation.
Question 17

Define Biodiversity.

Biodiversity is the variety and variability of life forms on Earth.
It includes diversity at three levels: Genetic, Species, and Ecosystem.
It is a measure of the health and stability of an environment.
Biodiversity provides us with food, medicine, and industrial raw materials.
It ensures the functioning of ecosystems through various services.
Protecting biodiversity is a global priority for environmentalists.
Question 18

Explain Genetic Diversity.

It refers to the variation of genes within a single species.
Every individual of a species is slightly different due to genetic makeup.
For example, there are thousands of varieties of rice and mangoes.
Genetic diversity allows a species to adapt to changing environments.
It prevents the species from being wiped out by a single disease.
Higher genetic diversity increases the chances of survival for a population.
Question 19

Explain Species Diversity.

It refers to the variety of different species found in a particular region.
Some areas, like tropical rainforests, have very high species diversity.
It is calculated by the number of species and their relative abundance.
Different species perform different roles, maintaining the ecological balance.
Extinction of one species can trigger a chain reaction affecting others.
Human activities are currently causing a rapid decline in species diversity.
Question 20

Explain Ecosystem Diversity.

it refers to the variety of ecosystems present in a geographical area.
Examples include forests, grasslands, deserts, wetlands, and oceans.
Each ecosystem has its own unique set of biotic and abiotic factors.
A region with multiple ecosystems supports a wider range of life.
It involves the complex interactions between different habitats.
Protecting entire ecosystems is the best way to conserve biodiversity.
Question 21

What are Sacred Groves?

These are patches of forest traditionally protected by local communities.
They are conserved in the name of God or a local deity.
Hunting or cutting trees in these areas is strictly prohibited by tradition.
Sacred groves act as "natural sanctuaries" for many rare plant species.
In India, they are found in the Western Ghats, Rajasthan, and Meghalaya.
They represent an ancient and successful model of community-based conservation.
Question 22

Name some endangered animal species in India.

The Royal Bengal Tiger is a major endangered species in India.
The Asiatic Lion is found only in the Gir Forest of Gujarat.
The One-horned Rhinoceros is native to the marshes of Assam.
The Snow Leopard is found in the high-altitude Himalayan regions.
The Great Indian Bustard is a critically endangered bird in Rajasthan.
Conservation programs like Project Tiger have been launched to protect them.
Question 23

Mention the "3 R's" of environmental management.

Reduce: Lowering the consumption of resources to minimize waste.
Reuse: Using items multiple times instead of discarding them.
Recycle: Processing waste materials into new, useful products.
This hierarchy helps in reducing the burden on landfills and nature.
It promotes the sustainable use of materials like plastic, glass, and paper.
Adopting the 3 R's is a simple way for individuals to help the planet.
Question 24

What are Rare Species?

These are species with small populations that are not yet endangered.
They are often confined to specific geographical areas or habitats.
They are at high risk if their environment changes or is disturbed.
Examples include the Red Panda and the Musk Deer.
They require close monitoring to prevent them from becoming endangered.
Their unique genetic traits are valuable for biological research.
Question 25

What are Vulnerable Species?

These are species whose population is decreasing rapidly.
If the negative factors continue, they are likely to become endangered.
Habitat loss and poaching are the primary reasons for their decline.
Examples include the Lion-tailed Macaque and the Blue Sheep.
Protection measures are needed immediately to stabilize their numbers.
They serve as a "warning sign" of a degrading ecosystem.
Question 26

What are Indeterminate Species?

These are species that appear to be endangered but there is not enough data.
Due to their secretive habits, their exact population is hard to count.
The Giant Squirrel (Shekru) of Maharashtra is a prime example.
They are often shy and hide in dense forests or remote areas.
Scientists are working to collect more information about their status.
They are given protection based on the "precautionary principle."
Question 27

Mention the objectives of the Social Forestry program.

To plant trees on barren lands and around human settlements.
To provide fuel, fodder, and timber to local rural communities.
To reduce the pressure on traditional, dense forest areas.
To improve the local microclimate and control soil erosion.
To generate employment opportunities for the rural population.
To involve the general public in the greening of the country.
Question 28

Define "Environmental Management."

It is the systematic approach to managing human impact on the environment.
It aims to achieve a balance between development and conservation.
It involves planning, monitoring, and implementing environmental policies.
Environmental management uses scientific data to make sustainable choices.
It includes waste management, pollution control, and resource protection.
The goal is to ensure the long-term survival of the biosphere.
Question 29

What is a "Hotspot" of biodiversity?

[Image of global biodiversity hotspots map]
A hotspot is a region with high biodiversity that is under threat.
To be a hotspot, an area must contain at least 1500 endemic plant species.
It must have lost at least 70% of its original primary vegetation.
There are currently 36 biodiversity hotspots identified globally.
In India, the Western Ghats and Eastern Himalayas are major hotspots.
These areas are top priorities for international conservation efforts.
Question 30

Name the acts related to environmental protection in India.

The Wildlife Protection Act (1972) prohibits poaching and trade.
The Forest Conservation Act (1980) regulates the use of forest land.
The Environment Protection Act (1986) provides a framework for pollution control.
The Biological Diversity Act (2002) aims to preserve genetic resources.
The National Green Tribunal Act (2010) handles environmental legal cases.
These laws provide the legal authority to punish environmental offenders.
Question 31

What are the roles of an individual in environmental conservation?

To minimize the use of non-biodegradable materials like plastic.
To save water and electricity in daily household activities.
To plant trees and participate in local greening initiatives.
To spread awareness among friends, family, and the community.
To follow waste segregation (wet and dry waste) at the source.
To support eco-friendly products and sustainable businesses.
Question 32

Who was Jadav Molai Payeng?

He is a famous environmental activist from Assam, India.
He single-handedly planted a forest on a barren sandbar of the Brahmaputra.
The forest, now known as "Molai Forest," covers over 1360 acres.
He started his work in 1979 and continued for over 30 years.
He was awarded the "Padma Shri" for his extraordinary contribution.
His story proves that a single dedicated person can create a massive impact.
Question 33

What is the Chipko Movement?

It was a non-violent social and ecological movement in the 1970s.
It started in the Garhwal Himalayas of Uttarakhand, India.
Local villagers, especially women, hugged trees to prevent them from being cut.
It was led by activists like Sunderlal Bahuguna and Gaura Devi.
The movement forced the government to rethink its forest policies.
It became a symbol of peaceful resistance for environmental causes.
Question 34

Define Global Warming.

It is the long-term rise in the average temperature of Earth's climate.
It is primarily caused by the increase of greenhouse gases in the air.
Greenhouse gases like $CO_2$ and Methane trap heat from the Sun.
Human activities like burning fossil fuels and deforestation are the causes.
It leads to the melting of glaciers and a rise in sea levels.
Global warming results in unpredictable and extreme weather patterns.
Question 35

What are the effects of Global Warming?

Rising sea levels threaten to submerge coastal cities and islands.
Changes in rainfall patterns lead to frequent droughts and floods.
It causes the loss of habitats for many cold-climate animals like polar bears.
Increased temperatures can lead to the spread of tropical diseases.
It affects agricultural productivity, threatening food security worldwide.
Bleaching of coral reefs is a severe consequence of warmer oceans.
Question 36

Explain the Greenhouse Effect.

It is a natural process that warms the Earth's surface.
Sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms the ground.
The Earth radiates this heat back toward space as infrared radiation.
Greenhouse gases trap some of this heat, keeping the planet livable.
Without this effect, the Earth would be too cold for life to exist.
However, human activity has intensified this effect to dangerous levels.
Question 37

What is Acid Rain?

[Image illustrating the formation and effects of acid rain]
Acid rain occurs when sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides mix with water.
These gases are released by burning coal and industrial processes.
They react with atmospheric moisture to form sulfuric and nitric acids.
The rain that falls has a low pH, making it acidic and harmful.
It damages the leaves of trees and makes soil unfit for farming.
It corrodes statues and buildings, like the yellowing of the Taj Mahal.
Question 38

Define Ozone Layer Depletion.

The ozone layer in the stratosphere protects Earth from harmful UV rays.
Chemicals like Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) react with and destroy ozone.
CFCs are found in old refrigerators, ACs, and aerosol sprays.
Depletion leads to the formation of "Ozone Holes" over the poles.
Increased UV radiation causes skin cancer, cataracts, and crop damage.
Global agreements like the Montreal Protocol aim to phase out CFCs.
Question 39

What is Radioactive Pollution?

It is the contamination of the environment with radioactive substances.
Sources include nuclear power plants, medical waste, and atomic tests.
Radioactive particles emit harmful radiation like Alpha, Beta, and Gamma rays.
Radiation causes genetic mutations and diseases like leukemia (blood cancer).
Accidents like Chernobyl and Fukushima have shown its catastrophic impact.
Radioactive waste remains dangerous for thousands of years.
Question 40

What are the advantages of using Bio-pesticides?

They are derived from natural materials like plants, bacteria, and minerals.
They are generally less toxic than chemical pesticides.
They target specific pests and do not harm beneficial insects.
They decompose quickly, leaving no harmful residues in the soil.
Using them prevents soil and water pollution in agricultural areas.
They are an essential part of sustainable "Organic Farming."
Question 41

What is Noise Pollution?

It is the presence of excessive or disturbing sound in the environment.
Sources include loud music, traffic, industrial machinery, and construction.
It is measured in units called "Decibels" (dB).
Prolonged exposure can cause permanent hearing loss and tinnitus.
It leads to psychological stress, high blood pressure, and sleep disorders.
Noise pollution also disturbs the communication and behavior of animals.
Question 42

How does plastic affect the environment?

Plastic is non-biodegradable and stays in the environment for centuries.
It litters our land and clogs drainage systems, causing flooding.
Animals often mistake plastic for food, which leads to their death.
In oceans, it breaks down into "microplastics" that enter the food chain.
Burning plastic releases toxic gases like dioxins into the atmosphere.
Chemical additives in plastic can leach into soil and groundwater.
Question 43

Mention the ways to manage solid waste.

Segregation of waste into biodegradable and non-biodegradable categories.
Composting of organic waste to produce natural fertilizer.
Recycling of materials like paper, glass, metals, and certain plastics.
Incineration (controlled burning) for medical and hazardous waste.
Sanitary landfills for the disposal of non-recyclable materials.
Educating people to minimize waste generation at home.
Question 44

What is "E-waste" and why is it harmful?

E-waste refers to discarded electronic devices like phones and computers.
It contains toxic metals like lead, mercury, cadmium, and arsenic.
If not disposed of properly, these metals can leach into the soil.
They cause severe damage to the human nervous system and kidneys.
E-waste is often shipped to developing countries, causing local pollution.
Proper recycling is needed to recover valuable metals and manage toxins.
Question 45

What is the role of the National Green Tribunal (NGT)?

The NGT is a specialized judicial body for environmental cases in India.
It provides a fast-track procedure for resolving ecological disputes.
It has the power to order compensation for victims of pollution.
It can stop projects that violate environmental laws or safety norms.
The NGT helps reduce the burden on traditional courts.
It ensures that the "Polluter Pays" principle is strictly implemented.
Question 46

What is Wildlife Poaching?

It is the illegal hunting, capturing, or killing of wild animals.
Animals are poached for their skin, fur, tusks, horns, or bones.
It is a major threat to endangered species like tigers and rhinos.
Poaching disrupts the food chain and balance of the ecosystem.
It is often fueled by high demand in the international black market.
Anti-poaching laws and patrols are essential for wildlife survival.
Question 47

Mention the importance of Wetlands.

[Image of a wetland ecosystem]
Wetlands act as natural water filters by trapping pollutants.
They serve as "sponges" that absorb excess water during floods.
They are important habitats for many migratory birds and amphibians.
Wetlands help in recharging the groundwater levels of an area.
They are highly productive ecosystems that support a variety of life.
Protecting wetlands is vital for local climate and water security.
Question 48

What is Deforestation?

It is the large-scale clearing of forests by humans.
Major causes include agriculture, logging, and infrastructure projects.
It leads to the loss of habitat for millions of species.
Deforestation increases $CO_2$ levels, accelerating global warming.
It causes soil erosion and reduces the fertility of the land.
The disruption of the water cycle leads to reduced rainfall and droughts.
Question 49

Define Afforestation.

Afforestation is the process of planting trees on land that was not forested.
It helps in increasing the total forest cover of a country.
It is a powerful tool to combat climate change and air pollution.
Afforestation restores degraded lands and prevents desertification.
It provides new habitats for birds and small animals.
Communities often participate in afforestation through "Van Mahotsav."
Question 50

What is an "Endemic Species"?

These are species found only in one specific geographical area.
They are not found naturally anywhere else in the world.
For example, the Kangaroo is endemic to the continent of Australia.
The Lion-tailed Macaque is endemic to the Western Ghats of India.
They are very vulnerable to extinction if their local habitat is destroyed.
Conservation of endemic species is crucial for maintaining global diversity.
Question 51

What are the symptoms of Environmental Stress on humans?

Increase in respiratory ailments like coughing and wheezing.
Frequent headaches and irritability due to noise and air pollution.
Skin allergies and rashes caused by exposure to toxic chemicals.
Weakened immune systems leading to more frequent infections.
Anxiety and depression related to the loss of natural spaces.
Long-term health issues like hormonal imbalances and cancers.
Question 52

Mention the role of IUCN.

IUCN stands for International Union for Conservation of Nature.
It is the world's oldest and largest global environmental network.
It maintains the "Red List" of threatened and endangered species.
It provides scientific data to help governments draft conservation laws.
IUCN promotes the sustainable use of natural resources worldwide.
It coordinates international efforts to protect the Earth's biodiversity.
Question 53

What is the "Red List"?

It is a document maintained by IUCN that categorizes species by risk.
Categories include Extinct, Endangered, Vulnerable, and Least Concern.
It acts as an early warning system for the extinction of species.
The list helps in prioritizing conservation funding and efforts.
It is updated regularly based on the latest scientific research.
Being on the Red List brings international attention to a species' plight.
Question 54

What is the impact of excessive urbanization on the environment?

Destruction of natural habitats to build roads, buildings, and malls.
Increased demand for energy, leading to higher fossil fuel burning.
Generation of massive amounts of solid waste and sewage.
Over-extraction of groundwater, causing local water scarcity.
Higher levels of air and noise pollution in densely populated areas.
Creation of "Urban Heat Islands" with higher local temperatures.
Question 55

Define Sustainable Development.

It is development that meets current needs without compromising future ones.
It balances economic growth with social and environmental protection.
It encourages the use of renewable energy over fossil fuels.
Sustainable development promotes the efficient use of all resources.
It aims to reduce poverty while protecting the planet's ecosystems.
The UN's "Sustainable Development Goals" (SDGs) provide a roadmap for this.
Question 56

What are Renewable Energy sources?

[Image showing solar, wind, and hydro energy sources]
These are energy sources that are replenished naturally and quickly.
Examples include solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal energy.
They produce little to no greenhouse gas emissions during operation.
Using them reduces our dependence on finite fossil fuels like coal.
They are essential for a clean and sustainable energy future.
Technology for renewable energy is becoming cheaper and more efficient.
Question 57

What are Non-renewable Energy sources?

These are energy sources that take millions of years to form.
Examples include coal, petroleum, and natural gas (fossil fuels).
Burning them releases large amounts of $CO_2$ and pollutants.
Their extraction causes habitat destruction and oil spills.
These resources are limited and will eventually run out.
Shifting away from them is the primary challenge of modern energy.
Question 58

What is the "Polluter Pays Principle"?

It is an environmental law principle where the polluter bears the costs.
The company or person responsible for pollution must pay for the cleanup.
It encourages industries to adopt cleaner technologies to avoid fines.
This principle helps in the restoration of damaged environments.
It serves as a deterrent against irresponsible waste disposal.
In India, it is strictly applied by the National Green Tribunal.
Question 59

Mention the importance of Environmental Education.

It creates awareness about the importance of nature and ecosystems.
It teaches children and adults how to live a more sustainable life.
Education helps people understand the consequences of their actions.
It empowers communities to fight for their local environmental rights.
It is a mandatory part of the curriculum in most modern schools.
Knowledge is the first step toward any meaningful conservation action.
Question 60

What is "Biomagnification"?

[Image of biomagnification in an aquatic food chain]
It is the increase in the concentration of toxins at higher trophic levels.
Toxins like Mercury or DDT enter the food chain through producers.
These chemicals are not easily excreted or broken down by organisms.
As one animal eats another, the toxin concentration multiplies.
Top predators (like eagles or humans) end up with the highest levels.
This can lead to reproductive failure or death in apex species.
Question 61

What are the characters of a Biosphere Reserve?

It is a large area of land or sea protected for conservation.
It consists of three zones: Core, Buffer, and Transition zones.
The Core zone is strictly protected for wildlife and research.
The Buffer zone allows limited human activity like tourism and education.
The Transition zone allows local communities to live and work sustainably.
India has 18 Biosphere Reserves, such as Nilgiri and Nanda Devi.
Question 62

Define "In-situ Conservation".

It is the conservation of species in their natural, original habitat.
Examples include National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, and Sacred Groves.
It allows species to live and evolve in their natural environment.
It protects the entire ecosystem and all the species within it.
This is the most effective and preferred method of conservation.
It requires large areas of land to be legally protected from human use.
Question 63

Define "Ex-situ Conservation".

It is the conservation of species outside their natural habitat.
Examples include Zoos, Botanical Gardens, and Gene Banks.
It is used when a species is at extremely high risk of extinction.
It provides a safe environment for breeding and medical care.
Genetic material like seeds or sperm can be stored for the future.
It serves as a "backup plan" if a species disappears from the wild.
Question 64

What are the effects of radioactive accidents?

Immediate death of nearby organisms due to high-intensity radiation.
Long-term increase in cancer and birth defects in the local population.
Contamination of soil and water, making them unfit for use for centuries.
Total evacuation of towns and cities, creating "ghost towns."
Disruption of local food chains and mass death of wildlife.
Enormous economic costs for containment and long-term monitoring.
Question 65

BNHS is one of the largest non-governmental organizations in India.

It publishes scientific journals and provides data for conservation.

Question 66

What is the impact of fertilizers on water bodies?

[Image showing eutrophication in a lake due to fertilizers]
Rainwater washes excess fertilizers into nearby lakes and rivers.
The high nutrient content causes a rapid growth of algae (Algal Bloom).
This process, called Eutrophication, blocks sunlight from reaching water.
When the algae die, bacteria use up all the oxygen to decompose them.
Fish and other aquatic life die due to the lack of dissolved oxygen.
The water body eventually becomes "dead" and emits foul odors.
Question 67

What is "Desertification"?

[Image showing the process of soil desertification]
It is the process by which fertile land becomes a desert.
Major causes include overgrazing, deforestation, and poor irrigation.
It results in the total loss of vegetation and biodiversity.
The soil becomes sandy, dry, and incapable of supporting life.
It is a significant threat in arid and semi-arid regions of the world.
Combating desertification involves large-scale tree planting and soil care.
Question 68

What are the benefits of Organic Farming?

It avoids the use of harmful chemical fertilizers and pesticides.
It maintains and improves the long-term health of the soil.
Organic products are safer and healthier for human consumption.
It promotes biodiversity by using natural pest control methods.
It reduces the pollution of groundwater and nearby water bodies.
Organic farming is more sustainable and environment-friendly.
Question 69

Define "Carrying Capacity" of an ecosystem.

It is the maximum population of a species an environment can support.
It depends on available resources like food, water, and space.
If the population exceeds this capacity, resources become depleted.
This leads to competition, starvation, and a population crash.
Maintaining populations within this limit is vital for stability.
Human population growth is currently testing the Earth's carrying capacity.
Question 70

What are the dangers of modern industrialization?

Release of massive amounts of greenhouse gases into the air.
Dumping of toxic chemical waste into rivers and oceans.
High demand for raw materials leading to the destruction of forests.
Creation of large landfills filled with non-biodegradable waste.
Increased risk of industrial accidents like gas leaks or oil spills.
Disruption of the traditional lifestyles of local and indigenous people.
Question 71

What is the role of microorganisms in the environment?

They act as decomposers, recycling nutrients back into the soil.
Some bacteria fix atmospheric nitrogen, helping plants to grow.
Microbes help in the treatment of sewage in urban areas.
They are used in "Bioremediation" to clean up oil spills.
They form a crucial part of the food chain in many ecosystems.
Certain microbes help in the production of oxygen in the oceans.
Question 72

Mention the importance of the Tiger in our ecosystem.

The tiger is an "Apex Predator" at the top of the food chain.
It helps in regulating the population of large herbivores like deer.
By controlling herbivores, it protects the forest vegetation.
Protecting tigers requires protecting large areas of forest (Umbrella species).
Tigers are indicators of the health of the entire forest ecosystem.
Their presence ensures the survival of hundreds of other species.
Question 73

What is "Ecotourism"?

It is responsible travel to natural areas that conserves the environment.
It aims to minimize the impact of tourists on the local habitat.
Ecotourism provides financial benefits for local conservation efforts.
It helps in educating visitors about the importance of biodiversity.
It respects the culture and rights of the local indigenous people.
When done correctly, it is a win-win for both people and nature.
Question 74

Define "Biogeochemical Cycles."

These are pathways through which essential elements move through Earth.
Major cycles include the Carbon, Nitrogen, Oxygen, and Water cycles.
Elements move between biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) parts.
These cycles ensure that essential nutrients are never permanently lost.
Human activity, like pollution, can disrupt the balance of these cycles.
Maintaining these cycles is fundamental to the sustainability of life.
Question 75

What is the impact of Mining on the environment?

It causes the total destruction of forests and topsoil in the area.
Mining leads to severe soil and water pollution from toxic minerals.
It creates huge open pits that destroy the local landscape.
The process uses large amounts of water, leading to local scarcity.
It produces massive amounts of "tailings" or waste material.
Dust from mining operations causes respiratory issues for locals.
Question 76
