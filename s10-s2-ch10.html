Chapter 10: Disaster Management (1-100)

1

Define "Disaster."

A disaster is a sudden event that causes huge loss of life and property.
It disrupts the normal functioning of a society or community.
Disasters can be natural (earthquakes) or man-made (wars).
They usually require external help to manage the aftermath.
Disasters are unpredictable and occur with little to no warning.
The impact depends on the intensity and the vulnerability of the area.
2

How are disasters classified based on origin?

Geophysical: Includes geological (earthquakes) and atmospheric (storms).
Biological: Includes plant-related (forest fires) and animal-related (epidemics).
Man-made: Includes intentional (terrorism) and unintentional (gas leaks).
This classification helps in planning specific rescue operations.
Some disasters, like droughts, can be a mix of natural and human factors.
Understanding the origin is key to prevention and mitigation.
3

List example of Geophysical disasters.

Earthquakes: Sudden shaking of the ground due to tectonic shifts.
Volcanoes: Eruption of molten rock and gases from the earth's crust.
Tsunami: Massive sea waves caused by underwater earthquakes.
Landslides: Rapid downward movement of rock or soil on slopes.
Floods: Overflow of water onto land that is normally dry.
Cyclones: Intense circular storms with high-speed winds.
4

List examples of Biological disasters.

Epidemics: Outbreak of infectious diseases like Flu or Cholera.
Pandemics: Global outbreaks of diseases like COVID-19 or AIDS.
Locust attacks: Swarms of insects destroying vast crop areas.
Fungal infections: Diseases affecting plants and reducing food supply.
Poisonous animal bites: Large scale incidents involving snakes or insects.
Forest fires (natural): Fires ignited by lightning or extreme heat.
5

List examples of Man-made disasters.

Wars: Armed conflicts causing mass destruction and migration.
Terrorism: Acts of violence intended to create fear in a population.
Chemical leaks: Accidental release of toxic gases (e.g., Bhopal Gas Tragedy).
Nuclear accidents: Radiation leaks from power plants or weapons.
Bomb blasts: Explosions caused by high-grade explosives in public areas.
Cyber-terrorism: Attacks on digital infrastructure to cause chaos.
6

What are the "Phases of Disaster"?

Pre-disaster phase: Planning, preparation, and warning systems.
During-disaster phase: Immediate rescue and emergency operations.
Post-disaster phase: Rehabilitation, rebuilding, and long-term recovery.
Each phase requires a different set of skills and resources.
Effective management involves coordinating all three phases.
The goal is to reduce loss during and speed up recovery after.
7

What is the "Phase of Emergency"?

This is the most critical time during and immediately after a disaster.
Focus is on saving lives through search and rescue operations.
Providing immediate medical aid and first aid to the injured.
Communication and transport systems are often disrupted.
Food, water, and temporary shelter must be provided urgently.
Every second counts in reducing the death toll during this phase.
8

Explain the "Phase of Rehabilitation."

It begins once the immediate danger has passed.
People are moved from temporary shelters to more permanent ones.
Focus shifts to psychological healing and clearing debris.
Restoring essential services like electricity, water, and roads.
Providing financial aid or loans to help people restart businesses.
This phase bridges the gap between the emergency and full recovery.
9

Explain the "Phase of Reconstruction."

It involves long-term rebuilding of houses and infrastructure.
New structures are built to be "disaster-resistant."
Farming and industrial activities are restarted to stabilize the economy.
It is the longest phase and can take years to complete.
The objective is to make the community more resilient than before.
It includes permanent relocation of villages if the site is too risky.
10

What are the "Objectives of Disaster Management"?

To minimize the loss of human lives and livestock.
To provide immediate relief and first aid to victims.
To facilitate the rehabilitation of the displaced population.
To assess the damage and rebuild infrastructure quickly.
To develop warning systems to prevent future tragedies.
To educate the public on safety measures and mock drills.
11

What is the role of the "National Disaster Response Force" (NDRF)?

It is a specialized force established under the Disaster Management Act, 2005.
They are trained in high-tech rescue operations (e.g., collapsed buildings).
They assist in both natural and man-made disasters across India.
NDRF teams are equipped with dogs, sensors, and heavy-duty cutters.
They work closely with the army, navy, and local police.
There are 12 battalions of NDRF stationed in different parts of India.
12

Explain the "Disaster Management Authority" hierarchy.

National level: Chaired by the Prime Minister (NDMA).
State level: Chaired by the Chief Minister (SDMA).
District level: Chaired by the District Collector (DDMA).
Taluka level: Chaired by the Tehsildar.
Village level: Chaired by the Sarpanch.
This structure ensures that aid and orders flow from top to bottom.
13

What is the role of the District Collector in disasters?

The Collector is the head of the District Disaster Management Authority.
They are responsible for implementing disaster plans at the ground level.
They coordinate between different departments (Police, Health, Fire).
They have the power to order evacuations and distribute relief funds.
They monitor warning systems and issue alerts to the public.
The Collector evaluates the damage and reports to the State Govt.
14

Define "First Aid."

It is the immediate assistance given to an injured person.
It is provided before professional medical help arrives.
The main aim is to preserve life and prevent the condition from worsening.
It includes simple techniques like CPR, bandaging, and splinting.
Basic first aid knowledge can save lives in everyday accidents.
Every household and school should have a first aid kit.
15

What are the "Main Objectives of First Aid"?

To preserve the life of the victim (Life-saving).
To prevent the injury or condition from becoming worse.
To promote the recovery of the patient.
To provide comfort and reassurance to the victim.
To facilitate the safe transport of the patient to a hospital.
To minimize pain and bleeding as much as possible.
16

What is the "ABC" of First Aid?

A - Airway: Ensure the person's breathing passage is clear and open.
B - Breathing: Check if the person is breathing; if not, give artificial breath.
C - Circulation: Check for a pulse; perform chest compressions if needed.
This sequence is vital for resuscitating an unconscious person.
It is now often updated to CAB (Compressions, Airway, Breathing) in modern CPR.
Checking ABC is the first step in any emergency medical response.
17

How to handle "Bleeding" in an emergency?

Apply direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or bandage.
Elevate the injured part above the level of the heart if possible.
Do not remove any embedded objects; pad around them instead.
If blood soaks through, add another layer of cloth on top.
Tie a bandage firmly but not so tight that it stops circulation.
Seek medical help immediately if the bleeding is heavy or spurting.
18

How to treat "Burns and Scalds"?

For minor burns, hold the area under cool running water for 10-15 mins.
Do not use ice, butter, or ointments on a fresh burn.
Remove any jewelry or tight clothing before the area swells.
Cover the burn loosely with a sterile non-stick bandage.
Do not pop blisters, as they protect against infection.
For severe burns, cover with a clean sheet and go to the hospital.
19

What to do in case of a "Fracture"?

Do not try to move the injured limb or "set" the bone yourself.
Immobilize the area using a splint (stick, rolled newspaper, etc.).
Tie the splint above and below the site of the fracture.
Apply an ice pack wrapped in a cloth to reduce swelling.
If it's an open fracture (bone sticking out), cover with a clean cloth.
Treat the person for shock and transport them carefully.
20

What is "CPR" (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)?

It is a life-saving technique used when the heart stops beating.
It involves repeated, firm chest compressions (about 100-120 per min).
Compressions help pump blood to the brain and vital organs.
It may be combined with rescue breaths (mouth-to-mouth).
CPR should continue until the patient revives or an ambulance arrives.
It is a skill that everyone should learn through formal training.
21

How to help someone who is "Choking"?

Encourage the person to cough forcefully to dislodge the object.
If they cannot breathe, perform "Abdominal Thrusts" (Heimlich Maneuver).
Stand behind them, wrap your arms around their waist, and pull inward/upward.
For infants, use back blows and chest thrusts instead.
Never try to reach into the mouth if you can't see the object.
Call for emergency help if the person becomes unconscious.
22

What are the signs of "Heat Stroke"?

Extremely high body temperature (above 104°F/40°C).
Hot, red, dry skin (no sweating).
Rapid pulse, headache, dizziness, and confusion.
Nausea, vomiting, and potential loss of consciousness.
It is a medical emergency caused by prolonged exposure to heat.
Move the victim to a cool place and sponge them with cold water.
23

How to handle a "Snake Bite"?

Keep the victim calm and restrict their movement to slow venom spread.
Keep the bitten limb below the level of the heart.
Clean the wound with water but do not scrub it.
Do not try to suck out the venom or cut the wound.
Do not use a tight tourniquet; use a firm pressure bandage instead.
Try to remember the snake's appearance and rush to the hospital for anti-venom.
24

What is a "Mock Drill"?

It is a practice session to test the readiness for a disaster.
It simulates a real emergency (like a fire or earthquake) in a building.
It helps people know the exit routes and assembly points.
It tests the functioning of fire extinguishers and alarms.
Drills identify weaknesses in the emergency response plan.
Regular mock drills reduce panic and save lives during actual events.
25

List items in a standard "First Aid Kit."

Sterile gauze pads and adhesive bandages (different sizes).
Antiseptic liquid (Dettol/Savlon) and antibiotic ointment.
Pair of scissors, tweezers, and safety pins.
Triangular bandages (for slings) and crepe bandages.
Thermometer, disposable gloves, and a flashlight.
Basic medicines for pain, fever, and acidity (consult a doctor).
26

What is the impact of "Floods" on a community?

Destruction of homes, bridges, and electrical grids.
Contamination of drinking water leads to water-borne diseases.
Loss of crops and livestock causes long-term economic distress.
Erosion of fertile topsoil and damage to the ecosystem.
Displacement of thousands of people into temporary camps.
Restoration of normalcy takes months of cleaning and disinfection.
27

How to prepare for an "Earthquake"?

Identify safe spots in each room (under heavy tables or desks).
Fix heavy furniture (cupboards, mirrors) securely to the walls.
Keep an emergency "Go-Bag" ready with food, water, and documents.
Learn how to turn off gas, electricity, and water valves.
Participate in school/office mock drills seriously.
Have a family communication plan in case you are separated.
28

What is the "Drop, Cover, and Hold on" technique?

[Image of Drop, Cover, and Hold On earthquake safety steps]
Drop: Get down on your hands and knees to prevent being knocked over.
Cover: Cover your head and neck with your arms; crawl under a sturdy desk.
Hold on: Stay there and hold onto your shelter until the shaking stops.
This is the safest action to take during an earthquake.
If no shelter is nearby, crawl next to an interior wall away from windows.
Do not run outside or use elevators during the shaking.
29

What is "Disaster Warning Systems"?

Systems that detect early signs of disasters (like Tsunami buoys).
They use satellites, weather radars, and sensors.
Warnings are sent via sirens, radio, TV, and SMS alerts.
They provide "Lead Time" for people to evacuate to safer ground.
The India Meteorological Department (IMD) issues cyclone warnings.
Effective communication of these warnings is vital for saving lives.
30

Explain "Social Responsibility" during disasters.

Helping neighbors and the elderly during evacuation.
Donating money, clothes, or food to recognized relief funds.
Avoiding spreading rumors or fake news on social media.
Volunteering with NGOs or local authorities if you are trained.
Following the instructions of the rescue teams without argument.
Respecting the privacy and dignity of the victims.
31

What is the "Red Cross" role in disaster management?

They provide emergency medical care and blood bank services.
They help in tracing missing family members (Restoring Family Links).
They distribute hygiene kits, blankets, and food to survivors.
They provide psychological support and counseling to victims.
Red Cross works on global principles of neutrality and humanity.
They are often the first international NGO to arrive at a disaster site.
32

What is "Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder" (PTSD)?

A mental health condition triggered by witnessing a terrifying event.
Symptoms include nightmares, severe anxiety, and "flashbacks."
Victims may feel emotionally numb or easily startled.
It can last for months or years if not treated by a professional.
Children are especially vulnerable to PTSD after a disaster.
Counseling and group therapy are essential parts of rehabilitation.
33

How to purify water after a flood?

Boiling water for at least 1 minute is the safest method.
Using chlorine tablets (Halazone) as per the instructions.
Using a cloth filter followed by UV or RO purification.
Adding "Alum" to settle the mud before filtering and boiling.
Never drink flood water directly, even if it looks clear.
Store purified water in clean, covered containers.
34

What is "Nuclear Fallout"?

The residual radioactive dust and ash propelled into the atmosphere.
It can travel hundreds of miles from the site of a nuclear explosion.
Exposure causes radiation sickness, cancer, and genetic mutations.
People are advised to stay in underground shelters or thick concrete rooms.
Iodine tablets are sometimes used to protect the thyroid gland.
Fallout can contaminate soil and food chains for decades.
35

What is the importance of "Emergency Communication"?

Mobile networks often fail during disasters due to tower damage.
"HAM Radio" (Amateur Radio) is used as an alternative by volunteers.
Satellite phones are used by rescue teams in remote areas.
Use "Short Messaging Service" (SMS) as it uses less network capacity.
Keep phone calls short to leave the lines open for emergencies.
Carry a battery-operated radio to listen to government bulletins.
36

Explain "Epidemic Management."

Identifying the source of the infection and isolating the patients.
Massive vaccination drives and distribution of medicines.
Sanitizing public areas and improving waste management.
Educating the public about handwashing and social distancing.
Tracking the spread of the disease using data and maps.
Enforcing lockdowns or quarantine if the disease is highly contagious.
37

What are "Temporary Shelters"?

Tents or prefabricated structures set up for displaced people.
They must provide protection from rain, cold, and heat.
Common areas include schools, community halls, or open fields.
They must have separate toilets for men and women for hygiene.
Safe drinking water and a community kitchen are essential.
Security is needed to protect vulnerable groups like children and women.
38

What is "Search and Rescue"?

The process of locating and extracting victims trapped in a disaster.
Search involves using drones, thermal cameras, and sniffer dogs.
Rescue involves using heavy machinery, ropes, and medical kits.
"The Golden Hour" is the first hour where chances of survival are highest.
Rescuers must prioritize their own safety to avoid becoming victims.
It requires specialized training and teamwork.
39

How to handle an "Electric Shock" victim?

Do not touch the person if they are still in contact with the wire.
Turn off the main power switch immediately if possible.
Use a dry wooden stick or plastic object to pull the person away.
Once safe, check for breathing and start CPR if necessary.
Cover any electrical burns with a sterile dressing.
Call for an ambulance as internal damage may not be visible.
40

What is "Panic" and how to control it?

Panic is a sudden feeling of fear that prevents logical thinking.
It can cause stampedes in crowded places during a disaster.
To control it, take deep breaths and speak in a calm, firm voice.
Follow the established emergency plan and don't shout.
Focus on small, immediate tasks rather than the whole disaster.
Leadership and clear instructions are the best tools against panic.
41

What is "Drought" management?

Promoting water conservation and rainwater harvesting.
Growing "Short-duration" crops that require less water.
Constructing check dams and deepening existing wells.
Providing fodder and water for cattle in affected areas.
Using drip irrigation and sprinklers for farming.
Cloud seeding is sometimes used as an experimental method.
42

What is "Cyclonic Storm" safety?

Stay indoors and away from windows or glass doors.
If the "Eye" of the storm passes, don't go out; the wind will return.
Disconnect all electrical appliances to prevent surges.
Store enough dry food, candles, and batteries for 3-4 days.
If in a kutcha house, move to a pucca building or cyclone shelter.
Avoid going near the sea or under old trees and poles.
43

What is "Forest Fire" (Wildfire) prevention?

Do not throw lit cigarettes or matches in dry grass areas.
Clear dry leaves and bushes around residential areas near forests.
Properly extinguish any campfires or cooking fires before leaving.
Use "Fire Breaks" (cleared paths) to stop the fire from spreading.
Report any signs of smoke to the forest department immediately.
High-altitude "Water Bombing" using planes is used for large fires.
44

What is "Landslide" awareness?

Avoid building houses on steep slopes or near the edges of cliffs.
Look for signs like tilted trees, cracked walls, or blocked drains.
During heavy rain, stay alert for unusual sounds like trees cracking.
Plant trees on slopes to hold the soil together (Afforestation).
If caught in a landslide, curl into a ball and protect your head.
Move away from the path of the flow as quickly as possible.
45

What is "Community Based Disaster Management" (CBDM)?

It involves training local villagers to be the first responders.
Locals know the geography and vulnerable people in their area.
They form "Village Disaster Management Committees."
They maintain their own emergency kits and early warning bells.
CBDM reduces the time taken for help to arrive in remote areas.
It empowers people to be "Self-Reliant" during a crisis.
46

What is "Biological Warfare"?

The intentional use of viruses, bacteria, or toxins as weapons.
It aims to kill or incapacitate humans, animals, or plants.
Examples include Anthrax, Smallpox, or plague being spread.
It is extremely difficult to detect and control once started.
International treaties (BWC) ban the use of such weapons.
Public health systems must be ready for "Bio-defense."
47

What is "Chemical Disaster" protocol?

If there is a gas leak, move in the "Upwind" direction (against the wind).
Cover your mouth and nose with a wet cloth to filter the air.
Close all doors and windows and seal the gaps with wet towels.
Do not use matches or lighters as the gas might be flammable.
Listen to the radio for instructions on evacuation or "Shelter-in-place."
Decontaminate by showering and changing clothes after reaching safety.
48

What is the "108" Ambulance service?

It is a free emergency response service available in many Indian states.
It provides medical, police, and fire assistance through one number.
Ambulances are equipped with "Life Support" and trained EMTs.
It works 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.
The service aims to reach the victim within 15-20 minutes.
It has significantly improved the "Golden Hour" survival rates.
49

How to help "Vulnerable Groups" in a disaster?

Prioritize the evacuation of children, pregnant women, and the elderly.
Provide special assistance to "Differently-abled" people (e.g., wheelchairs).
Ensure that children are never separated from their family or guardians.
Provide emotional support to those who are orphaned or widowed.
Ensure shelters have safe and accessible facilities for everyone.
Protective measures are needed to prevent exploitation in camps.
50

What is "Tsunami" safety?

If you feel a strong earthquake near the coast, move inland immediately.
If the sea recedes significantly, do not go to the beach to watch; run!
Move to a height of at least 30 meters (100 feet) above sea level.
A Tsunami is a series of waves; the first one is not always the largest.
Stay away from the coast until the authorities say it is safe.
Coastal "Mangroves" act as a natural barrier against Tsunami waves.
51

What is "International Disaster Response"?

When a disaster is too big for one country, others send help.
The UN (United Nations) coordinates global relief efforts.
Teams like "Doctors Without Borders" provide medical care.
Countries send food, blankets, and specialized search teams.
It shows global solidarity and humanity during crises.
Logistics and customs rules are often relaxed for aid ships/planes.
52

What is "Insurance" in disaster management?

Crop insurance helps farmers recover from droughts or floods.
Home insurance covers the cost of rebuilding after an earthquake.
Health insurance helps victims pay for long-term medical treatment.
It reduces the financial burden on the government and individuals.
Awareness about insurance is low in rural areas and needs to increase.
It is a key tool for financial "Resilience."
53

What is "Sustainable Development" and disasters?

Developing in a way that doesn't increase disaster risk.
Example: Not building on floodplains or destroying wetlands.
Using renewable energy reduces the risk of climate-related disasters.
Planting trees prevents soil erosion and landslides.
Sustainable cities are designed to handle extreme weather.
It ensures that future generations are safe and healthy.
54

What are "Disaster Resistant Houses"?

Houses built using "L-shaped" or "T-shaped" wall reinforcements.
Using lightweight materials for roofs in earthquake zones.
Houses built on "Stilts" (pillars) in flood-prone areas.
Using fire-retardant materials for construction.
These designs follow the "National Building Code" of India.
They may be slightly costlier but save lives and assets.
55

How to handle "Pet/Livestock" during a disaster?

Do not leave pets tied up; they need a chance to escape.
Have an emergency kit for pets (food, water, leash).
Ensure livestock have ear-tags for identification.
Move cattle to higher ground before a flood hits.
Governments set up "Cattle Camps" for fodder and water.
Losing livestock is a major economic blow to rural families.
56

What is "Disaster Mapping"?

Using maps to identify high-risk areas (Vulnerability Mapping).
Marking evacuation routes and safe assembly points on a map.
Using GIS (Geographic Information Systems) for better planning.
Maps help in deciding where to build hospitals or fire stations.
It helps rescue teams navigate during an emergency.
Digital maps can be updated in real-time during a disaster.
57

What is "Triage" in medical emergencies?

It is the process of sorting victims based on the severity of injury.
Red: Immediate attention needed (Life-threatening).
Yellow: Serious but can wait for a short time.
Green: Minor injuries ("Walking wounded").
Black: Deceased or injuries too severe to survive.
Triage ensures that limited medical resources save the most lives.
58

What is the "Good Samaritan" spirit in disasters?

It is the act of helping others without expecting any reward.
It involves sharing your food, water, or shelter with strangers.
It keeps the morale of the community high during dark times.
Many people risk their own lives to save people they don't know.
This spirit is what makes a society resilient and strong.
Government honors such people with bravery awards.
59

What are "Disaster Relief Funds"?

The PM Cares Fund and CM Relief Fund are examples in India.
Money is used to provide immediate cash to victims' families.
It funds the reconstruction of public schools and roads.
It is used to purchase rescue equipment for the NDRF.
Audit and transparency are important for these public funds.
Donations to these funds are often tax-exempt.
60

What is "Mental Health First Aid"?

Providing emotional support to someone in extreme shock.
Using "Psychological First Aid" (PFA) techniques.
Look, Listen, and Link: Look for safety, Listen to needs, Link to help.
Do not force people to talk about the trauma if they aren't ready.
Helping children find their toys or comfort items helps.
It prevents the development of long-term mental illnesses.
61

What is "Avalanche" safety?

An avalanche is a mass of snow sliding down a mountain.
Avoid skiing or trekking during "High Risk" warning days.
Wear an "Avalanche Transceiver" so rescuers can find you.
If caught, try to "Swim" on top of the snow.
Create an air pocket in front of your face with your hands.
Avalanches can reach speeds of 120 km/h and are very deadly.
62

What is "Industrial Safety" to prevent disasters?

Regular audits of boilers, gas pipes, and chemical tanks.
Training workers in the use of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
Installing automatic fire sprinklers and gas sensors.
Having a "Safety Officer" on-site at all times.
Proper disposal of hazardous industrial waste.
Mock drills for chemical spills should be done every 6 months.
63

What is "Mass Casualty Incident" (MCI)?

Any event where the number of victims exceeds the available help.
Examples: Train accidents, bridge collapses, or stadium crushes.
It requires a "Command Center" to coordinate multiple ambulances.
Hospitals must activate "Code Orange" to clear beds for victims.
Communication between the site and the hospital is crucial.
It is a test of the city's entire emergency infrastructure.
64

What is "Drowning" first aid?

Reach, Throw, Row, Go: Use a pole or lifebuoy before jumping in.
Once the victim is out, check for breathing and pulse.
If not breathing, start CPR immediately.
Do not try to "drain the water" from the stomach; focus on lungs.
Keep the person warm to prevent hypothermia.
Even if they seem fine, they must go to a hospital (Dry Drowning risk).
65

What is "Hypothermia" and its treatment?

It occurs when the body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C).
Caused by cold water, rain, or being trapped in snow.
Symptoms include shivering, slurred speech, and confusion.
Remove wet clothes and wrap the person in dry blankets.
Give warm (not hot) sweet drinks if they are conscious.
Do not rub the skin or use direct heat like hot water bottles.
66

What is "Fire Extinguisher" usage (PASS)?

[Image of PASS technique for fire extinguishers: Pull, Aim, Squeeze, and Sweep]
P - Pull: Pull the pin to break the seal.
A - Aim: Aim the nozzle at the base of the fire.
S - Squeeze: Squeeze the handle to release the agent.
S - Sweep: Sweep from side to side at the base of the fire.
Use the right extinguisher (Water for wood, CO2 for electrical).
Never use water on a grease/oil fire; use baking soda or a lid.
67

What is "Shelter-in-Place"?

It means staying inside your current building during a disaster.
Used during chemical leaks, nuclear fallout, or active shooters.
Turn off fans and ACs to stop outside air from entering.
Move to an interior room with no windows if possible.
Listen to official news for the "All Clear" signal.
It is the opposite of evacuation.
68

What is the "Disaster Management Act, 2005"?

It is the law that created the NDMA and SDRF in India.
It defines the roles and responsibilities of every government level.
It provides legal powers to officers during an emergency.
It sets the rules for the management of relief funds.
It focuses on shifting from "Relief-centric" to "Proactive" management.
This Act has made India much better prepared for disasters.
69

What is "Crowd Management" during festivals?

Using "Barricades" to control the flow of people.
Marking clear "Entry" and "Exit" points.
Having a "Public Address System" for constant instructions.
Stationing ambulances and first aid booths near the crowd.
"CCTV" monitoring to detect any potential stampede triggers.
Training volunteers to guide people calmly.
70

What is "Lightning" safety?

"When thunder roars, go indoors."
Avoid open fields, tall trees, and water bodies.
If outside, squat low on the balls of your feet (Lightning Crouch).
Do not use landline phones or touch metal pipes.
Unplug electrical appliances like TVs and computers.
If someone is hit, they do not carry a charge; give CPR immediately.
71

What is "Heat Wave" management?

Drink plenty of water even if you don't feel thirsty.
Wear lightweight, light-colored, loose cotton clothes.
Avoid strenuous physical work during the peak sun (12 PM - 4 PM).
Use ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) or homemade lassi/lemon water.
Keep animals in the shade and provide them with cool water.
Use hats, umbrellas, and sunglasses when going outside.
72

What is "Epidemiological Surveillance"?

The continuous monitoring of disease patterns in a population.
It helps in detecting an outbreak before it becomes a disaster.
Doctors report every case of specific diseases to the government.
It involves testing water and food samples regularly.
Surveillance was key in the eradication of Smallpox and Polio.
It is the "Early Warning System" for biological disasters.
73

What is "Refugee" management?

Providing for people fleeing war or persecution.
Refugee camps must provide basic human rights and safety.
International laws (Geneva Convention) protect refugees.
Focus is on providing education for children and healthcare.
Long-term solutions involve peace talks or resettlement.
Migration due to climate change (Climate Refugees) is a new challenge.
74

What is "Cyber-Security" as disaster prevention?

Protecting the power grid and banks from hacker attacks.
Using "Firewalls" and encrypted data for government systems.
Having a "Backup" of all critical data in a different location.
Training staff to recognize phishing and social engineering.
Cyber-attacks can cause physical disasters (e.g., dam failure).
It is the most modern form of disaster management.
75

What is the "Civil Defense" organization?

A volunteer-based organization that assists during emergencies.
They are trained in firefighting, first aid, and rescue.
They help the police in maintaining order during disasters.
They educate the public on air-raid and earthquake safety.
Any citizen above 18 can join and get trained.
It is an important link between the government and the community.
76

What is "Logistics" in disaster management?

The process of moving food, water, and medicine to the disaster site.
It involves coordinating trucks, planes, and ships.
Managing "Warehouses" for storing relief materials.
Ensuring that the "Last Mile" delivery reaches the victims.
Logistics often fails due to broken roads or fuel shortages.
Good logistics is the backbone of any relief operation.
77

What is "Dam Failure" and safety?

Dams can fail due to earthquakes, poor construction, or floods.
It causes a sudden, massive wave that destroys everything downstream.
Warning sirens are placed in villages below the dam.
Regular "Structural Audits" are done to check for cracks.
Controlled release of water during monsoons prevents pressure.
Evacuation maps for dam-bursts are mandatory for high-risk zones.
78

What is "Urban Flooding"?

Flooding in cities caused by heavy rain and blocked drains.
Concrete prevents water from soaking into the ground.
Construction on natural "Nalas" (streams) blocks the water flow.
Cities like Mumbai and Chennai face this frequently.
Management involves cleaning drains and "Sponge City" designs.
It causes huge traffic jams and electrical hazards.
79

How to help "Stray Animals" in disasters?

Place water bowls in safe, shaded areas during heatwaves.
Do not block their path if they are running away from fire or flood.
Coordinate with local animal shelters for rescue operations.
Provide them with leftover food in community shelters.
If they are injured, call an animal welfare NGO.
Stray animals are part of the ecosystem and deserve care.
80

What is "Quarantine"?

The separation of people who might have been exposed to a disease.
It is different from "Isolation" (which is for those already sick).
The period depends on the "Incubation Period" of the disease.
It stops the silent spread of infections in the community.
Quarantine can be at home or in a government facility.
It is a powerful tool during pandemics like COVID-19.
81

What is "Debris Management"?

Clearing fallen trees, bricks, and metal after a disaster.
Debris can block roads and prevent ambulances from reaching victims.
It must be searched carefully for trapped survivors before removal.
Sorting debris into "Recyclable" and "Waste" is important.
Improper disposal can lead to soil and water pollution.
It is one of the first tasks in the Reconstruction phase.
82

What is "Disaster Diplomacy"?

Using disaster relief to improve relations between hostile countries.
Example: Countries sending aid to rivals after an earthquake.
It opens up communication channels that were previously closed.
It reminds leaders that human life is more important than politics.
"Medical Diplomacy" (sending vaccines) is a modern example.
It can lead to long-term peace if managed well.
83

What is "Sponge City" concept?

A city designed to absorb, store, and purify rainwater.
Using permeable pavements that allow water to soak through.
Creating "Rain Gardens" and "Wetlands" within the city.
It reduces the risk of urban flooding significantly.
The stored water can be used during droughts.
It is a key strategy for "Climate-resilient" urban planning.
84

What is "Gender-Sensitive" disaster management?

Recognizing that women and men have different needs in a crisis.
Providing sanitary napkins and privacy in relief camps.
Ensuring women are involved in the planning and distribution of aid.
Protecting women from violence in crowded shelters.
Nutritional support for pregnant and breastfeeding mothers.
Women often play the lead role in family and community recovery.
85

What is "Disaster Journalism"?

Reporting news during and after a disaster.
Journalists provide vital information about safe areas and aid.
They must avoid "Sensationalism" or showing graphic images of victims.
They hold the government accountable for the quality of relief.
"Citizen Journalism" (videos by locals) is now very common.
Media should focus on "Solutions" and "Hope" rather than just tragedy.
86

What is "Self-Evacuation"?

When people leave a danger zone before the government orders it.
It happens when people trust their own senses or local warnings.
It can reduce the burden on government rescue teams.
However, it can lead to traffic jams if not coordinated.
Education helps people know "When" and "Where" to go.
Always carry your "Go-Bag" during self-evacuation.
87

What is "Economic Resilience"?

The ability of a business or city to recover financially after a disaster.
Small businesses need "Disaster Loans" to buy new equipment.
Diversifying the economy so one disaster doesn't kill all jobs.
Having an "Emergency Fund" at the corporate level.
Digital backups of financial records are essential.
Insurance is the most important part of economic resilience.
88

What is "Locust Control"?

Locusts are grasshoppers that eat all vegetation in their path.
Controlled using aerial spraying of pesticides (Malathion).
Using noise (drums/sirens) to prevent them from landing.
Monitoring their breeding grounds in desert areas.
International cooperation is needed as they cross borders easily.
A large swarm can cause a "Famine" (total food shortage).
89

What is "Food Security" after a disaster?

Ensuring that survivors have access to enough nutritious food.
Building "Grain Banks" in disaster-prone villages.
Distributing "Ready-to-eat" meals (MREs) during the emergency phase.
Preventing "Hoarding" and "Black marketing" of food supplies.
Fortified biscuits and milk powder are given to children.
Restarting local markets is the goal of the rehabilitation phase.
90

What is "Volunteer Management"?

Coordinating the thousands of people who want to help.
Volunteers must be registered and given specific tasks.
Untrained volunteers can sometimes get in the way of experts.
"Virtual Volunteers" help by mapping or translating from home.
Providing food and safety for the volunteers themselves.
Good management turns "Chaos" into "Productive help."
91

What is "Disaster Ethics"?

The moral principles followed during a disaster.
Who gets the last bed in the hospital? (Triage ethics).
Respecting the "Dead" by providing a dignified burial/cremation.
Ensuring aid reaches the poorest, not just the influential.
Honesty in reporting the number of casualties.
Ethics prevent the disaster from becoming a moral tragedy.
92

What is "Climate Change" and disasters?

Global warming is making cyclones and floods more intense.
Rising sea levels are threatening to submerge coastal cities.
Changing rainfall patterns cause unpredictable droughts.
Disaster management must now include "Climate Adaptation."
Reducing carbon footprint is the long-term solution.
The frequency of "Extreme Weather" events is increasing.
93

What is "Disaster Education" in schools?

Teaching children about different disasters and their causes.
Including first aid and mock drills in the daily routine.
Students act as "Change Agents" by educating their parents.
Creating a "School Disaster Management Plan."
Identifying the safest parts of the school building.
Education reduces fear and builds a "Culture of Safety."
94

What is "Structural Mitigation"?

Physical changes to the environment to reduce disaster risk.
Building sea walls, levees, and embankments.
Retrofitting old buildings to make them earthquake-proof.
Deepening river beds to increase water-carrying capacity.
Installing "Fire-escapes" in high-rise buildings.
These are long-term engineering solutions.
95

What is "Non-Structural Mitigation"?

Changes in behavior, laws, and policies to reduce risk.
Land-use zoning (not allowing houses in risky areas).
Public awareness campaigns and training programs.
Strict enforcement of building codes and safety laws.
Insurance and disaster relief planning.
It is often cheaper and more effective than structural changes.
96

What is "Hazard" vs "Disaster"?

A Hazard is a dangerous situation (e.g., a volcano).
A Disaster is when that hazard actually hurts people.
A volcano on an uninhabited island is a hazard, not a disaster.
Disaster = Hazard + Vulnerability.
We can't always stop hazards, but we can reduce vulnerability.
This distinction is the basis of modern disaster theory.
97

What is "Dignified Management of the Dead"?

Identifying the bodies through DNA, dental records, or photos.
Respecting the religious and cultural beliefs of the deceased.
Providing a clear process for families to claim their loved ones.
Preventing the "Mass burial" of unidentified bodies if possible.
Providing psychological support to the families.
It is a key part of the social health of a recovering society.
98

What is "Remote Sensing" in disasters?

Using satellite images to see the extent of a flood or fire.
It helps in mapping areas that are cut off by road.
It can track the movement of a cyclone in real-time.
It helps in "Damage Assessment" without going to the site.
India's "ISRO" provides vital satellite data for disasters.
It is an essential tool for high-level decision-making.
99

What is "Resilience"?

The ability of a person or city to "Bounce Back" after a disaster.
It involves physical strength and mental toughness.
A resilient city has strong buildings and even stronger people.
Resilience is built through planning, training, and community bonds.
It is the ultimate goal of all disaster management activities.
A resilient society doesn't just survive; it learns and grows.
100

What is the conclusion of Chapter 10?

Disasters are a part of life, but their impact can be reduced.
Science and technology play a vital role in warning and rescue.
Every citizen has a role to play in disaster management.
First aid and mock drills are life-saving skills everyone should have.
Compassion and social responsibility are the keys to recovery.
"Prevention is better than cure" applies to disasters too.
