Chapter 02: Life Processes Part-1

Full Study Guide | ATP, Respiration & Cell Division

Question 01

Define Life Processes.

Life processes are the various functions carried out by living beings to maintain life.
These include nutrition, respiration, excretion, sensation, and response to stimuli.
Every life process requires a continuous supply of energy to function effectively.
Coordination between these processes is essential for the survival of the organism.
In multicellular organisms, different organ systems work together for these processes.
Failure of any major life process can lead to the death of the organism.
Question 02

What is the main source of energy for living organisms?

Carbohydrates are the primary source of energy for all living organisms.
We obtain them through our daily diet in the form of starch and sugars.
Glucose is the simplest carbohydrate used by cells to produce energy.
Excess carbohydrates are stored in the liver and muscles in the form of glycogen.
In plants, the excess energy is stored in the form of starch.
We get 4 kcal of energy per gram of carbohydrates consumed.
Question 03

Define Cellular Respiration.

[Image of cellular respiration overview]
It is the process by which food (glucose) is oxidized to release energy inside the cell.
It occurs in two main ways: Aerobic respiration and Anaerobic respiration.
In Aerobic respiration, oxygen is used to break down glucose completely.
In Anaerobic respiration, glucose is partially broken down without using oxygen.
The energy released is stored in the form of ATP molecules.
It involves three main steps: Glycolysis, Tricarboxylic Acid Cycle, and Electron Transfer Chain.
Question 04

Explain the process of Glycolysis.

Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm of the cell and is common to both types of respiration.
One molecule of glucose is oxidized step-by-step to produce two molecules of pyruvic acid.
Additionally, two molecules each of ATP, $NADH_2$, and water are formed.
Pyruvic acid molecules are then converted into molecules of Acetyl-Coenzyme-A.
During this conversion, two molecules of $NADH_2$ and two molecules of $CO_2$ are released.
Glycolysis does not require oxygen to initiate the breakdown of glucose.
Question 05

What is the Tricarboxylic Acid (TCA) Cycle?

This cycle is also known as the Krebs Cycle, named after Sir Hans Krebs.
Both molecules of Acetyl-CoA enter the mitochondria to begin this process.
The acetyl part of Acetyl-CoA is completely oxidized through a cyclic chain of reactions.
The molecules $CO_2$, $H_2O$, $NADH_2$, and $FADH_2$ are produced as result.
This cycle is essential for extracting high-energy electrons from carbon compounds.
It takes place specifically in the mitochondrial matrix.
Question 06

Describe the Electron Transfer Chain (ETC) reaction.

The ETC reaction occurs only in the mitochondria of the cell.
Molecules of $NADH_2$ and $FADH_2$ produced in previous steps participate here.
For every molecule of $NADH_2$, three molecules of ATP are produced.
For every molecule of $FADH_2$, two molecules of ATP are produced.
Water molecules are also formed during this series of complex reactions.
ATP is generated using energy released by the flow of electrons.
Question 07

What is ATP?

ATP stands for Adenosine Triphosphate and is the energy currency of the cell.
It is an energy-rich molecule where energy is stored in the phosphate bonds.
It consists of an adenosine nucleoside and three phosphate groups.
When a phosphate bond is broken, energy is released for cellular work.
ATP is consumed for various processes like muscle contraction and protein synthesis.
It is constantly regenerated from ADP (Adenosine Diphosphate) using food energy.
Question 08

Why is ATP called the "Energy Currency"?

Energy is stored in ATP in a form that cells can easily access.
It can be transported to any part of the cell where energy is needed.
Just as we use money to buy goods, cells "spend" ATP to perform functions.
The amount of ATP in a cell reflects its immediate energy status.
Without ATP, biochemical reactions would not have the energy to proceed.
Cells do not store large amounts of ATP but produce it as per demand.
Question 09

Explain Anaerobic Respiration in living organisms.

It is the process of energy production without the involvement of oxygen.
It involves only two stages: Glycolysis and Fermentation.
Glucose is incompletely oxidized, resulting in less energy compared to aerobic respiration.
Pyruvic acid produced by glycolysis is converted into organic acids or alcohol.
This process is common in some bacteria, yeast, and even human muscle cells.
Only 2 molecules of ATP are gained from one molecule of glucose in this process.
Question 10

What happens during Fermentation?

Fermentation is the second stage of anaerobic respiration.
Pyruvic acid is converted into other products like ethanol or lactic acid.
This conversion is carried out with the help of various enzymes.
Yeast performs alcoholic fermentation to produce ethanol and $CO_2$.
Lactobacilli perform lactic acid fermentation, used in making yogurt from milk.
No additional ATP is produced during the fermentation stage itself.
Question 11

Why do we feel tired after heavy exercise?

During heavy exercise, our muscles require more energy than usual.
If the oxygen supply is insufficient, muscles switch to anaerobic respiration.
This leads to the accumulation of lactic acid in the muscle tissues.
Lactic acid is a byproduct of anaerobic metabolism in human cells.
The buildup of this acid causes muscle fatigue, pain, and soreness.
Once we rest and oxygen supply restores, the lactic acid is eventually cleared.
Question 12

How do we get energy from Amino Acids?

Amino acids are the breakdown products of proteins we consume.
If carbohydrates are insufficient, the body uses proteins for energy.
Excess amino acids are not stored in the body; they are broken down.
The amino group is removed (deamination) and converted into ammonia.
Ammonia is then eliminated from the body through the urea cycle.
The remaining carbon skeleton is converted into glucose or other intermediates.
Question 13

What is the role of Fatty Acids in energy production?

Fatty acids are obtained from the digestion of lipids (fats).
They are converted into Acetyl-Coenzyme-A through a process called beta-oxidation.
This Acetyl-CoA then enters the Krebs cycle in the mitochondria.
Lipids provide about 9 kcal of energy per gram, which is more than carbohydrates.
Excess lipids are stored in adipose connective tissue in the body.
These stores serve as an energy reserve during periods of fasting.
Question 14

Explain the importance of Vitamins.

Vitamins are a group of heterogeneous compounds required in small quantities.
They are essential for the smooth operation of various life processes.
Vitamins like B-complex and C are water-soluble.
Vitamins A, D, E, and K are fat-soluble and stored in the body.
Riboflavin (Vitamin $B_2$) and Nicotinamide (Vitamin $B_3$) are precursors for $FADH_2$ and $NADH_2$.
Deficiency of vitamins can lead to specific diseases and metabolic disorders.
Question 15

What is the role of Water in our body?

There is about 65-70% water in the human body by weight.
Every cell contains 70% water by its own weight (cytoplasm).
Blood plasma also contains 90% water to maintain its fluidity.
Water acts as a universal solvent for biochemical reactions.
Even a slight loss of water (dehydration) can disturb body functions.
It helps in temperature regulation through the process of sweating.
Question 16

Why are Fibers considered an essential nutrient?

Fibers are indigestible substances found in plants, like cellulose.
Although we cannot digest them, they are crucial for the digestive system.
They help in the easy egestion of undigested substances (bowel movement).
Fibers prevent constipation and maintain a healthy gut environment.
We obtain fibers from leafy vegetables, fruits, and cereals.
They also play a role in regulating blood sugar and cholesterol levels.
Question 17

Define Cell Division.

Cell division is a fundamental property of all living organisms.
It is the process by which a parent cell divides into two or more daughter cells.
It is essential for the growth of a multicellular organism.
It is necessary for the restoration of emaciated or injured bodies.
It plays a key role in asexual and sexual reproduction.
There are two main types of cell division: Mitosis and Meiosis.
Question 18

What is Mitosis?

[Image of mitosis process summary]
Mitosis is a type of cell division that occurs in somatic cells and stem cells.
It results in two daughter cells that are genetically identical to the parent.
The chromosome number remains the same (diploid to diploid).
It is completed in two main steps: Karyokinesis and Cytokinesis.
Mitosis is responsible for the growth and repair of the body.
It ensures that every cell in the body has the same genetic information.
Question 19

Describe Karyokinesis (Nuclear Division).

Karyokinesis is the division of the cell nucleus during mitosis.
It is completed in four stages: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase.
It involves the condensation of chromatin into visible chromosomes.
The nuclear membrane and nucleolus disappear during the early stages.
Duplicated chromosomes are separated and moved to opposite poles.
It ensures that each daughter nucleus receives an equal set of chromosomes.
Question 20

What happens during Prophase?

[Image of cell in prophase]
Thin, thread-like chromosomes start to condense and become thick.
Each chromosome appears with its pair of sister chromatids.
Centrioles duplicate and move to opposite poles of the cell.
The nuclear membrane starts to disappear gradually.
The nucleolus also begins to vanish from the nucleus.
Spindle fibers start forming between the two centrioles.
Question 21

What happens during Metaphase?

The nuclear membrane disappears completely by the start of this phase.
Chromosomes finish their condensation and are clearly visible.
All chromosomes arrange themselves on the equatorial plane (center) of the cell.
Special flexible protein fibers called spindle fibers are formed.
These fibers attach to the centromere of each chromosome.
This stage is ideal for counting and studying the shape of chromosomes.
Question 22

What happens during Anaphase?

The centromeres of the chromosomes split into two.
Sister chromatids of each chromosome are pulled apart by spindle fibers.
These separated chromatids are now called "daughter chromosomes."
They are pulled towards the opposite poles of the cell.
The moving chromosomes look like a bunch of bananas.
An equal number of chromosomes reach each pole by the end of this phase.
Question 23

What happens during Telophase?

Chromosomes that reached the poles start to de-condense and become thin.
They turn back into thread-like chromatin and become invisible.
A nuclear membrane forms around each set of chromosomes at the poles.
Two daughter nuclei are formed within the single cell.
The nucleolus reappears in each daughter nucleus.
Spindle fibers disappear completely, concluding karyokinesis.
Question 24

Define Cytokinesis.

[Image comparing cytokinesis in animal and plant cells]
Cytokinesis is the division of the cytoplasm after nuclear division.
In animal cells, a notch (furrow) forms at the equatorial plane.
This notch deepens gradually and eventually joins to divide the cell.
In plant cells, a cell plate is formed along the midline of the cell.
This plate grows outwards to meet the existing cell walls.
Two separate daughter cells are finally formed through this process.
Question 25

Why is Mitosis important for wound healing?

When a tissue is injured, cells are lost or damaged.
Mitosis allows neighboring healthy cells to divide and produce new cells.
These new cells replace the dead or damaged ones in the tissue.
Since mitosis produces identical cells, the tissue function is restored perfectly.
It ensures that the skin or organ regains its original structure.
Continuous mitosis is necessary to maintain the integrity of our body surfaces.
Question 26

Define Meiosis.

Meiosis is a special type of cell division that occurs in germ cells.
It reduces the chromosome number by half (diploid to haploid).
It results in the formation of four daughter cells from one parent cell.
Meiosis is essential for sexual reproduction and gamete formation.
It consists of two stages: Meiosis-I and Meiosis-II.
Genetic recombination occurs during this process, leading to variation.
Question 27

Explain Meiosis-I.

It is the first stage of meiosis where the diploid cell becomes haploid.
Homologous chromosomes undergo crossing over (genetic exchange).
It is divided into Prophase-I, Metaphase-I, Anaphase-I, and Telophase-I.
Prophase-I is very long and further divided into five sub-stages.
Homologous chromosomes are separated into two daughter nuclei.
It ensures that offspring receive a unique mix of parental genes.
Question 28

What is "Crossing Over"?

[Image showing crossing over between homologous chromosomes]
It occurs during the Pachytene stage of Prophase-I in Meiosis.
It is the exchange of genetic material between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes.
This process leads to new combinations of genes in the gametes.
It is the primary reason why siblings look different from each other.
Crossing over increases genetic diversity within a species.
It provides the variation necessary for the process of evolution.
Question 29

Explain Meiosis-II.

Meiosis-II is very similar to a standard mitotic division.
Two haploid daughter cells formed in Meiosis-I participate in this stage.
Sister chromatids are separated and move to opposite poles.
No further reduction in chromosome number occurs here.
It results in the final production of four haploid daughter cells.
These four cells eventually develop into gametes (sperm or eggs).
Each gamete receives one copy of each chromosome from the parent cell.
Question 30

Compare Mitosis and Meiosis.

[Image comparing Mitosis and Meiosis side by side]
Mitosis occurs in somatic cells, while meiosis occurs in germ cells.
Mitosis produces two daughter cells; meiosis produces four.
Mitosis maintains the chromosome number (2n); meiosis halves it (n).
No genetic recombination occurs in mitosis; it is a key feature of meiosis.
Mitosis is for growth and repair; meiosis is for reproduction.
Mitosis involves one division; meiosis involves two successive divisions.
Question 31-50

Remaining Topics: Metabolism, Stem Cells, & Chromosomes

NADH2/FADH2: Electron carriers produced from B-vitamins for ATP production.
Stem Cells: Undifferentiated cells used in regenerative medicine and growth.
Chromosome Structure: DNA + Histone proteins, centromeres, and p/q arms. [Image of chromosome structure]
Diploid vs Haploid: Somatic cells (2n=46) vs Germ cells (n=23).
Question 31

What are $NADH_2$ and $FADH_2$?

They are co-enzymes that act as electron carriers in cellular respiration.
$NADH_2$ stands for Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide.
$FADH_2$ stands for Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide.
They carry high-energy electrons from the Krebs cycle to the ETC.
They are produced using vitamins (B3 and B2) from our diet.
Their oxidation in the mitochondria results in the production of ATP.
Question 32

What happens if there is a lack of carbohydrates?

The body shifts its metabolism to alternative energy sources.
First, stored glycogen in the liver is converted back into glucose.
If glycogen is exhausted, the body begins to break down lipids (fats).
Lipids are converted into fatty acids and then into Acetyl-CoA.
If lipids are also low, proteins are finally used for energy production.
This shift ensures the brain and vital organs continue to receive energy.
Question 33

Define "Gluconeogenesis".

It is the metabolic pathway that results in the generation of glucose.
This happens from non-carbohydrate carbon substrates like amino acids or glycerol.
It occurs primarily in the liver during periods of fasting or starvation.
It is essential for maintaining blood glucose levels for the brain.
The process is essentially the reverse of glycolysis with different enzymes.
It ensures that life-sustaining cellular respiration never stops.
Question 34

What is the role of Oxygen in Aerobic Respiration?

Oxygen acts as the final electron acceptor in the Electron Transfer Chain.
It combines with electrons and hydrogen ions to form water.
Without oxygen, the entire ETC would back up and stop functioning.
This would prevent the regeneration of $NAD^+$ and $FAD$ from their reduced forms.
Consequently, the Krebs cycle and glycolysis would also eventually halt.
Thus, oxygen is vital for the maximum yield of ATP from glucose.
Question 35

How is energy released from ATP?

ATP molecules have high-energy chemical bonds between phosphate groups.
A process called hydrolysis (adding water) breaks the terminal phosphate bond.
This converts ATP into ADP (Adenosine Diphosphate) and an inorganic phosphate.
The breaking of this bond releases a significant amount of free energy.
This energy is immediately captured by the cell to perform work.
The cell then uses food energy to re-attach the phosphate and "recharge" the ATP.
Question 36

What are the proteins found in the Skin?

Melanin is the primary pigment protein that gives skin its color.
It protects the underlying tissues from harmful UV radiation.
Keratin is a tough, fibrous protein found in the outer layer of the skin.
It provides structural strength and makes the skin waterproof.
Collagen is found in the deeper layers to provide elasticity and firmness.
These proteins are synthesized from amino acids obtained through our diet.
Question 37

List proteins found in other parts of the body.

Ossein is the major protein found in the matrix of bones.
Actin and Myosin are contractile proteins found in all types of muscles.
Hemoglobin is the protein in blood that transports oxygen to tissues.
Various hormones like Insulin and Pituitary hormones are also proteins.
Enzymes that catalyze biochemical reactions are essentially globular proteins.
Antibodies produced by the immune system are specialized defensive proteins.
Question 38

Explain the importance of "Stem Cells" in cell division.

Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that have the potential to become any cell type.
They undergo mitosis to produce more stem cells or specialized cells.
They are found in embryos, umbilical cord blood, and adult bone marrow.
Stem cells are used in regenerative medicine to treat damaged tissues.
They play a vital role in the initial development of an organism.
Understanding their division helps in treating diseases like leukemia and Parkinson's.
Question 39

What is a "Haploid" cell?

A haploid cell contains only one complete set of chromosomes (n).
In humans, gametes (sperm and egg) are haploid cells.
They are formed as a result of meiotic cell division.
Haploid cells in humans contain 23 chromosomes.
When two haploid gametes fuse during fertilization, they form a diploid zygote.
This ensures the species-specific chromosome number is maintained across generations.
Question 40

What is a "Diploid" cell?

A diploid cell contains two complete sets of chromosomes (2n).
One set is inherited from the father and one from the mother.
All somatic cells in the human body (like skin, muscle, bone) are diploid.
In humans, the diploid number is 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
Diploid cells divide by mitosis to maintain the chromosome count.
The zygote is the first diploid cell of a new organism.
Question 41

Describe the structure of a Chromosome.

A chromosome is a condensed structure of DNA and proteins (histones).
It consists of two sister chromatids joined at a point called the centromere.
It has two arms: a shorter 'p' arm and a longer 'q' arm.
The tips of the chromosomes are called telomeres, which protect the DNA.
Chromosomes carry genes that determine the traits of an individual.
They are only visible under a microscope during the stages of cell division.
Question 42

What is the role of Centrioles in animal cell division?

Centrioles are pair of cylinder-shaped organelles found near the nucleus.
Before mitosis starts, the centrioles duplicate.
They move to the opposite poles of the cell during prophase.
They act as anchors for the organization of spindle fibers.
Spindle fibers originate from the centrioles and attach to chromosomes.
They help in the precise separation of sister chromatids during anaphase.
Question 43

Why do seeds germinate? (Link to life processes)

A seed contains an embryo that is in a dormant (resting) state.
Upon receiving water and warmth, cellular respiration increases rapidly.
Stored food in the seed (starch) is broken down to produce ATP.
This energy triggers rapid mitosis in the embryonic cells.
New cells lead to the growth of the radicle (root) and plumule (shoot).
Thus, germination is a result of active metabolism and cell division.
Question 44

What is the purpose of the Cell Plate in plants?

Plant cells have a rigid cell wall that cannot be "pinched" like animal cells.
During cytokinesis, vesicles from the Golgi apparatus align at the center.
These vesicles fuse to form a structure called the cell plate.
The cell plate grows outward until it reaches the parent cell wall.
It eventually matures into a new primary cell wall between the daughter cells.
This allows plant cells to divide effectively despite their rigid exterior.
Question 45

Explain the significance of Meiosis in evolution.

Meiosis introduces genetic variation through the process of crossing over.
It ensures that no two gametes produced by an individual are exactly the same.
This variation is passed on to the offspring during sexual reproduction.
Genetic diversity allows a population to adapt to changing environments.
It prevents the accumulation of harmful mutations through recombination.
Without meiosis, all offspring would be clones, slowing down the evolutionary process.
Question 46

What are "Homologous Chromosomes"?

[Image of homologous chromosome pair]
They are a pair of chromosomes, one from each parent, that are similar in shape and size.
They carry the same genes at the same locations (loci).
However, they may carry different versions (alleles) of those genes.
They pair up during Prophase-I of meiosis to undergo crossing over.
During Anaphase-I, they are separated into different daughter cells.
Humans have 22 pairs of homologous autosomes and 1 pair of sex chromosomes.
Question 47

What is the importance of "Sister Chromatids"?

Sister chromatids are two identical copies of a single replicated chromosome.
They are produced during the S-phase (Synthesis phase) of the cell cycle.
They remain attached at the centromere until anaphase of mitosis or meiosis-II.
Their separation ensures that each daughter cell gets an exact copy of the DNA.
Any error in their separation can lead to genetic disorders like Down Syndrome.
They are the units of genetic material that move during the division process.
Question 48

How do bacteria produce energy?

Most bacteria produce energy through glycolysis and fermentation (anaerobic).
Some bacteria are capable of aerobic respiration if oxygen is present.
Because they lack mitochondria, respiration occurs in the cytoplasm and cell membrane.
Bacteria can use a wide variety of substrates like sulfur or iron for energy.
The total yield of ATP is much lower in anaerobic bacteria (2 ATP).
This energy is used for binary fission, which is their method of reproduction.
Question 49

What is the role of the Nucleolus in cell division?

The nucleolus is a dense region inside the nucleus responsible for ribosome production.
It disappears during prophase as the chromatin condenses into chromosomes.
This disappearance signals the suspension of normal protein synthesis.
It reappears during telophase in each of the new daughter nuclei.
Once it reappears, the cell can resume making ribosomes and proteins.
Its cycle of disappearance and reappearance is a key landmark of cell division.
Question 50

Why is "Life Process Part-1" focused on energy and division?

Energy (ATP) is the prerequisite for all other biological activities.
Without cellular respiration, no life process can take place.
Cell division is the prerequisite for the growth of any living being.
Together, they explain how a single zygote becomes a complex organism.
They explain how the body repairs itself and maintains its functions daily.
These topics form the foundation for understanding reproduction and heredity.
