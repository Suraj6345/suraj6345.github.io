Question 01

What is the difference between natural and artificial satellites?

A natural satellite is a celestial body that orbits a planet, such as the Moon.
An artificial satellite is a man-made object sent into space to orbit the Earth or other planets.
Natural satellites are formed by astronomical processes over millions of years.
Artificial satellites are designed by scientists for specific tasks like communication or weather monitoring.
The Earth has only one natural satellite but thousands of artificial ones.
Artificial satellites require launch vehicles (rockets) to reach their intended orbits.

To enhance national security through surveillance and reconnaissance.
To study the Earth's resources and environmental changes from a global perspective.
Question 03

Explain the concept of Critical Velocity ($v_c$).

Critical velocity is the minimum horizontal velocity required for a satellite to stay in a stable circular orbit.
If the velocity is less than $v_c$, the satellite will fall back to Earth due to gravity.
If the velocity is much higher than $v_c$, the satellite may escape Earth's gravity or move into an elliptical orbit.
It is calculated using the formula $v_c = \sqrt{GM / (R+h)}$.
Here, $G$ is the gravitational constant, $M$ is Earth's mass, $R$ is Earth's radius, and $h$ is the altitude.
The critical velocity depends on the height of the satellite above the Earth's surface.
Question 04, 05, 06

Types of Earth Orbits (LEO, MEO, HEO).

[Image comparing Low Earth Orbit, Medium Earth Orbit and High Earth Orbit altitudes]
LEO: Altitude 180km to 2000km. Used for ISS and Hubble. Orbit time: 90-120 mins.
MEO: Altitude 2000km to 35,780km. GPS satellites reside here. Orbit time: approx 12 hours.
HEO: Altitude higher than 35,780km. Includes Geostationary orbits (24-hour period).
Question 07 & 08

Satellite Launch Vehicles and Multi-stage Rockets.

It works on the principle of Newton's Third Law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
Modern launch vehicles usually have multiple stages to reduce weight during flight.
As the fuel in one stage is exhausted, that part of the rocket is detached and falls off.
Multi-stage rockets allow the vehicle to shed empty fuel tanks and heavy engines during flight.
This significantly reduces the total mass that needs to be accelerated as the flight progresses.
Question 09

Define Escape Velocity ($v_{esc}$).

Escape velocity is the minimum speed an object must reach to break free from a planet's gravitational pull.
For Earth, the escape velocity is approximately 11.2 km/s.
If an object travels at this speed, it will never fall back to the planet.
The formula for escape velocity is $v_{esc} = \sqrt{2GM/R}$.
It is $\sqrt{2}$ times greater than the critical velocity at the Earth's surface.
Question 10

What is Space Debris and why is it a problem?

[Image illustrating the Kessler Syndrome with space junk surrounding Earth]
Space debris consists of non-functional man-made objects in orbit, such as old satellites.
It includes broken pieces of rockets and fragments from satellite collisions.
Even a small piece of debris can cause catastrophic damage to functional satellites or the ISS.
The increasing amount of debris creates a risk known as the "Kessler Syndrome."
Question 11, 12, 13

ISRO's Major Achievements (Chandrayaan, Mangalyaan).

Chandrayaan-1: India's first Moon mission (2008); discovered water molecules on the lunar surface.
Mangalyaan (MOM): India reached Mars orbit on the first attempt (2013); studied Martian methane levels.
ISRO: Established in 1969; developed indigenous rockets like PSLV and GSLV; provides data for farmers and disaster management.
Question 14, 15, 16

Functions of Different Satellites.

Weather Satellites: Monitor atmosphere for cyclones; INSAT and METSAT series are primary for India.
Communication Satellites: Relay stations for TV and internet; GSAT series is India's backbone.
Remote Sensing: Collect data on forests/minerals using sensors; India's IRS series is world-class.
Question 17 & 18

Launch Vehicles: PSLV vs GSLV.

PSLV: 4-stage workhorse; uses solid and liquid propellants; launched Chandrayaan-1 and Mangalyaan.
GSLV: Uses Cryogenic Upper Stage (liquid hydrogen and oxygen) for heavy communication satellites.
GSLV Mark III (LVM3) is currently ISRO's most powerful rocket.
Question 19, 20, 21

Space Stations and Exploration.

Future Needs: Space mining for rare minerals and providing a "Plan B" for humanity.
ISS: Collaborative modular lab in LEO; astronauts research biology and physics in microgravity.
The Moon: A stepping stone for Mars missions; contains Helium-3 for clean energy.
Question 22 & 23

Manned Missions and Risks.

Risks: Cosmic radiation, bone density loss in microgravity, and extreme re-entry heat.
Gaganyaan: India's first manned mission to send a 3-member crew into LEO; features humanoid robot "Vyommitra".
Question 24, 25, 26, 27

Global Space Science.

[Image showing the Gravity Assist/Slingshot maneuver around a planet]
NASA: Led Apollo Moon landings; operates Hubble and James Webb telescopes.
Disaster Management: Satellites map flood areas and detect forest fire heat signatures.
Gravity Assist: "Slingshot" technique to increase spacecraft speed using a planet's gravity.
Hubble: Space-based telescope; determined the age of the universe to be 13.8 billion years.
Question 28, 29, 30

Sun-Synchronous Orbit, Rakesh Sharma, and Future.

Sun-Synchronous Orbit: Polar orbit used for consistent lighting in remote sensing images.
Rakesh Sharma: First Indian in space (1984); said "Sare Jahan Se Achha" from space.
Future of ISRO: Aditya-L1 (Sun study), Shukrayaan (Venus study), and building an Indian space station.
