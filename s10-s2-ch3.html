Chapter 03: Life Processes in Living Organisms Part-2

Question 1

Define Reproduction and its significance.

Reproduction is the process by which a living organism produces a new organism of its own kind.
It is one of the most important characteristics of all living organisms.
It does not help an individual to remain alive, but it is essential for the survival of the species.
It ensures the continuity of a particular species over many generations.
Reproduction is responsible for passing genetic information from parents to offspring.
It plays a vital role in the process of evolution by introducing variations.
Question 2

What is Asexual Reproduction?

It is the process of formation of a new organism without the involvement of gametes.
This process involves only one parent (uniparental) and occurs through mitosis.
Since there is no fusion of gametes, the offspring is genetically identical to the parent.
It is a faster method of multiplication compared to sexual reproduction.
It is commonly observed in unicellular organisms and some simple multicellular organisms.
The lack of genetic variation is the primary disadvantage of this method.
Question 3

Explain Binary Fission in unicellular organisms.

In this method, the parent cell divides into two equal daughter cells.
It is observed in prokaryotes like bacteria and eukaryotes like Amoeba and Paramoecium.
Amoeba can divide in any plane due to its lack of a specific shape (Simple binary fission).
Paramoecium divides horizontally, which is known as transverse binary fission.
Euglena divides vertically, referred to as longitudinal binary fission.
This process usually occurs when there is an abundant supply of food and favorable conditions.
Question 4

Describe Multiple Fission in Amoeba.

Multiple fission is an asexual method used by Amoeba during unfavorable conditions.
The Amoeba stops moving by withdrawing its pseudopodia and becomes spherical.
It forms a hard protective covering around its body called a "Cyst."
Inside the cyst, the nucleus divides many times through repeated nuclear division.
This is followed by cytoplasmic division, creating many small "amoebulae."
When favorable conditions return, the cyst breaks open, releasing many daughter amoebae.
Question 5

What is Budding in Yeast?

Yeast is a unicellular fungus that reproduces asexually through the process of budding.
The parent cell develops a small outgrowth or bulge called a "Bud."
The nucleus of the parent cell divides mitotically into two daughter nuclei.
One of these nuclei enters the newly formed bud.
The bud grows in size and eventually separates from the parent cell to live independently.
Sometimes, multiple buds form before separation, resulting in a chain of yeast cells.
Question 6

Explain Fragmentation in multicellular organisms.

This method is common in organisms with simple body organization like Spirogyra.
The body of the parent organism breaks into several small pieces or fragments.
Each fragment then starts to grow and live as a new, independent individual.
In Spirogyra, this occurs when there is plenty of water and nutrients available.
Sponges like Sycon also use fragmentation to reproduce when their body is broken.
This method relies purely on the regenerative capacity of the cells in the fragments.
Question 7

Define Regeneration in Planaria.

[Image of regeneration in planaria]
Regeneration is the ability of an organism to regrow lost or broken body parts.
Some animals like lizards use limited regeneration to escape predators (tail regrowth).
Planaria uses regeneration as a method of reproduction under certain conditions.
If a Planaria is cut into pieces, each piece can grow back its missing parts.
This results in the formation of multiple new individuals from a single parent.
It involves complex cellular processes to reorganize and create new tissues.
Question 8

Explain Budding in Hydra.

[Image of budding in hydra]
In favorable conditions, an outgrowth is formed on the body wall of Hydra due to repeated cell division.
This outgrowth is called a "Bud" and it grows gradually to form a small Hydra.
The dermal layers and digestive cavity of the budding Hydra are continuous with the parent.
The parent Hydra provides nutrition and support to the developing bud.
Once the small Hydra is sufficiently developed, it detaches from the parent body.
After separation, it starts an independent life as a new individual.
Question 9

What is Vegetative Propagation?

It is the reproduction in plants with the help of vegetative parts like roots, stems, leaves, and buds.
In potatoes, it occurs through the "eyes" (buds) present on the tuber.
In Bryophyllum, new plantlets grow from the buds present on the leaf margins.
In plants like sugarcane and grasses, propagation occurs through buds on the nodes of the stem.
Carrots and radishes use their modified roots for vegetative reproduction.
This method is widely used in agriculture and horticulture to produce clones of plants.
Question 10

Explain Spore Formation in Mucor.

Fungi like Mucor (bread mold) have a filamentous body with structures called sporangia.
Inside the sporangia, thousands of tiny, dust-like spores are produced.
Once the spores are mature, the sporangium bursts open to release them.
Spores are lightweight and can be carried by air or water over long distances.
When spores land on a moist and warm surface (like bread), they germinate.
Upon germination, a new fungal colony (mycelium) is formed.
Question 11

What is Sexual Reproduction?

It is the production of a new organism through the fusion of male and female gametes.
This process always involves two parents: one male and one female.
It involves two major steps: Gamete formation (meiosis) and Fertilization.
The zygote formed is diploid (2n) and contains genetic material from both parents.
Because of genetic recombination, the offspring are not identical to the parents.
This variation is the basis for evolution and adaptation to changing environments.
Question 12

Describe the structure of a Flower.

A flower is the structural unit of sexual reproduction in plants.
It consists of four whorls: Calyx, Corolla, Androecium, and Gynoecium.
Calyx (sepals) and Corolla (petals) are called accessory whorls as they protect inner parts.
Androecium (stamens) is the male reproductive part of the flower.
Gynoecium (carpels) is the female reproductive part of the flower.
If a flower has both Androecium and Gynoecium, it is called a "Bisexual" flower.
Question 13

Explain the parts of the Androecium.

The Androecium is made up of individual units called stamens.
Each stamen consists of a long stalk called a "Filament."
At the top of the filament is a sac-like structure called the "Anther."
Inside the anther, pollen grains are produced through the process of meiosis.
Each pollen grain contains the male gametes required for fertilization.
When mature, the anther bursts to release the pollen into the environment.
Question 14

Explain the parts of the Gynoecium.

The Gynoecium is the female whorl, made up of one or more carpels.
Each carpel consists of three parts: Stigma, Style, and Ovary.
The "Stigma" is the sticky top part that receives the pollen grains.
The "Style" is a long tube that connects the stigma to the ovary.
The "Ovary" is the swollen basal part containing one or more ovules.
Inside each ovule, an embryo sac is formed where the egg cell resides.
Question 15

Define Pollination and its types.

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma of a flower.
It is the first step toward fertilization in flowering plants.
"Self-pollination" occurs when pollen is transferred within the same flower or plant.
"Cross-pollination" occurs between flowers of two different plants of the same species.
Abiotic agents like wind and water can help in the transfer of pollen.
Biotic agents like insects, birds, and animals are also frequently involved.
Question 16

Explain Double Fertilization in plants.

[Image of double fertilization in angiosperms]
After pollination, a pollen tube grows from the pollen grain down through the style.
The pollen tube carries two male gametes into the ovule.
One male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form a diploid zygote.
The second male gamete fuses with two polar nuclei to form the endosperm.
Because two fusions take place, it is called "Double Fertilization."
The zygote develops into the embryo, while the endosperm provides nutrition.
Question 17

What happens after fertilization in plants?

The ovule develops into a seed, which contains the dormant embryo.
The ovary ripens and turns into a fruit to protect the seeds.
Other parts of the flower like petals and stamens usually wither and fall off.
Under favorable conditions, the seed germinates to form a new plantlet.
This process of a zygote becoming a plantlet is called seed germination.
The endosperm stored in the seed provides energy for initial growth.
Question 18

Describe the Male Reproductive System in humans.

[Image of the male reproductive system]
It consists of testes, various ducts, and accessory glands.
Testes are located in the scrotum outside the abdominal cavity.
Testes produce sperms and the male hormone, testosterone.
Sperms travel through the vas deferens to the ejaculatory duct.
Glands like the prostate and seminal vesicles add secretions to form semen.
The penis is the organ through which semen is delivered out of the body.
Question 19

Why are Testes located in the Scrotum?

Sperm production (spermatogenesis) is a temperature-sensitive process.
It requires a temperature that is 2 to 3 degrees Celsius lower than the normal body temperature.
The internal body temperature of 37¬∞C is too high for healthy sperm development.
The scrotum is a pouch of skin that hangs outside the warm abdominal cavity.
This external position allows the testes to remain cool for optimal sperm production.
Without this temperature regulation, male fertility would be significantly compromised.
Question 20

Describe the Female Reproductive System in humans.

[Image of the female reproductive system]
It consists of a pair of ovaries, a pair of oviducts, a uterus, and a vagina.
Ovaries produce the egg cells (oocytes) and hormones like estrogen and progesterone.
Every month, one mature egg is released from either of the ovaries into the oviduct.
The oviducts (Fallopian tubes) are the site where fertilization usually occurs.
The uterus is a muscular, bag-like organ where the fetus develops during pregnancy.
The vagina is the birth canal and the passage for menstrual flow.
Question 21

Explain the process of Gamete Formation in humans.

Gametes (sperms and eggs) are formed through the process of meiosis.
In males, sperms are produced in the testes starting from puberty until old age.
In females, oocytes (eggs) are produced in the ovaries before birth.
However, the maturation of these eggs starts only after reaching puberty.
Meiosis results in haploid gametes containing 23 chromosomes.
This ensures that the zygote formed after fusion has 46 chromosomes.
Question 22

Explain Fertilization in humans.

[Image of sperm fertilizing an egg]
Fertilization is the fusion of a haploid sperm with a haploid egg cell.
In humans, this process is internal and occurs in the oviduct.
Out of millions of sperms, only one successfully penetrates the egg.
The fusion results in the formation of a diploid cell called a "Zygote."
The zygote then begins to divide by mitosis as it moves toward the uterus.
Fertilization determines the genetic makeup of the future individual.
Question 23

Describe Development and Birth in humans.

[Image of human fetal development stages]
The zygote undergoes repeated mitotic divisions to form an embryo.
The embryo reaches the uterus and gets implanted in its thick wall.
An organ called the "Placenta" is formed to provide nutrition from the mother.
The development of the fetus inside the uterus takes about 9 months (36-40 weeks).
During this period, organs are formed and the fetus grows in size.
Once development is complete, the baby is delivered through the vagina.
Question 24

What is the Menstrual Cycle?

It is a natural, cyclic change that occurs in the female reproductive system every month.
It is controlled by four main hormones: FSH, LH, Estrogen, and Progesterone.
The cycle lasts for approximately 28 to 30 days.
It involves the maturation of a follicle, ovulation, and thickening of the uterine wall.
If the egg is not fertilized, the uterine lining breaks down and is shed.
This shedding of blood and tissue is called menstruation or a "period."
Question 25

Explain the four phases of the Menstrual Cycle.

[Image of the phases of the menstrual cycle and hormone levels]
The "Menstrual Phase" (Days 1-5) involves the discharge of blood and mucus.
The "Follicular Phase" (Days 6-13) involves the maturation of an oocyte due to FSH.
The "Ovulatory Phase" (Day 14) is when the mature egg is released from the ovary.
The "Luteal Phase" (Days 15-28) involves the thickening of the uterus for potential pregnancy.
If no pregnancy occurs, the Corpus Luteum degenerates, leading to low hormone levels.
This drop in hormones triggers the start of the next menstrual phase.
Question 26

What is Menopause?

Menopause is the permanent stoppage of the menstrual cycle in females.
It usually occurs between the ages of 45 and 50.
It happens because the ovaries stop responding to hormones and egg production ceases.
The production of estrogen and progesterone decreases significantly.
This leads to various physical and emotional changes in the woman's body.
Menopause marks the end of the reproductive phase of a woman's life.
Question 27

Explain IVF (In Vitro Fertilization).

IVF is a modern medical technique used to help couples with fertility issues.
Fertilization is carried out in a laboratory glass dish (in vitro) instead of the body.
Eggs are collected from the mother and sperms are collected from the father.
The resulting zygote or embryo is then implanted into the mother's uterus.
It is used when there are problems like blocked oviducts or low sperm count.
IVF has helped millions of couples worldwide to have their own children.
Question 28

What is Surrogacy?

Surrogacy is a method where a woman carries a pregnancy for another couple.
The woman who carries the baby is known as the "Surrogate Mother."
It is used when a woman has issues with her uterus or cannot carry a pregnancy safely.
In many cases, the embryo is created using IVF and then implanted into the surrogate.
The surrogate mother has no genetic link to the baby if the couple's gametes are used.
Legal agreements are made to ensure the baby is handed over to the intended parents.
Question 29

What is a Sperm Bank?

A sperm bank is a facility that collects, stores, and provides human semen.
It is similar to a blood bank and is used by couples facing male infertility.
Donors undergo thorough physical and genetic screening before donation.
The semen is frozen and stored at very low temperatures.
When needed, the sperm is used to fertilize the woman's egg via IVF or IUI.
The identity of the donor is usually kept confidential by the bank.
Question 30

Explain the formation of Monozygotic Twins.

Monozygotic (identical) twins are formed from a single embryo.
Within 8 days of zygote formation, the embryo cells divide into two separate groups.
These two groups develop independently as two separate fetuses.
Since they come from the same zygote, they have exactly the same genetic makeup.
They are always of the same gender and look very similar to each other.
If the division occurs after 8 days, there is a risk of "Siamese twins" (joined twins).
Question 31

Explain the formation of Dizygotic Twins.

Dizygotic (fraternal) twins are formed from two separate eggs and two separate sperms.
Occasionally, two eggs are released from the ovaries at the same time.
Both eggs are fertilized by two different sperms, forming two separate zygotes.
These two zygotes develop into two separate embryos in the uterus.
Genetically, they are no more similar than ordinary siblings.
They can be of the same gender or different genders.
Question 32

What is Reproductive Health?

According to the WHO, it is the total well-being in all aspects of reproduction.
This includes physical, emotional, social, and behavioral aspects.
Maintaining hygiene of the reproductive organs is crucial for preventing infections.
Awareness about sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) is a part of reproductive health.
In India, social taboos and lack of education often hinder reproductive health.
Regular check-ups and open communication are necessary for a healthy lifestyle.
Question 33

What is Syphilis?

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted disease caused by a bacterium.
The primary symptom is the appearance of painless sores (chancres) on the genitals.
Other symptoms include skin rashes, fever, and inflammation of the joints.
If left untreated, it can cause hair loss and damage to the brain and heart.
It is primarily spread through sexual contact with an infected person.
It can be treated effectively with antibiotics if diagnosed early.
Question 34

What is Gonorrhea?

Gonorrhea is a bacterial infection spread through sexual contact.
Symptoms in men include pain during urination and discharge from the penis.
In women, symptoms may include pelvic pain and abnormal vaginal discharge.
It can also affect the throat, eyes, and rectum.
If untreated, it can lead to infertility in both men and women.
Like syphilis, it is treated using specific antibiotics prescribed by a doctor.
Question 35

Discuss the issue of Population Explosion in India.

Population explosion is the rapid increase in the number of people in a country.
India's population has grown significantly over the last few decades.
It creates immense pressure on resources like food, water, and housing.
It leads to problems like unemployment, poverty, and environmental pollution.
High birth rates and improved medical facilities contributing to lower death rates are the causes.
Controlling the population is essential for the sustainable development of the nation.
Question 36

Explain the importance of Family Planning.

Family planning allows couples to decide the number and spacing of their children.
It helps in maintaining the health of the mother and the quality of life for children.
Various contraceptive methods like condoms, pills, and IUCDs are used for this.
It is a key tool in controlling the national population growth.
"Small family, happy family" is a popular slogan promoting this concept.
Government programs provide free or subsidized family planning services to the public.
Question 37

Why is there a gender imbalance in some societies?

Many societies have a traditional preference for male children.
This leads to the illegal practice of female foeticide (killing the female fetus).
Prenatal sex determination is a major cause of this imbalance.
Social evils like the dowry system contribute to the view of daughters as a burden.
This imbalance leads to social problems and a decrease in the female population.
Laws like the PCPNDT Act have been enacted in India to stop sex-selective abortion.
Question 38

What are the accessory glands in the male reproductive system?

The three main glands are the Seminal Vesicles, Prostate Gland, and Cowper's Gland.
Seminal vesicles secrete a fluid rich in fructose to provide energy to sperms.
The prostate gland secretes an alkaline fluid to neutralize the acidity of the vagina.
Cowper's glands (bulbourethral) produce a lubricating fluid for the urethra.
The combined secretions of these glands and the sperms form the "Semen."
These secretions are essential for the survival and motility of the sperms.
Question 39

Describe the structure of a human Sperm.

A sperm is a microscopic, single-celled male gamete.
It consists of three main parts: the Head, Middle piece, and Tail.
The head contains the nucleus with 23 chromosomes and an acrosome at the tip.
The acrosome contains enzymes to help the sperm penetrate the egg.
The middle piece contains numerous mitochondria to provide energy for swimming.
The long tail provides motility, allowing the sperm to swim toward the egg.
Question 40

What is the role of FSH and LH in the menstrual cycle?

FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) stimulates the growth of follicles in the ovary.
It also triggers the follicle to secrete the hormone estrogen.
LH (Luteinizing Hormone) triggers the release of the mature egg (ovulation).
After ovulation, LH helps convert the empty follicle into the Corpus Luteum.
These hormones are secreted by the pituitary gland in the brain.
Their balance is crucial for a regular and healthy menstrual cycle.
Question 41

What is the role of Estrogen and Progesterone?

Estrogen is produced by the developing follicle and thickens the uterine lining.
It also develops secondary sexual characteristics in females.
Progesterone is produced by the Corpus Luteum after ovulation.
It prepares the uterus for the implantation of a fertilized egg.
Progesterone maintains the pregnancy if fertilization occurs.
If progesterone levels drop, the uterine lining sheds, leading to menstruation.
Question 42

Explain the process of 'Implantation'.

[Image of blastocyst implantation in the uterine lining]
After fertilization in the oviduct, the zygote divides into a ball of cells.
This ball of cells (blastocyst) travels down to the uterus.
It attaches itself to the thick, nutrient-rich lining of the uterus.
This attachment process is known as "Implantation."
Once implanted, the embryo starts receiving oxygen and nutrients from the mother.
Implantation marks the official beginning of a pregnancy.
Question 43

What is the role of the Placenta?

The placenta is a temporary organ formed between the mother and the fetus.
It connects to the fetus through the umbilical cord.
Its main function is to supply oxygen and nutrients from the mother's blood.
It also removes waste products like CO2 and urea from the fetal blood.
The placenta acts as a barrier, protecting the fetus from certain infections.
It also produces hormones necessary for maintaining the pregnancy.
Question 44

Define 'Ovulation'.

Ovulation is the release of a mature egg from the ovary.
It usually happens around the 14th day of a 28-day menstrual cycle.
It is triggered by a sudden surge in the level of Luteinizing Hormone (LH).
The egg enters the oviduct where it survives for about 24 hours.
This is the "fertile window" when the chance of pregnancy is highest.
If the egg is not fertilized during this time, it begins to disintegrate.
Question 45

What are Sexually Transmitted Diseases (STDs)?

STDs are infections passed from one person to another through sexual contact.
They can be caused by bacteria, viruses, or parasites.
Common STDs include HIV/AIDS, Syphilis, Gonorrhea, and Herpes.
Many STDs do not show symptoms initially but can cause long-term damage.
Prevention includes using protection and maintaining a single faithful partner.
Early diagnosis and treatment are essential to prevent complications and spread.
Question 46

Explain the difference between primary and accessory whorls.

Primary whorls are directly involved in the process of reproduction.
In a flower, the Androecium and Gynoecium are the primary whorls.
Accessory whorls do not produce gametes but help in the process.
The Calyx and Corolla are the accessory whorls of a flower.
Accessory whorls protect the inner parts and attract pollinators with color or scent.
A flower can survive without accessory whorls, but not without primary ones for sexual reproduction.
Question 47

What is 'Bisexual' and 'Unisexual' in terms of organisms?

A bisexual organism (hermaphrodite) has both male and female reproductive organs.
Earthworms and leeches are examples of bisexual animals.
Hibiscus is an example of a bisexual flower.
A unisexual organism has either male or female reproductive organs, not both.
Humans and most mammals are unisexual organisms.
Papaya and watermelon are examples of unisexual flowers.
Question 48

Why is cleanliness important for reproductive health?

The reproductive organs are prone to bacterial and fungal infections.
Menstrual hygiene is vital for women to prevent pelvic inflammatory diseases.
Lack of cleanliness can lead to itching, burning, and foul odors.
Infections can spread to the urinary tract or internal organs if not managed.
Simple habits like washing and wearing clean cotton underwear are effective.
Health education helps in removing myths about reproductive hygiene.
Question 49

What is 'Tuberous Root' propagation?

[Image of tuberous roots in sweet potato]
Some plants store food in their roots, which become swollen and tuberous.
Sweet potatoes and Dahlias are common examples of this.
These roots contain adventitious buds that can grow into new plants.
When planted in soil, these buds sprout using the stored nutrients.
This is a form of natural vegetative propagation.
It allows the plant to survive through unfavorable seasons and multiply.
Question 50

Summarize the role of science in improving reproductive outcomes.

Science has provided a deep understanding of the hormonal control of reproduction.
Medical advancements like IVF and surrogacy have solved infertility problems.
Modern contraception has allowed for effective family planning and population control.
Diagnostic tools like ultrasound help in monitoring the health of the fetus.
Treatments for STDs have improved life expectancy and quality of life.
Genetic screening allows for the early detection of hereditary disorders in the fetus.
