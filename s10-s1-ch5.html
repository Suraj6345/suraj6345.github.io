1. Define Latent Heat of Fusion.

It is the heat energy absorbed at a constant temperature when a solid converts into a liquid.
This energy is utilized to weaken the bonds between the molecules of the solid.
The temperature of the substance does not rise during this phase transition.
For ice, the specific latent heat of fusion is 80 cal/g.
In the SI system, it is measured in Joules per kilogram (J/kg).
The process occurs specifically at the melting point of the substance.

2. Explain the concept of Latent Heat of Vaporization.

It is the heat energy absorbed at a constant temperature during the transformation of liquid into gas.
The supplied heat is used to completely break the intermolecular forces of the liquid.
The temperature remains stationary until the entire liquid has turned into vapor.
For water, the specific latent heat of vaporization is 540 cal/g.
This "hidden" heat does not increase the kinetic energy of the molecules.
It explains why steam at 100°C is more dangerous than boiling water at the same temperature.

3. Describe the phenomenon of Regelation.

Regelation is the process where ice melts under pressure and refreezes when pressure is removed.
When pressure is applied to ice, its melting point decreases below 0°C.
This causes the ice to turn into water at a temperature lower than its normal melting point.
As soon as the pressure is released, the melting point returns to 0°C.
The water then immediately converts back into ice.
This property allows a wire with weights to pass through a block of ice without cutting it in two.

4. What is the Anomalous Behavior of Water?

Most liquids expand on heating, but water shows an exception between 0°C and 4°C.
If water is heated from 0°C, it contracts instead of expanding until it reaches 4°C.
At 4°C, the volume of a given mass of water is at its minimum.
As the temperature rises above 4°C, water begins to expand like other liquids.
If water is cooled from 4°C to 0°C, its volume increases.
This unique property is known as the anomalous expansion of water.

5. How does the anomalous behavior of water support aquatic life?

In cold regions, the surface water of lakes cools down to 4°C first.
Being dense, this 4°C water sinks to the bottom, pushing warmer water up.
Eventually, the entire body of water reaches 4°C.
If cooling continues, surface water becomes lighter (less than 4°C) and stays at the top.
The surface eventually freezes into ice at 0°C, which floats on the denser 4°C water.
Ice acts as an insulator, keeping the water below liquid and allowing fish to survive.

6. Explain the construction of Hope’s Apparatus.

It consists of a tall metallic cylinder designed to hold water.
A flat trough or bowl is attached to the center of the cylinder.
This central trough is filled with a freezing mixture of ice and salt.
There are two thermometers, one at the top (T1) and one at the bottom (T2).
These thermometers monitor the temperature changes in the different layers of water.
It is specifically designed to study the density changes in water near 4°C.

7. Describe the observations of Hope’s experiment.

Initially, both thermometers show the same room temperature.
As the middle part cools, the bottom thermometer (T2) drops rapidly to 4°C.
T2 then stays constant at 4°C while the top thermometer (T1) starts dropping.
T1 eventually drops all the way to 0°C while T2 remains at 4°C.
This happens because water at 4°C is at its maximum density and stays at the bottom.
Water cooler than 4°C is lighter and rises to the top layer.

8. Define Dew Point Temperature.

Air has a certain capacity to hold water vapor which depends on its temperature.
If unsaturated air is cooled, its capacity to hold moisture decreases.
A specific temperature is reached where the air becomes fully saturated with vapor.
This temperature is called the dew point temperature.
Below this temperature, excess water vapor condenses into liquid droplets.
The dew point depends on the initial amount of vapor present in the air.

9. Distinguish between Saturated and Unsaturated Air.

Saturated air contains the maximum possible water vapor at a given temperature.
Unsaturated air contains less water vapor than its total holding capacity.
Saturated air has a relative humidity of exactly 100%.
Unsaturated air can still absorb more moisture if it is added.
The amount of vapor for saturation decreases as the temperature drops.
We feel humid in saturated air and dry in unsaturated air.

10. Define Absolute Humidity.

It is the mass of water vapor present in a unit volume of air.
It is typically expressed in the unit of grams per cubic meter (g/m³).
It indicates the actual quantity of moisture without referring to temperature.
It does not provide information about how close the air is to saturation.
It is a critical measurement for meteorological studies.
The absolute humidity of air changes as air volume changes with pressure.

11. Define Relative Humidity.

It is the ratio of actual vapor mass in air to the mass needed for saturation.
It is always expressed as a percentage value.
The formula is: (Actual mass of vapor / Mass for saturation) × 100.
If relative humidity is greater than 60%, we feel the air is humid.
If relative humidity is less than 60%, we feel the air is dry.
At the dew point, the relative humidity is always 100%.

12. Why do we see water droplets on the outside of a cold glass?

The air surrounding the cold glass contains invisible water vapor.
When this air touches the cold surface, its temperature drops.
As the temperature falls, the air reaches its dew point.
The air becomes saturated and can no longer hold its water vapor.
The excess vapor undergoes condensation into liquid water.
These droplets then accumulate on the outer surface of the glass.

13. Explain the formation of white trails behind airplanes.

Airplane engines release water vapor as a byproduct of fuel combustion.
At high altitudes, the atmospheric temperature is extremely low.
The vapor released by the engine condenses rapidly into ice crystals.
If the surrounding air has high relative humidity, these trails last a long time.
If the air is dry (low humidity), the trails disappear quickly.
These trails are essentially narrow clouds formed by artificial condensation.

14. Define the unit 'Calorie'.

One calorie is the heat needed to raise the temperature of 1g of water by 1°C.
The temperature rise is specifically measured from 14.5°C to 15.5°C.
It is the standard unit of heat in the CGS system.
1 kilocalorie (kcal) is equal to 1000 calories.
1 kcal is the heat needed to raise 1kg of water by 1°C.
The conversion rate to work is 1 calorie = 4.18 Joules.

15. Define Specific Heat Capacity.

It is the heat needed to raise the temperature of unit mass of a substance by 1°C.
Every substance has a unique specific heat capacity based on its nature.
It is denoted by the letter ‘c’.
The SI unit for specific heat capacity is J/kg°C.
The CGS unit for specific heat capacity is cal/g°C.
Materials with low specific heat capacity heat up and cool down very quickly.

16. State the Principle of Heat Exchange.

This principle applies to an isolated system containing hot and cold objects.
Heat energy always flows from the hot object to the cold object.
The flow continues until both objects reach a common temperature.
Law: Heat energy lost by hot object = Heat energy gained by cold object.
It assumes that no heat is lost to the surrounding environment.
This principle is used in calorimetry to find the specific heat of substances.

17. Give the formula for heat absorbed by an object.

The heat absorbed (Q) is directly proportional to the mass (m) of the object.
It is also proportional to the specific heat capacity (c) of the material.
It depends on the change in temperature (ΔT).
Q = m × c × ΔT
Here, ΔT is calculated as (Final temperature - Initial temperature).
This formula is used only when there is no change in the state of matter.

18. Describe the Wax Tray Experiment (Iron, Copper, Lead).

Iron, copper, and lead spheres of equal mass are heated to 100°C.
They are then placed simultaneously on a thick tray of paraffin wax.
The iron sphere sinks deepest into the wax tray.
The lead sphere sinks the least among the three.
Iron sinks deeper because it absorbed and stored more heat than the others.
This proves that different substances have different specific heat capacities.

19. Why is the specific heat of water significant?

Water has a very high specific heat capacity of 1 cal/g°C.
It can absorb a large amount of heat without a significant rise in temperature.
This makes water an excellent coolant for car radiators and industrial machines.
Oceans and lakes help regulate the Earth's temperature because of this property.
Water is used in hot water bags for fermentation as it stays warm for a long time.
High specific heat prevents biological cells from freezing or overheating easily.

20. Explain why steam causes more severe burns than boiling water.

Both steam and boiling water can exist at a temperature of 100°C.
Steam contains an additional amount of heat called the latent heat of vaporization.
This extra energy is approximately 540 calories for every gram of steam.
When steam hits the skin, it releases this extra energy during condensation.
The total heat transferred to the skin is much higher than that of boiling water.
Therefore, steam results in deeper and more painful tissue damage.

21. What are the factors affecting the boiling point of a liquid?

Atmospheric pressure is the primary factor affecting the boiling point.
As external pressure increases, the boiling point of the liquid also increases.
In a pressure cooker, high pressure allows water to boil at temperatures above 100°C.
At high altitudes, low pressure causes water to boil at less than 100°C.
The presence of dissolved impurities like salt increases the boiling point.
The nature of the chemical bonds in the liquid also determines its boiling point.

22. How does the melting point change with pressure?

For substances like ice, the melting point decreases as pressure increases.
This is the reason why ice melts when we apply pressure during regelation.
For most other substances, the melting point increases with increased pressure.
Increased pressure usually forces molecules closer, favoring the solid state.
The change in melting point depends on whether the substance expands or contracts on melting.
This relationship is crucial in geological processes deep within the Earth.

23. Define Thermal Equilibrium.

It is the state where two objects in contact reach the same temperature.
At this point, the net exchange of heat energy between them becomes zero.
It is the final condition predicted by the principle of heat exchange.
A thermometer measures the temperature of an object once it reaches thermal equilibrium.
In an isolated system, the total energy remains constant during the reach for equilibrium.
Objects in thermal equilibrium do not feel hot or cold relative to each other.

24. Why is salt used to melt ice on roads?

Adding salt to ice creates an impurity in the pure substance.
This impurity lowers the freezing point of water below 0°C.
This phenomenon is known as the depression of freezing point.
At normal winter temperatures, the salt-ice mixture stays in the liquid state.
This prevents the formation of dangerous black ice on the road surface.
It is a practical application of the effect of impurities on phase changes.

25. Distinguish between Heat and Temperature.

Heat is the total thermal energy possessed by the molecules of a substance.
Temperature is the measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules.
Heat flows from an object at higher temperature to one at lower temperature.
The SI unit of heat is the Joule, while for temperature it is Kelvin.
Heat is an extensive property, while temperature is an intensive property.
Adding heat to a body usually increases its temperature (except during phase change).

26. What happens to the temperature during the boiling of water?

When water reaches 100°C at standard pressure, it begins to boil.
As heat is continuously added, the temperature stays exactly at 100°C.
The heat is absorbed as latent heat of vaporization to turn liquid to gas.
The molecules use this energy to overcome the atmospheric pressure and escape.
The kinetic energy of the molecules does not increase during this stage.
The temperature only rises again once all the water has become steam.

27. What is the role of a calorimeter in experiments?

A calorimeter is a container used to measure the heat of chemical reactions or physical changes.
It is usually made of a good conductor like copper to reach equilibrium quickly.
It is placed in an insulating jacket to prevent heat loss to the environment.
A thermometer is used to record the initial and final temperatures of the contents.
It allows for the practical application of the principle of heat exchange.
It is used to calculate specific heat, latent heat, or heat of reaction.

28. Define Humidity and its effect on our daily life.

Humidity is the measure of the amount of water vapor present in the atmosphere.
High humidity makes us feel "sticky" because sweat does not evaporate easily.
Low humidity makes our skin feel dry and can cause chapped lips.
It determines the rate at which wet clothes dry when hung outside.
Humidity levels are crucial for weather forecasting and predicting rain.
High humidity can lead to the growth of mold and mildew in houses.

29. Why does ice float on water?

Ice floats because its density is lower than the density of liquid water.
Due to anomalous expansion, water expands as it cools from 4°C to 0°C.
This expansion increases the volume of the same mass of water.
Increased volume leads to a decrease in density (Density = Mass / Volume).
At 0°C, ice forms a crystalline structure with more space between molecules.
Therefore, ice is lighter than the water it displaces and it floats.

30. Explain the conversion of units between Calorie and Joule.

Both calories and Joules are units used to measure energy and heat.
The Joule is the standard SI unit used globally in physics.
The calorie is a CGS unit often used in chemistry and nutrition.
1 calorie is defined as exactly 4.184 Joules of energy.
For simple calculations, it is often taken as 4.2 Joules.
To convert calories to Joules, we multiply the value by 4.184.
