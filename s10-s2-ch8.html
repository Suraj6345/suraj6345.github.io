Chapter 8: Cell Biology & Biotechnology

Question 1

Define Cytology (Cell Biology).

[Image of animal cell structure with organelles]
It is the branch of biology that deals with the study of cells.
It includes the study of cell structure, types, and various cell organelles.
Modern cytology also examines cell division and cell functions at a molecular level.
It is the foundation for understanding complex biological processes in organisms.
Advances in cytology have led to major breakthroughs in medical science.
Research in this field is crucial for understanding diseases like cancer.
Question 2

What are Stem Cells?

Stem cells are special types of primary cells in the bodies of multicellular organisms.
These cells are undifferentiated and have the potential to become any type of cell.
They give rise to all other types of cells present in the body.
They are the source of all tissues and organs during embryonic development.
Stem cells have the unique property of self-renewal through cell division.
They play a vital role in the healing and regeneration of damaged tissues.
Question 3

What is Stem Cell Preservation?

It involves collecting and storing stem cells for potential future medical use.
Stem cells from the umbilical cord blood are often collected at the time of birth.
These cells are also collected from bone marrow or adipose (fat) tissue.
The collected samples are kept in sterile vials to maintain their viability.
These vials are kept in liquid nitrogen at extremely low temperatures (-135Â°C to -190Â°C).
This ensures the cells can be used decades later for personalized therapy.
Question 4

Explain Embryonic Stem Cells.

[Image of blastocyst showing inner cell mass]
These are stem cells derived from the early-stage embryo (blastocyst).
Before cells start to differentiate, they are called embryonic stem cells.
There are about 220 different types of cells in the human body derived from them.
They are "pluripotent," meaning they can transform into almost any cell type.
They are primarily used in research to understand human development.
Their use is often subject to ethical debates and strict regulations.
Question 5

What are Adult Stem Cells?

These are stem cells found in developed tissues of the body after birth.
They are found in bone marrow, adipose tissue, and even in the blood.
They can also be obtained from the umbilical cord immediately after birth.
These cells are "multipotent," meaning they can form a limited range of cell types.
They are primarily used by the body for natural repair and maintenance.
They are widely used in medical treatments like bone marrow transplants.
Question 6

List the main uses of Stem Cells in Regenerative Therapy.

They are used to replace dead or damaged cells in organs like the heart and liver.
They help in treating conditions like Parkinson's disease and Alzheimer's.
Stem cells are used to produce new blood cells in patients with Leukemia.
They can be used to treat Diabetes by replacing insulin-producing cells.
They are used in skin grafting for patients with severe burn injuries.
Research is ongoing to grow entire functional organs using stem cell scaffolds.
Question 7

Define Biotechnology.

It is the use of living organisms or their products to modify human health and the environment.
It involves the integration of natural sciences and engineering for the benefit of mankind.
It uses techniques like genetic engineering and tissue culture to improve traits.
It includes various fields like agriculture, medicine, and food technology.
Modern biotechnology relies heavily on DNA manipulation and cloning.
It aims to solve global problems like food shortage and incurable diseases.
Question 8

What are the main areas of Biotechnology?

Genetic engineering focuses on manipulating the DNA of organisms directly.
Tissue culture involves growing cells and tissues in a controlled environment.
Micro-biology uses microbes for industrial production of enzymes and acids.
Protein engineering is used to produce synthetic proteins and hormones.
Environmental biotechnology focuses on waste management and pollution control.
Food biotechnology improves the nutritional value and shelf life of products.
Question 9

How does Biotechnology benefit Agriculture?

It helps in the production of high-yielding and disease-resistant crop varieties.
It allows for the development of crops that can grow in harsh climates.
The use of chemical fertilizers and pesticides is reduced significantly.
The nutritional value of food can be enhanced (e.g., Vitamin A in rice).
Post-harvest losses are reduced by increasing the shelf life of fruits.
It enables the production of "Stress-resistant" plants that survive droughts.
Question 10

What are Genetically Modified (GM) Crops?

These are crops whose DNA has been altered using genetic engineering.
Foreign genes are inserted to provide specific beneficial characteristics.
These genes may come from other plants, bacteria, or even animals.
Examples include resistance to pests, herbicides, or environmental stress.
GM crops aim to increase agricultural productivity and sustainability.
They are strictly tested for safety before being released for farming.
Question 11

Explain "BT Cotton".

A gene from the bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis is inserted into cotton.
This gene produces a toxin that is lethal to the bollworm larvae.
When the bollworm eats the cotton leaves, the toxin paralyzes its gut.
This results in the death of the pest, protecting the cotton crop.
It significantly reduces the need for external chemical pesticides.
BT Cotton has greatly increased the profit margins for cotton farmers.
Question 12

What is "Golden Rice"?

[Image comparing normal rice and golden rice]
It is a genetically modified variety of rice produced using biotechnology.
It is engineered to contain a high amount of Beta-carotene (Pro-vitamin A).
The rice appears golden-yellow in color due to this added nutrient.
It was developed to combat Vitamin A deficiency in developing countries.
Modern varieties contain 23 times more Beta-carotene than normal rice.
It is a key example of "Bio-fortification" for public health.
Question 13

What is Tissue Culture?

It is the growth of plant or animal cells in an artificial nutrient medium.
The process is carried out in completely sterile, laboratory conditions.
A single cell or a piece of tissue (explant) can grow into a whole plant.
It allows for the rapid multiplication of rare or endangered species.
It is used to produce "Disease-free" plants from infected parents.
This technique is essential for the commercial production of flowers and fruits.
Question 14

Name the stages of Tissue Culture.

[Image illustrating stages of plant tissue culture]
Primary cleaning and sterilization of the explant (source tissue).
Inoculation of the tissue onto a nutrient-rich agar medium.
Multiplication and callus (undifferentiated mass) formation.
Organogenesis, where roots and shoots begin to develop.
Hardening, where plants are gradually adjusted to outside conditions.
Final transfer of the hardened plants to the soil in a nursery.
Question 15

How is Biotechnology used in Human Health?

It is used for the early diagnosis of hereditary and infectious diseases.
It enables the production of life-saving hormones like Insulin.
Vaccines are produced using recombinant DNA for better safety.
Gene therapy is used to treat genetic disorders at the molecular level.
It helps in producing "Interferons" for treating viral infections and cancer.
Personalized medicine is developed based on an individual's genetic makeup.
Question 16

What is "Humulin"?

It is human insulin produced using genetically modified bacteria.
The gene for human insulin is inserted into the DNA of E. coli.
The bacteria then act as "factories" to produce the hormone.
Previously, insulin was extracted from the pancreas of slaughtered animals.
Humulin is safer as it does not cause allergic reactions in humans.
It has made diabetes management much more accessible and effective.
Question 17

Explain "Edible Vaccines".

These are vaccines produced within the edible parts of plants like potatoes.
Genes from a pathogen are inserted into the plant's genome.
Eating the raw plant material triggers an immune response in the body.
They do not require cold storage or syringes for administration.
This makes them ideal for use in remote and underdeveloped areas.
Research is ongoing for edible vaccines against cholera and hepatitis.
Question 18

What is Gene Therapy?

[Image showing functional gene insertion into a cell]
It is a technique to treat diseases by correcting faulty genes.
A functional "normal" gene is inserted to replace a defective one.
It is primarily used for genetic disorders like Cystic Fibrosis.
It can be done in somatic cells (body cells) or germ cells.
"Somatic Gene Therapy" is currently the only approved method in humans.
It offers hope for curing diseases that were previously thought incurable.
Question 19

What are Interferons?

These are a group of small proteins produced by cells in response to viruses.
They "interfere" with viral replication and stimulate the immune system.
Nowadays, they are produced industrially using biotechnology.
They are used in the treatment of Hepatitis B, C, and some cancers.
Most interferons are produced using genetically modified E. coli bacteria.
They are essential components of modern anti-viral therapy.
Question 20

What is "DNA Fingerprinting"?

It is a technique used to identify individuals based on their DNA sequence.
Since the DNA of every person is unique, it acts like a digital fingerprint.
It is widely used in forensic science to solve crimes using blood or hair.
It is used in paternity tests to determine biological parents.
It helps in the identification of victims in mass disasters.
The Center for DNA Fingerprinting and Diagnostics (CDFD) is located in Hyderabad.
Question 21

Explain the "Green Revolution".

It refers to the massive increase in food grain production since the 1960s.
It involved the use of high-yielding varieties (HYV) of wheat and rice.
Dr. Norman Borlaug (USA) and Dr. M.S. Swaminathan (India) were the pioneers.
It used improved irrigation, chemical fertilizers, and modern machinery.
India became self-sufficient in food grains due to this revolution.
However, it also led to concerns regarding soil health and water table depletion.
Question 22

What is the "White Revolution"?

It refers to the large-scale increase in milk production in India.
It is also known as "Operation Flood," launched in 1970.
Dr. Verghese Kurien is known as the "Father of the White Revolution."
It was achieved through the cooperative movement (Anand, Gujarat).
It involved better breeding of cattle and modern processing technology.
India is now the largest producer of milk in the world.
Question 23

Explain the "Blue Revolution".

It refers to the rapid increase in the production of fish and marine products.
It involves "Aquaculture" (farming of aquatic organisms) on a large scale.
Government support is provided through the "Nil Kranti Mission."
It includes the breeding of freshwater fish like Rohu and marine prawns.
It provides a rich source of protein and creates employment in coastal areas.
Modern techniques like "Cage Culture" are used to increase productivity.
Question 24

What is "Organ Donation"?

It is the process of donating an organ to a person who needs a transplant.
Organs can be donated by a living person or after "Brain Death."
Liver, kidney, and skin can often be donated by living donors.
After brain death, vital organs like the heart, lungs, and eyes can be harvested.
One donor can save or improve the lives of up to eight people.
It is considered a noble act that requires legal consent and ethical handling.
Question 25

Why is Organ and Body Donation important?

There is a huge gap between the demand and supply of functional organs.
Many patients die waiting for a suitable donor for heart or kidney transplants.
Body donation is essential for medical students to study human anatomy.
It promotes a culture of social responsibility and human welfare.
It helps in advancing medical research for complex diseases.
Donating eyes can give sight to blind individuals through corneal transplant.
Question 26

Name the types of Stem Cells based on their sources.

Embryonic stem cells are obtained from the blastocyst stage of an embryo.
Umbilical cord stem cells are collected immediately after birth from the cord.
Bone marrow stem cells are found in the red bone marrow of adults.
Adipose tissue stem cells are extracted from the fat layers of the body.
Peripheral blood stem cells are found in the circulating blood.
Amniotic fluid stem cells are present in the fluid surrounding the fetus.
Question 27

What is "Pluripotency"?

It is the ability of a stem cell to differentiate into any of the 220 cell types.
Only embryonic stem cells (from the 14-day old embryo) are truly pluripotent.
They can form cells of all three germ layers: Ectoderm, Mesoderm, and Endoderm.
They cannot, however, form extra-embryonic tissues like the placenta.
This property makes them the most valuable for medical research.
Controlling this pluripotency is the main challenge in stem cell therapy.
Question 28

How is Biotechnology used in Environment Management?

Microbes are used to decompose domestic and industrial organic waste.
"Bioremediation" uses microbes to clean up oil spills and heavy metals.
Specific bacteria are used to treat sewage and produce biogas.
Plants are used to absorb toxic chemicals from the soil (Phytoremediation).
It helps in the production of biodegradable plastics (Bio-plastics).
It enables the detection of pollutants using sensitive biosensors.
Question 29

What are "Bio-pesticides"?

These are pesticides derived from natural materials like plants and microbes.
They are non-toxic to humans and beneficial insects like bees.
Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) is a common microbial bio-pesticide.
Neem extract is a popular plant-based bio-pesticide used in India.
They do not leave harmful chemical residues on the food crops.
They are essential for sustainable and organic farming practices.
Question 30

Explain "Bioremediation" of heavy metals.

Some microbes can absorb or neutralize toxic metals like Lead and Mercury.
The bacterium Pseudomonas is used to remove heavy metals from wastewater.
The fungus Saccharomyces can absorb heavy metals like Arsenic.
These microbes prevent the metals from entering the food chain.
It is a cost-effective alternative to chemical cleaning of soil.
Genetic engineering is used to create "Super-microbes" for better results.
Question 31

What is the role of the "National Board of Stem Cell Research" (NBSCR)?

It is the regulatory body in India for stem cell research and therapy.
It ensures that all stem cell research follows strict ethical guidelines.
It prevents the commercial exploitation and misuse of stem cell technology.
It provides approval for clinical trials using stem cells.
It encourages scientific collaboration and funding in the field.
It protects the rights and safety of patients undergoing stem cell therapy.
Question 32

What is "Cloning"?

[Image of somatic cell nuclear transfer process]
It is the process of creating a genetically identical copy of an organism.
"Reproductive cloning" aims to produce a whole new organism (e.g., Dolly the sheep).
"Therapeutic cloning" aims to produce stem cells for medical treatment.
In cloning, the nucleus of a somatic cell is transferred to an enucleated egg.
It can be used to preserve endangered species from extinction.
Human reproductive cloning is globally banned due to ethical concerns.
Question 33

Who was "Dolly" the sheep?

[Image of Dolly the cloned sheep]
Dolly was the first mammal to be cloned from an adult somatic cell.
She was born in 1996 at the Roslin Institute in Scotland.
She was created using the nucleus of a mammary gland cell of a Finn Dorset sheep.
Her birth proved that specialized cells could be "reprogrammed" to be embryonic.
Dolly lived for six years and gave birth to several lambs naturally.
Her creation was a landmark event in the history of biotechnology.
Question 34

What is "Animal Husbandry" in Biotechnology?

It involves using biotechnology to improve the health and yield of livestock.
Artificial Insemination (AI) is used to produce high-quality offspring.
Embryo Transfer (ET) allows a superior cow to produce many calves a year.
Genetically modified animals are produced for better meat or milk quality.
Vaccines are developed for animals to prevent diseases like Foot and Mouth.
It helps in the conservation of rare and high-yielding local breeds.
Question 35

Explain "Bio-fortification".

It is the process of increasing the nutritional value of crops through breeding.
It can be done through traditional breeding or genetic engineering.
The goal is to make crops richer in vitamins, minerals, or healthy fats.
Examples include Golden Rice (Vitamin A) and Iron-fortified beans.
It is a sustainable way to fight "Hidden Hunger" in poor populations.
It improves public health without requiring a change in dietary habits.
Question 36

What is "Pharmacogenomics"?

It is the study of how genes affect a person's response to drugs.
It combines pharmacology (science of drugs) and genomics.
The goal is to develop "Personalized Medicine" for patients.
It helps in predicting which drug will be most effective for an individual.
It reduces the risk of adverse drug reactions and side effects.
It is a major application of biotechnology in modern healthcare.
Question 37

What are "BT Crops"?

These are crops engineered with a gene from the soil bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis.
The gene produces a "Cry protein" which is toxic to specific insects.
Common examples include BT Cotton, BT Brinjal, and BT Corn.
They provide "In-built" protection against pests throughout the season.
This reduces the farmer's expenditure on chemical sprays.
BT Brinjal has been a subject of intense safety debate in India.
Question 38

What is the "Human Genome Project" (HGP)?

[Image representing the human genome mapping]
It was an international research project to map all human genes.
It aimed to determine the sequence of the 3 billion chemical base pairs in DNA.
The project was completed in 2003 after 13 years of work.
It revealed that humans have approximately 20,000 to 25,000 genes.
It has provided invaluable data for understanding genetic diseases.
It is the foundation for the field of "Genomics."
Question 39

How is Biotechnology used in the Food Industry?

Microbes are used for the large-scale production of yogurt, cheese, and wine.
Enzymes are used to improve the texture and flavor of processed foods.
Natural food colors and preservatives are produced using biotechnology.
It helps in the production of low-calorie sweeteners like Stevia.
Fermentation technology is used to produce "Single Cell Protein" (SCP).
It improves the safety of food by detecting harmful pathogens quickly.
Question 40

What is "Biosafety"?

It refers to the measures taken to prevent the accidental release of GM organisms.
It ensures that biotechnology does not harm human health or the environment.
Strict regulations are in place for the transport and use of GM seeds.
Labs working with high-risk microbes must follow "Biosafety Levels" (BSL 1-4).
It involves assessing the long-term impact of GM crops on biodiversity.
Global bodies like the WHO set international biosafety standards.
Question 41

What is "Molecular Diagnosis"?

It is the use of biotechnology to detect diseases at the DNA or RNA level.
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) is a common technique used.
It can detect pathogens like HIV or Malaria even at very low levels.
It allows for the early diagnosis of diseases before symptoms appear.
It is much more accurate and faster than traditional blood tests.
It was widely used for testing during the COVID-19 pandemic.
Question 42

What are "Transgenic Animals"?

These are animals whose genome has been altered by inserting a foreign gene.
They are used as "Bio-reactors" to produce valuable human proteins.
For example, transgenic sheep can produce a human protein in their milk.
They are used as models to study human diseases like cancer and diabetes.
Transgenic mice are commonly used for testing the safety of new drugs.
The ethics of creating transgenic animals are strictly monitored.
Question 43

What is "Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer" (SCNT)?

[Image showing nuclear transfer steps]
It is the primary technique used for animal cloning.
The nucleus of a somatic (body) cell is removed from the donor.
This nucleus is inserted into an egg cell whose own nucleus was removed.
An electric pulse triggers the egg to start dividing as an embryo.
The embryo is then implanted into a surrogate mother.
SCNT is also used in research to produce patient-specific stem cells.
Question 44

What are the ethical issues in Biotechnology?

Cloning of humans is considered unethical and a violation of human dignity.
Patenting of living organisms and genes is a controversial topic.
There are concerns about the long-term health effects of eating GM food.
Genetic modification of human embryos ("Designer Babies") is debated.
The potential use of biotechnology for "Biological Warfare" is a threat.
Using animals for research raises concerns about animal cruelty.
Question 45

What is "Apiculture"?

It is the commercial rearing of honey bees for honey and wax.
Biotechnology helps in selecting high-yielding and docile bee varieties.
It is used to diagnose and treat diseases in bee colonies.
Honey is a natural antiseptic and a rich source of energy.
Beeswax is used in the pharmaceutical and cosmetic industries.
Bees are vital pollinators for more than 70% of our food crops.
Question 46

What is "Sericulture"?

[Image showing silkworm life cycle and silk cocoons]
It is the rearing of silkworms for the production of silk.
Biotechnology is used to improve the quality of mulberry leaves (food).
It helps in developing silkworm varieties that produce more silk.
Disease-resistant silkworms are produced using selective breeding.
Silk is a natural protein fiber known for its strength and luster.
India is the second-largest producer of silk in the world.
Question 47

Explain the "Yellow Revolution".

It refers to the rapid increase in the production of edible oilseeds.
It was launched in the 1980s to make India self-sufficient in oil.
It focused on crops like Mustard, Groundnut, and Sunflower.
It involved the use of improved seeds and modern extraction technology.
The "Technology Mission on Oilseeds" was the driving force.
It significantly reduced the import of edible oils into the country.
Question 48

What is "Fishery" biotechnology?

It involves using genetic tools to improve fish growth and disease resistance.
"Transgenic fish" are produced to grow much faster than normal varieties.
Cryopreservation is used to store fish sperm for long-term breeding.
Biotechnology helps in identifying the sex of fish at an early stage.
It is used to produce vaccines for fish against bacterial infections.
It enhances the production of ornamental (aquarium) fish.
Question 49

What is "Bio-piracy"?

It is the unauthorized use of biological resources or traditional knowledge.
It usually involves companies from developed nations patenting local knowledge.
For example, the attempt to patent Neem or Turmeric properties.
It deprives the local communities of the benefits of their own heritage.
International laws like the "Nagoya Protocol" aim to stop bio-piracy.
India has created a "Traditional Knowledge Digital Library" (TKDL) to prevent this.
Question 50

What is "Bio-patent"?

It is a legal right granted for a biological invention (like a new GM seed).
It allows the inventor to exclude others from using the invention for a set time.
It encourages innovation by providing financial rewards to researchers.
However, it can make seeds and medicines more expensive for poor farmers.
Patents on natural human genes are generally not allowed anymore.
The criteria for a bio-patent are novelty, utility, and non-obviousness.
Question 51

How do microbes help in "Oil Spill" cleanup?

Bacteria like Alcanivorax borkumensis eat hydrocarbons in oil.
They break down the oil into non-toxic $CO_2$ and water.
Biotechnology creates "Superbugs" that work faster in cold ocean water.
They are much more effective than mechanical skimming of oil.
Nutrients are often added to the water to help these bacteria grow.
This is the most eco-friendly way to handle large-scale marine pollution.
Question 52

What is "Somatotropin"?

It is a growth hormone produced by the pituitary gland.
Biotechnology is used to produce synthetic somatotropin using bacteria.
It is used to treat growth disorders in children.
In some countries, it is given to cattle to increase milk production.
However, its use in livestock is controversial and banned in many places.
It is a key example of a protein produced through recombinant DNA.
Question 53

What is "Erythropoietin" (EPO)?

It is a hormone that stimulates the production of red blood cells.
Synthetic EPO is produced using biotechnology for medical use.
It is used to treat anemia in patients with chronic kidney disease.
It is also used during chemotherapy to boost blood cell counts.
Unfortunately, it is sometimes misused by athletes for "blood doping."
It has significantly improved the quality of life for dialysis patients.
Question 54

What is "Plasmids" in Genetic Engineering?

Plasmids are small, circular DNA molecules found in bacteria.
They are separate from the main bacterial chromosome.
They are used as "vectors" (carriers) to transport foreign genes.
They can replicate independently within the host cell.
Scientists use them to insert human genes into bacteria like E. coli.
They are the most important tools in the recombinant DNA "toolbox."
Question 55

What are "Restriction Enzymes"?

These are special proteins that act like "molecular scissors."
They cut DNA at very specific sequences of base pairs.
They allow scientists to isolate a particular gene from a long DNA strand.
They were originally discovered in bacteria as a defense against viruses.
Different enzymes cut at different "recognition sites."
They are essential for creating recombinant DNA molecules.
Question 56

What is "DNA Ligase"?

It is an enzyme that acts like "molecular glue."
It is used to join two separate DNA fragments together.
It creates a strong bond between the sugar-phosphate backbones.
In biotechnology, it is used to "paste" a human gene into a bacterial plasmid.
Without ligase, recombinant DNA would fall apart.
It is a naturally occurring enzyme involved in DNA repair.
Question 57

What is "Genomics"?

It is the study of the entire genome (all genes) of an organism.
It focuses on the structure, function, and evolution of genomes.
It uses high-speed computers to analyze massive amounts of DNA data.
It helps in identifying the genes responsible for complex diseases.
Agricultural genomics helps in developing better crop varieties.
It is a rapidly growing field that powers modern biotechnology.
Question 58

What is "Proteomics"?

It is the large-scale study of all proteins produced by an organism.
Since genes code for proteins, proteomics is the next step after genomics.
It helps in understanding how cells function and communicate.
Many diseases are caused by faulty proteins, making this field vital.
It is used to find "Biomarkers" for the early detection of cancer.
It is much more complex than genomics because proteins change constantly.
Question 59

What are "Biosimilars"?

These are biological medicines that are "highly similar" to an original brand.
They are like "generic" versions of complex biotechnology drugs.
They are used to treat conditions like cancer and rheumatoid arthritis.
They are much cheaper than the original "biologic" drugs.
However, they are not exact copies due to the complexity of living cells.
They must undergo strict testing to ensure they are safe and effective.
Question 60

What is "Micropropogation"?

It is a tissue culture technique for producing thousands of plants quickly.
It uses small pieces of plant tissue (explants) grown in a lab.
All the resulting plants are "clones" (genetically identical) to the parent.
It is used for the mass production of bananas, orchids, and potatoes.
It ensures that all plants have the same superior traits (e.g., fruit size).
It is a billion-dollar industry worldwide.
Question 61

What is "Somaclonal Variation"?

These are genetic variations that occur during tissue culture.
Even though the plants are clones, sometimes mutations happen in the lab.
Scientists can use these variations to find new, beneficial traits.
It has led to the development of new varieties of wheat and tomato.
However, for commercial cloning, it is usually seen as a problem.
It shows that the environment of the lab can influence DNA.
Question 62

What is "Cryopreservation"?

It is the storage of biological samples at extremely low temperatures.
Usually, liquid nitrogen at -196Â°C is used.
It is used to preserve stem cells, sperm, eggs, and embryos.
It can also be used to store the seeds of endangered plants (Seed Banks).
At these temperatures, all biological activity stops, preventing aging.
It is a vital tool for both medicine and biodiversity conservation.
Question 63

What is "Bioprocess Engineering"?

It is the design and operation of large tanks (bioreactors) for microbes.
It combines biology and chemical engineering principles.
It ensures that microbes have the perfect conditions to produce products.
It is used to produce beer, antibiotics, and biofuels on a massive scale.
Sterilization and oxygen control are the biggest challenges.
It is the bridge between a lab discovery and a commercial product.
Question 64

What is "Metabolic Engineering"?

It is the manipulation of a cell's chemical reactions (metabolism).
The goal is to make the cell produce more of a specific substance.
For example, making yeast produce more ethanol or a specific vitamin.
It involves adding or deleting genes to redirect the flow of energy.
It is used to produce "Bio-plastics" and "Bio-fuels" more efficiently.
It is like "rewiring" the internal chemistry of a living cell.
Question 65

What is "Bio-hydrogen"?

It is hydrogen gas produced by microbes from organic waste.
Certain bacteria and algae produce hydrogen during fermentation or photosynthesis.
It is the cleanest fuel because it produces only water when burned.
Biotechnology is working to increase the efficiency of this production.
It could be the ultimate solution to the global energy and climate crisis.
Currently, it is still in the research and pilot project stage.
Question 66

What are "Bio-chips"?

[Image of a DNA microarray chip]
These are small devices that contain thousands of DNA or protein sensors.
They are used to perform thousands of biological tests simultaneously.
They can scan a person's entire genome for disease-causing mutations.
They are used in research to see which genes are active in a cancer cell.
They are a combination of biotechnology and semiconductor technology.
They are making medical diagnosis faster and much more detailed.
Question 67

What is "Synthetic Biology"?

It is an advanced field that aims to "build" new biological parts and systems.
It goes beyond just editing DNA; it involves designing DNA from scratch.
The goal is to create "Synthetic Life" that can perform specific tasks.
For example, a cell designed purely to detect and eat plastic in the ocean.
It is a highly powerful and controversial field of science.
It is sometimes called "Biological Engineering."
Question 68

What is "Bio-remediation" of Arsenic?

Arsenic is a toxic heavy metal often found in groundwater.
The fern Pteris vittata can absorb huge amounts of arsenic from the soil.
This is a type of "Phyto-remediation" (using plants).
Microbes can also convert toxic arsenic into less harmful forms.
It is a major solution for the "Arsenic Crisis" in states like West Bengal.
It is a natural and permanent way to clean up contaminated water.
Question 69

What is "BT Brinjal"?

It is a genetically modified brinjal (eggplant) containing the Bt gene.
It is resistant to the "Fruit and Shoot Borer" pest.
It was the first GM food crop developed in India.
However, its commercial release was put under a "Moratorium" (halt) in 2010.
There are concerns about its impact on local brinjal varieties and human health.
It is currently grown commercially in Bangladesh, but not in India.
Question 70

What are "Herbicides" and Biotech?

Herbicides are chemicals used to kill unwanted weeds in a farm.
Biotechnology creates "Herbicide-Tolerant" (HT) crops.
These crops are not affected when the farmer sprays herbicide on the field.
Only the weeds die, leaving the main crop healthy and competition-free.
HT Soybeans and HT Cotton are popular globally.
However, it can lead to the growth of "Super-weeds" that are also resistant.
Question 71

What is "Hormone Therapy"?

It is the medical use of hormones produced via biotechnology.
It is used for people whose bodies do not produce enough of a certain hormone.
Besides insulin and growth hormone, it includes FSH for fertility.
It is also used to manage symptoms of menopause.
Recombinant hormones are safer than those from animal sources.
It is a life-saving application of biotechnology for many.
Question 72

What are "Monoclonal Antibodies" (mAbs)?

These are laboratory-made proteins that mimic the immune system's attack.
They are designed to bind to specific targets, like a cancer cell.
They are produced by "Hybridoma" technology (fusing cells).
They are used to treat cancer, autoimmune diseases, and even COVID-19.
They are very expensive to produce compared to traditional drugs.
They represent the "Guided Missile" approach to medicine.
Question 73

What is "Bio-augmentation"?

It is the addition of specific, lab-grown microbes to a polluted site.
It is used when the local microbes cannot clean up a spill fast enough.
For example, adding specialized bacteria to an industrial wastewater tank.

Question 81

What is the role of "Agrobacterium" in Biotech?

It is a soil bacterium known as the "Natural Genetic Engineer."
It has the ability to transfer its own DNA into a plant cell.
Scientists "disarm" the bacteria by removing its disease-causing genes.
They then use it as a "delivery truck" to carry beneficial genes into plants.
It is the most common method for creating transgenic plants.
It works best with "Dicots" like tomatoes, tobacco, and beans.

These particles are fired at high speed into the plant tissue.

It is used for plants where Agrobacterium does not work well (like rice).
It is a mechanical alternative to biological gene transfer.
Question 83

This makes the plant develop its own cuticle and stronger roots.
Only "hardened" plants can survive when finally planted in the soil.
