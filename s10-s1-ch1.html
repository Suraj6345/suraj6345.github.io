1. What is the definition of the universal force of gravitation and its scope?

Fundamental Attraction: Gravitation is a universal force of attraction that exists between any two objects in the universe that possess mass, regardless of their chemical composition or size.
Universal Nature: This force is not restricted to objects on Earth; it operates between celestial bodies like stars, planets, and galaxies, maintaining the structure of the cosmos.
Historical Discovery: Sir Isaac Newton pioneered this concept by realizing that the force pulling an apple to the ground is the same force keeping the Moon in its orbit around Earth.
Physical Influence: It is responsible for diverse phenomena such as the weight of objects, the atmosphere surrounding planets, and the periodic rising and falling of ocean tides.
Architect of the Universe: Ultimately, gravity acts as the silent architect of the universe, ensuring planetary systems remain stable and that matter aggregates into planets and stars.

2. Explain why a force is necessary to change the state of motion of an object.

Principle of Inertia: According to Newton's First Law, every object has an inherent property called inertia, which is its resistance to changing its current state of rest or motion.
Speed Alteration: To change the speed of an object—either to accelerate it or bring it to a stop—an external unbalanced force must be applied to overcome this inertia.
Directional Change: Even if an object's speed remains constant, a force is required to change its direction, which is why objects in circular motion are constantly under the influence of force.
Vector Change: Since velocity is a vector quantity involving both speed and direction, any change in velocity constitutes acceleration, which fundamentally requires force ($F = ma$).
Orbital Maintenance: In the context of gravity, the Earth's pull acts as the necessary force that constantly redirects the Moon's path, preventing it from flying off in a straight line.

3. State Kepler’s First Law of Planetary Motion and its geometric implications.

The Law of Orbits: Kepler’s First Law states that the orbit of a planet revolving around the Sun is an ellipse rather than a perfect circle.
Position of the Sun: In this elliptical trajectory, the Sun is not located at the center of the orbit but occupies one of the two fixed points known as the "foci."
Departure from Tradition: This discovery was revolutionary as it overturned centuries of belief in "perfect" circular celestial motion, which had complicated astronomical calculations.
Variable Distance: Because the orbit is elliptical, the distance between the planet and the Sun is not constant, changing every moment as the planet moves along its path.
Perihelion and Aphelion: This geometry creates a "perihelion" point (closest to the Sun) and an "aphelion" point (furthest from the Sun), which directly influences the planet's orbital dynamics.

4. Describe Kepler’s Second Law and explain how it affects a planet's speed in orbit.

The Law of Areas: Kepler’s Second Law states that an imaginary line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time.
Non-Uniform Speed: A major consequence of this law is that a planet does not move at a constant or uniform speed throughout its entire revolution around the Sun.
Acceleration at Perihelion: When the planet is closer to the Sun (at perihelion), it must travel faster along its orbital path to ensure the area swept remains equal to other time segments.
Deceleration at Aphelion: Conversely, when the planet is at its furthest point (aphelion), its linear speed decreases significantly because the longer distance from the Sun compensates for a shorter path.
Energy Balance: This behavior illustrates the constant exchange between gravitational potential energy and kinetic energy, ensuring the conservation of angular momentum in the system.

5. Formulate Kepler’s Third Law mathematically and explain its importance.

The Law of Periods: This law states that the square of the orbital period ($T$) of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of its mean distance ($r$) from the Sun.
$$T^2 \propto r^3 \text{ or } \frac{T^2}{r^3} = K$$
Predictive Power: This formula allowed early astronomers to calculate the distance of distant planets simply by observing how long they took to complete one full revolution.
Universal Consistency: It proves that all planets in the solar system are bound by the same mathematical logic, where outer planets naturally move much slower than inner ones.
Foundation for Newton: This specific proportionality was the critical piece of evidence Sir Isaac Newton used to mathematically derive his Inverse Square Law of gravitation.

6. State Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation and describe its two main components.

Definition of Attraction: The law states that every particle of matter in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that acts along the line joining their centers.
Mass Proportionality: The magnitude of this force is directly proportional to the product of the masses ($m_1 \times m_2$) of the two bodies involved in the interaction.
Distance Inversely Proportionality: The force is also inversely proportional to the square of the distance ($d^2$) between the centers of the two objects, known as the inverse square law.
$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{d^2}$$
Broad Applicability: This single equation explains terrestrial phenomena like falling objects and celestial movements like the orbits of planets, unifying earthly and space mechanics.

7. What is the physical significance and SI value of the Universal Gravitational Constant (G)?

Physical Definition: The constant $G$ represents the gravitational force of attraction between two bodies, each of mass 1 kg, placed at a distance of 1 meter apart.
Universal Status: It is called "universal" because its value remains identical regardless of where it is measured in the universe or what medium (water, air, vacuum) lies between the objects.
Numerical Value: In the International System (SI), the value of $G$ is $6.673 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2$, a value first determined experimentally by Henry Cavendish.
Weakness of Gravity: The extremely small magnitude of $G$ (to the power of -11) proves that gravity is the weakest of the four fundamental forces in nature.
Large-Scale Requirement: Because $G$ is so small, gravitational forces only become significant and observable when at least one of the masses involved is very large, like a planet.

8. Explain the "Inverse Square Law" and how it was derived by Newton.

Concept of Decay: The Inverse Square Law states that the strength of the gravitational force drops off rapidly as the distance between two objects increases ($F \propto 1/d^2$).
Mathematical Impact: If you double the distance between two objects, the gravitational pull doesn't just halve; it becomes four times weaker (one-fourth of the original).
Derivation Process: Newton derived this by analyzing circular motion ($F = mv^2/r$) and substituting the velocity of a planet ($v = 2\pi r / T$) into the equation.
Use of Kepler: He then applied Kepler’s Third Law ($T^2/r^3 = K$) to replace the period variable, which left distance ($r^2$) in the denominator of the force formula.
Proof of Distance: This mathematical synthesis provided the first rigorous proof that gravity's reach is determined by the square of the distance, explaining why distant planets feel less pull.

9. Why is the value of gravitational acceleration (g) higher at the poles than at the equator?

Oblate Spheroid Shape: The Earth is not a perfect sphere; its rotation causes it to bulge at the equator and flatten significantly at the North and South poles.
Radius Variation: As a result of this shape, the distance from the Earth's center to the surface at the poles is about 21 km less than the distance at the equator.
Inverse Relationship: The acceleration due to gravity ($g$) is calculated using $g = GM/R^2$, meaning $g$ increases as the radius ($R$) of the planet decreases.
Numerical Difference: Because the polar radius is smaller, the value of $g$ is highest at the poles (approx. 9.832 m/s²) and lowest at the equator (approx. 9.78 m/s²).
Effect on Weight: This means a person would technically weigh slightly more at the poles than at the equator, although their mass would remain exactly the same.

10. How does the value of g change as we move from the Earth's surface to higher altitudes?

Increase in Distance: As an object moves away from the Earth's surface to a height ($h$), the total distance from the Earth's center becomes ($R + h$).
Inverse Square Effect: Since $g$ is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the center, increasing the height weakens the gravitational field strength.
$$g_h = \frac{GM}{(R+h)^2}$$
Atmospheric Impact: For small heights (like a building), the change is negligible, but at the height of Mt. Everest or orbiting satellites, the reduction in $g$ becomes measurable.
Outer Space: As the height increases toward infinity, the value of $g$ approaches zero, which is why objects eventually escape the Earth's primary gravitational "well."

11. Why does the value of g decrease as we go deep inside the Earth's interior?

Decreasing Distance: As you move toward the Earth's center, the distance ($r$) from the center decreases, which would normally tend to increase the value of $g$.
Effective Mass Reduction: However, the amount of Earth's mass ($M$) that is actually "below" you and pulling you toward the center also decreases as you go deeper.
Mass-Distance Conflict: In this scenario, the decrease in the effective mass occurs much faster than the decrease in distance squared, leading to an overall reduction in pull.
Shell Theorem: Gravity from the portion of the Earth "above" you (the outer shell) pulls in all directions and cancels out, leaving only the inner core to attract you.
Linear Decrease: Because of this mass loss, the value of $g$ continues to decrease linearly as you move deeper, eventually reaching its minimum value at the very center.

12. What is the value of g at the center of the Earth and what is the scientific reason?

Zero Value: At the exact geometric center of the Earth, the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) becomes exactly zero.
Symmetry of Pull: This occurs because the mass of the Earth is distributed all around you. Every part of the Earth pulls you with equal force in opposite directions.
Vector Cancellation: Since gravitational forces are vectors, the pulls from the "top," "bottom," "left," and "right" sides of the Earth perfectly cancel each other out.
Zero Effective Mass: Mathematically, the mass ($M$) within a radius of zero is zero; thus, the formula $g = GM/R^2$ results in a net force of zero at the core.
Weightlessness: A person at the center would experience total weightlessness, though they would still be under immense pressure from the surrounding matter of the planet.

13. Define Mass and state its key properties in terms of inertia and location.

Quantity of Matter: Mass is defined as the total amount of matter or atoms present within an object, representing its physical "bulk."
Measure of Inertia: In physics, mass is the direct quantitative measure of an object's inertia, meaning heavier objects are harder to start or stop moving.
Scalar Quantity: Mass is a scalar quantity, possessing only magnitude and no direction, and it is measured in the SI unit of kilograms (kg).
Universal Constancy: The mass of an object is an intrinsic property that remains constant everywhere in the universe, regardless of local gravity.
Independent Property: Whether an object is on Earth, the Moon, or floating in deep space, its mass does not change unless matter is physically added or removed.

14. Define Weight and explain why it is considered a vector quantity.

Force of Attraction: Weight is defined as the gravitational force with which a planet or celestial body attracts an object toward its center.
Calculated Value: It is determined by the product of the object's mass and the local acceleration due to gravity ($W = mg$), measured in Newtons (N).
Vector Nature: Weight is a vector quantity because it has both a magnitude (how strong the pull is) and a specific direction (always pointing toward the planet's center).
Local Variability: Unlike mass, weight changes depending on where the object is located because the value of $g$ varies from planet to planet.
Zero Potential: Weight can become zero (weightlessness) in certain environments like the center of the Earth or in free-falling space stations, even if the mass remains high.

15. Contrast the mass and weight of an object on Earth versus its values on Mars.

Constant Mass: An object with a mass of 10 kg on Earth will still have a mass of exactly 10 kg on Mars because the number of atoms is unchanged.
Lower Mars Gravity: Mars is smaller and less massive than Earth, resulting in a surface gravity ($g$) of only about 3.7 m/s², compared to Earth's 9.8 m/s².
Weight Reduction: Consequently, the weight of the object on Mars would be $10 \times 3.7 = 37 \text{ N}$, whereas on Earth it was $10 \times 9.8 = 98 \text{ N}$.
Physical Sensation: A human on Mars would feel roughly three times lighter, allowing them to jump higher and lift heavier objects with less effort.
Fundamental Lesson: This comparison proves that while your "identity" (mass) is constant, your "burden" (weight) is defined by the planet you stand upon.

16. What is the definition of free fall and why can it only happen in a vacuum?

Gravity-Only Motion: Free fall is defined as the motion of an object where the only force acting upon it is the force of gravity.
Acceleration Rate: During free fall near Earth, all objects accelerate downward at the constant rate of $g$ (9.8 m/s²), regardless of their size or mass.
The Problem of Air: In a normal environment, air resistance and buoyancy act as upward forces that oppose gravity and slow down light objects.
Vacuum Requirement: True free fall can only occur in a vacuum because the absence of air molecules removes these opposing forces, leaving gravity as the sole influence.
Practical Example: While we say a stone is in free fall when dropped, it is only "near" free fall; a feather and a stone only fall together in a vacuum chamber.

17. Describe the results of the "Feather and Coin" experiment in a vacuum chamber.

Simultaneous Drop: In a famous physics demonstration, a heavy coin and a light, airy feather are placed inside a tall glass tube from which all air has been pumped out.
Identical Acceleration: When the tube is inverted, both the feather and the coin fall at exactly the same speed and hit the bottom at the same precise moment.
Mass Independence: This result proves that the acceleration due to gravity does not depend on the mass or shape of the object being dropped.
Confirmation of Theory: It confirms Galileo’s hypothesis that without air resistance, a light object falls just as fast as a heavy one because gravity's pull scales with mass.
Visual Proof: This experiment is the most striking visual evidence we have for the uniform nature of gravitational acceleration in a controlled environment.

18. Explain the significance of the negative sign in the gravitational potential energy formula.

Potential Formula: The formula for gravitational potential energy is $PE = -GMM / (R+h)$, where the negative sign is a critical mathematical convention.
Reference Point: In physics, potential energy is defined as being zero at an infinite distance from the Earth, where gravity no longer has any influence.
Binding Energy: Since gravity is an attractive force, you must "spend" energy to move an object away from Earth toward infinity; therefore, energy on the surface must be "less than zero."
Mathematical logic: As you move further away ($h$ increases), the value becomes a "smaller" negative number (closer to zero), which physically means the energy is increasing.
The Gravitational Well: The negative sign signifies that the object is "trapped" or bound within the Earth's gravitational field and requires an input of energy to get out.

19. Define escape velocity and state its numerical value and formula for Earth.

Threshold Speed: Escape velocity is the minimum initial speed an object must have at the surface of a planet to escape its gravitational pull forever without falling back.
$$v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} \text{ or } v_{esc} = \sqrt{2gR}$$
Earth's Value: For the planet Earth, the escape velocity is approximately 11.2 km/s (or about 40,320 km/h), which is why rockets require immense thrust.
Atmospheric Retention: This velocity also determines which gases a planet can keep; if a gas molecule moves faster than $v_{esc}$ due to heat, it escapes into space.

20. Explain why an astronaut feels weightless in an orbiting satellite.

Continuous Free Fall: An astronaut feels weightless not because there is no gravity (gravity is actually quite strong in orbit), but because they are in a state of constant free fall.
Orbital Balance: The satellite and the astronaut are both falling toward Earth at the same rate, but their high sideways speed causes them to "miss" the Earth and curve around it.
No Reaction Force: Since the spacecraft floor and the astronaut are falling together, the floor never pushes back against the astronaut's feet to create the sensation of weight.
Apparent Weightlessness: Weight is a sensation caused by the "normal force" of a surface resisting gravity; without that resistance, the brain perceives the body as floating.
Internal Environment: Inside the station, objects stay where they are placed because every single atom in the station is accelerating at the same $g$ value toward the Earth.

21. If the distance between two bodies is tripled, explain the change in gravitational force using the law.

Inverse Square Relationship: Newton’s law states that force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance ($F \propto 1/d^2$).
Mathematical Substitution: If the original distance is $d$, and the new distance becomes $3d$, we substitute this into the denominator as $(3d)^2$.
Resulting Factor: Squaring the three gives a factor of 9, meaning the new force becomes $1/9$ of the original gravitational force.
Rapid Decay: This illustrates how even a small increase in distance leads to a massive reduction in the strength of the gravitational bond between objects.
Planetary Implications: This explains why outer planets like Neptune feel a significantly weaker pull from the Sun than inner planets like Mercury or Earth.

22. Calculate g on a planet where an object takes 5s to fall from a height of 5m.

Equation Selection: To solve this, we use the kinematic equation for displacement in free fall: $s = ut + 0.5 gt^2$.
Initial Conditions: Since the object is "falling," we assume it starts from rest, meaning initial velocity $u = 0$, which simplifies the formula to $s = 0.5 gt^2$.
Substitution: Plugging in the given values: $5 \text{ m} = 0.5 \times g \times (5 \text{ s})^2$, which becomes $5 = 0.5 \times g \times 25$.
Calculation: Multiplying $0.5$ by $25$ gives $12.5$; therefore, the equation is $5 = 12.5g$. Solving for $g$ gives $5 / 12.5 = 0.4 \text{ m/s}^2$.
Conclusion: The acceleration due to gravity on this mystery planet is $0.4 \text{ m/s}^2$, which is much weaker than Earth's gravity, suggesting a small moon-like body.

23. If planet A's radius is half of planet B's and $g_B$ is half of $g_A$, find the mass relationship.

Gravity Equation: We use the base formula $g = GM/R^2$ for both planets to establish a ratio.
Substituting A's values: Since $R_A = R_B / 2$, we can write $g_A = G M_A / (R_B/2)^2$, which simplifies to $g_A = 4 G M_A / R_B^2$.
Substituting B's values: For planet B, the formula is simply $g_B = G M_B / R_B^2$.
Relating the two: We are told $g_B = g_A / 2$. Substituting our expressions: $G M_B / R_B^2 = (4 G M_A / R_B^2) / 2$.
Final Mass Ratio: This simplifies to $M_B = 2 M_A$, meaning planet B must have exactly twice the mass of planet A to satisfy the given gravity and radius conditions.

24. Find the mass and weight on the Moon for an object weighing 49 N (mass 5 kg) on Earth.

Constant Mass: The mass of the object is an intrinsic property, so it remains exactly 5 kg on the Moon, just as it was on Earth.
Gravity Comparison: The Moon's gravity is roughly $1/6$ of Earth's gravity ($g_m \approx 1.63 \text{ m/s}^2$).
Weight Calculation: To find the weight on the Moon, we use $W = m \times g_m$, which is $5 \text{ kg} \times 1.63 \text{ m/s}^2 = 8.15 \text{ N}$.
Simplified Method: Alternatively, one can simply take the Earth weight and divide by 6: $49 \text{ N} / 6 \approx 8.17 \text{ N}$ (allowing for rounding).
Observations: The object feels significantly lighter on the Moon, making it much easier to handle, even though the amount of matter inside it is unchanged.

25. A ball reaches a height of 500m. Calculate the initial velocity required. (g = 10 m/s²)

Kinematic Choice: We use the third equation of motion: $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ to find the initial velocity ($u$).
Endpoint Values: At the maximum height of 500m, the final velocity ($v$) is 0 m/s. The acceleration ($a$) is $-10 \text{ m/s}^2$ because it acts against the motion.
Equation Setup: $0 = u^2 + 2(-10)(500)$, which simplifies to $0 = u^2 - 10,000$.
Solving for u: Moving $10,000$ to the other side gives $u^2 = 10,000$. Taking the square root gives $u = 100 \text{ m/s}$.
Magnitude: An initial speed of 100 m/s (360 km/h) is required for an object to reach half a kilometer in height against Earth's gravity.

26. How long will the ball from the previous question take to return to Earth?

Time to Peak: Using $v = u + at$, we find the time taken to reach the top: $0 = 100 + (-10) \times t$, which gives $10t = 100$, so $t = 10 \text{ seconds}$.
Symmetry Principle: In a frictionless environment, the time taken to rise to a certain height is exactly equal to the time taken to fall back to the start.
Descent Time: Therefore, the ball will take another 10 seconds to fall from 500m back to the ground.
Total Flight Time: The total time of flight is the sum of the upward and downward journeys: $10 \text{ s} + 10 \text{ s} = 20 \text{ seconds}$.
Assumption: This calculation assumes there is no air resistance; in reality, air would slow the ball and change these times slightly.

27. A ball falls from a table in 1s. Find its impact speed and the height of the table. (g = 10 m/s²)

Impact Speed: Using $v = u + at$ with $u = 0$, the speed after 1 second is $v = 0 + (10 \times 1) = 10 \text{ m/s}$.
Height Calculation: To find the height ($s$), we use $s = ut + 0.5 gt^2$. Since $u = 0$, it simplifies to $s = 0.5 \times 10 \times (1)^2$.
Result: The height is $5 \text{ meters}$. This is a common benchmark: objects falling for one second on Earth travel exactly 5 meters.
Velocity Context: The impact speed of 10 m/s is equivalent to 36 km/h, illustrating how quickly gravity accelerates objects even over short distances.
Standard Values: These values are useful for mental estimations of heights when watching objects fall in daily life.

28. Calculate the gravitational force between Earth and Moon with the given parameters.

Law of Gravitation: We use $F = G M_1 M_2 / d^2$. The masses are $6 \times 10^{24} \text{ kg}$ and $7.4 \times 10^{22} \text{ kg}$.
Distance Conversion: The distance $3.84 \times 10^5 \text{ km}$ must be converted to meters: $3.84 \times 10^8 \text{ m}$.
$$F = \frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 6 \times 10^{24} \times 7.4 \times 10^{22}}{(3.84 \times 10^8)^2} \approx 2.01 \times 10^{20} \text{ N}$$
Significance: This incredibly large force is what keeps the Moon locked in its orbit and prevents it from wandering away into deep space.

29. What is the source of the tidal force in the sea and which body has more influence?

Lunar Pull: The primary cause of ocean tides is the gravitational attraction of the Moon pulling on the Earth's liquid oceans.
Differential Gravity: Because the Earth has a large diameter, the Moon's pull is stronger on the side facing it than on the Earth's center, causing the water to "bulge."
Solar Contribution: The Sun also exerts a gravitational pull on the oceans, but its influence is only about 46% as strong as the Moon's.
Distance Factor: Even though the Sun is 27 million times more massive than the Moon, it is 400 times further away, and tidal forces depend on the cube of distance ($1/d^3$).
Tidal Cycle: As the Earth rotates through these water bulges, different coastal areas experience high and low tides twice every 24 hours.

30. Why does an orbiting object not fall into the planet despite the constant downward pull?

Centripetal Balance: An orbiting satellite is indeed falling toward Earth, but it is also moving sideways at a very high tangential velocity.
Curvature of Earth: As the satellite falls a few meters toward the center, the Earth's curved surface "drops away" beneath it by the same amount.
Perpetual Fall: Because of this balance, the satellite never actually gets closer to the surface; it perpetually falls "around" the curve of the planet.
Speed Requirement: To maintain this orbit near Earth, an object must travel at about 8 km/s; if it slows down, gravity will win and it will crash.
Vacuum Advantage: Since there is no air in space to cause friction and slow the satellite down, it can maintain this high-speed "sideways fall" for years or decades.
