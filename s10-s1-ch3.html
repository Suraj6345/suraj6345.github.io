Chapter 3: Chemical Reactions & Equations

1. Define a chemical reaction and explain how it differs from a physical change.

Definition: A chemical reaction is a process in which some substances undergo bond breaking and are transformed into new substances by the formation of new bonds.
New Identity: Unlike physical changes, chemical reactions result in products that have entirely different chemical properties than the starting reactants.
Permanence: Chemical changes are generally permanent and irreversible by simple physical means, whereas physical changes (like melting ice) are often temporary.
Energy Exchange: Most chemical reactions involve a significant absorption or release of energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity.
Molecular Rearrangement: At the molecular level, atoms are rearranged to create new molecules, whereas in physical changes, the molecules themselves remain intact.

2. What is a chemical equation and what are the rules for writing it correctly?

Representation: A chemical equation is a shorthand representation of a chemical reaction using symbols and formulae of the substances involved.
Reactants and Products: Reactants are always written on the left-hand side, while products are written on the right-hand side, separated by an arrow ($\rightarrow$).
Physical States: The physical states are indicated in brackets: (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, (g) for gas, and (aq) for aqueous solutions.
Conditions: Specific conditions like temperature, pressure, or catalysts are mentioned above or below the reaction arrow to provide complete information.
Direction: The arrow points towards the products, indicating the direction of the reaction; if a gas is evolved, an upward arrow ($\uparrow$) is often used.

3. Why is it essential to balance a chemical equation according to the Law of Conservation of Mass?

Mass Invariance: The Law of Conservation of Mass states that mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
Atomic Count: Consequently, the total number of atoms of each element on the reactant side must be exactly equal to the number of atoms of that element on the product side.
Stoichiometry: Balancing ensures that the relative amounts of substances are correctly represented, which is vital for calculating theoretical yields in laboratory settings.
Mathematical Accuracy: A balanced equation acts as a mathematical identity that allows chemists to predict how much reactant is needed to produce a specific amount of product.
Validation: An unbalanced equation is considered scientifically "invalid" because it implies that atoms disappeared or appeared out of nowhere during the process.

4. Describe the "Combination Reaction" with a suitable chemical example.

Mechanism: A combination reaction occurs when two or more reactants (elements or compounds) combine to form a single, more complex product.
General Form: The reaction can be summarized by the general formula: $A + B \rightarrow AB$.
Example: When magnesium ribbon is burnt in air, it combines with oxygen to form magnesium oxide ($2Mg + O_2 \rightarrow 2MgO$).
Energy Profile: Most combination reactions are exothermic, meaning they release heat as new, more stable bonds are formed between the reactants.
Simplicity: These are among the most straightforward reactions in chemistry, often involving the direct synthesis of a compound from its constituent elements.

5. Explain the "Decomposition Reaction" and name its three main types.

Definition: A decomposition reaction is a process where a single reactant breaks down into two or more simpler products.
Energy Requirement: Unlike combination reactions, decomposition usually requires energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity to break existing chemical bonds.
Thermal Decomposition: This occurs when heat is used to break down a substance, such as calcium carbonate turning into lime and $CO_2$.
Electrolytic Decomposition: This uses an electric current to decompose a liquid or solution, such as the electrolysis of water into hydrogen and oxygen.
Photochemical Decomposition: This involves light energy, such as silver bromide breaking down into silver and bromine when exposed to sunlight.

6. What is a "Displacement Reaction" and how does the reactivity series play a role?

Definition: A displacement reaction is one in which a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in its compound.
General Pattern: It follows the pattern $A + BC \rightarrow AC + B$, where element A must be more "aggressive" or reactive than element B.
The Series: Chemists use the "Reactivity Series" (a list of metals in decreasing order of reactivity) to predict if a displacement will occur.
Example: If an iron nail is placed in a copper sulphate solution, iron displaces copper because iron is higher in the reactivity series.
Observation: These reactions are often accompanied by a distinct color change in the solution or the deposition of a metal on the surface of the reactant.

7. Explain "Double Displacement Reaction" and the concept of a precipitate.

Ion Exchange: In a double displacement reaction, the ions of two different ionic compounds exchange places to form two new compounds.
General Pattern: The reaction follows the structure: $AB + CD \rightarrow AD + CB$.
Precipitation: Often, one of the new products formed is insoluble in water and settles down as a solid, which is called a "precipitate."
Example: When silver nitrate reacts with sodium chloride, a white precipitate of silver chloride is formed ($AgNO_3 + NaCl \rightarrow AgCl\downarrow + NaNO_3$).
Ionic Nature: These reactions usually occur between substances in aqueous solution, allowing the ions to move freely and switch partners easily.

8. Define "Exothermic Reactions" and provide common examples found in daily life.

Heat Release: An exothermic reaction is a chemical process that releases energy in the form of heat or light to the surroundings.
Temperature Rise: During these reactions, the temperature of the container or the surrounding medium increases noticeably.
Respiration: A vital biological example is cellular respiration, where glucose reacts with oxygen to release energy for our body’s functions.
Combustion: All burning processes, such as the combustion of LPG in a kitchen stove or wood in a fireplace, are highly exothermic.
Neutralization: The reaction between an acid and a base is also exothermic, which is why the test tube often feels warm during such experiments.

9. Define "Endothermic Reactions" and explain why photosynthesis is a key example.

Energy Absorption: An endothermic reaction is a chemical process that absorbs heat or energy from the surroundings to proceed.
Temperature Drop: These reactions often cause the surroundings to cool down because energy is being pulled into the chemical bonds.
Photosynthesis: Plants perform an endothermic reaction by absorbing solar energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.
Cooking: Most cooking processes are endothermic because we must constantly supply heat to the food to cause the chemical changes required for it to be edible.
Decomposition Link: Almost all decomposition reactions (like heating limestone) are endothermic because breaking bonds requires an input of external energy.

10. What are the five factors that affect the rate of a chemical reaction?

Nature of Reactants: Some substances are naturally more reactive than others; for example, aluminum reacts faster with acid than iron does.
Size of Particles: Smaller particles (powdered form) react much faster than large chunks because they provide a much greater surface area for the reaction.
Concentration: Higher concentrations of reactants lead to more frequent molecular collisions, thereby increasing the overall speed of the reaction.
Temperature: Increasing the temperature gives molecules more kinetic energy, making them move faster and collide with more force, speeding up the reaction.
Catalyst: A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction without being consumed itself, usually by providing an easier chemical path.

11. Define a "Catalyst" and explain its unique role in chemical processes.

Acceleration: A catalyst is a substance that significantly increases the rate of a chemical reaction without undergoing any permanent chemical change itself.
Activation Energy: It works by lowering the "activation energy" required for the reactants to transform into products, making the process more efficient.
Recovery: At the end of the reaction, the catalyst can be recovered in its original mass and chemical composition, allowing it to be reused multiple times.
Specificity: Many catalysts are highly specific, meaning a particular catalyst will only speed up one specific type of chemical reaction.
Industrial Importance: Catalysts are crucial in industries (like making fertilizers or plastics) to produce materials quickly and at lower temperatures.

12. What is "Oxidation" and what are the three different ways to define it?

Oxygen Gain: The most basic definition of oxidation is the addition of oxygen to a substance during a chemical reaction.
Hydrogen Loss: Oxidation is also defined as the removal of hydrogen from a substance, such as when ethanol turns into acetaldehyde.
Electron Loss: In modern chemistry, oxidation is defined as the loss of one or more electrons by an atom, ion, or molecule.
Example: When magnesium burns ($2Mg + O_2 \rightarrow 2MgO$), the magnesium is oxidized because it gains oxygen and loses two electrons.
Valency Change: During oxidation, the oxidation state or the positive charge of the element involved typically increases.

13. What is "Reduction" and how does it relate to oxidation?

Oxygen Loss: Reduction is defined as the removal of oxygen from a substance, often used in metallurgy to extract metals from their ores.
Hydrogen Gain: It is also defined as the addition of hydrogen to a substance, such as the hydrogenation of vegetable oils.
Electron Gain: In terms of electrons, reduction is the process where an atom or ion gains one or more electrons.
Complementary Nature: Reduction never happens alone; it always occurs simultaneously with oxidation, as one substance must lose the electrons that another gains.
Valency Change: During reduction, the oxidation state or the positive charge of the element involved decreases (it is "reduced").

14. Define "Redox Reactions" and explain why the name is a combination.

Simultaneous Process: A redox reaction is a chemical process where oxidation and reduction occur at the exact same time.
Portmanteau Name: The name "Redox" is a combination of "Red" from reduction and "Ox" from oxidation, highlighting their inseparable nature.
Electron Transfer: Essentially, a redox reaction is a "game of catch" with electrons, where one reactant throws (loses) electrons and the other catches (gains) them.
Equation Example: In the reaction $CuO + H_2 \rightarrow Cu + H_2O$, copper oxide is reduced to copper, while hydrogen is oxidized to water.
Universal Presence: Most important chemical reactions, including those in batteries, human metabolism, and combustion, are types of redox reactions.

15. What is an "Oxidizing Agent" and a "Reducing Agent"?

Oxidizing Agent: A substance that brings about oxidation in another substance by providing oxygen or removing hydrogen/electrons is an oxidizing agent.
Self-Reduction: Interestingly, the oxidizing agent itself gets reduced during the reaction because it accepts the electrons lost by the other substance.
Reducing Agent: A substance that brings about reduction in another substance by removing oxygen or providing hydrogen/electrons is a reducing agent.
Self-Oxidation: The reducing agent itself gets oxidized because it is the one losing electrons to the other participant.
Common Examples: Oxygen and Chlorine are powerful oxidizing agents, while Carbon and Hydrogen are common reducing agents used in industries.

16. Define "Corrosion" and describe its harmful effects on metals.

Definition: Corrosion is the slow and steady destruction of a metal due to its reaction with moisture, oxygen, and other gases in the atmosphere.
Rusting: The most common form of corrosion is the rusting of iron, where a reddish-brown flaky substance (hydrated iron oxide) forms on the surface.
Structural Weakness: Corrosion eats away at the metal, significantly reducing its strength and making structures like bridges and buildings dangerous over time.
Economic Loss: Every year, billions of dollars are spent worldwide to replace corroded parts in machinery, ships, and infrastructure.
Prevention: It can be prevented through methods like painting, oiling, galvanizing (coating with zinc), or creating alloys like stainless steel.

17. What is "Rancidity" and how does it affect the quality of food?

Oxidation of Fats: Rancidity is the process where oils and fats in food items undergo oxidation when exposed to air for a long time.
Foul Smell and Taste: This chemical change produces volatile substances that give the food a highly unpleasant smell and a bitter or "off" taste.
Health Risk: Consuming rancid food can be harmful to health as the oxidized fats can cause digestive issues or other toxic effects.
Prevention via Antioxidants: Rancidity is prevented by adding "antioxidants" to food or by storing food in airtight containers.
Nitrogen Flushing: Food manufacturers often flush packets (like potato chips) with nitrogen gas to remove oxygen and prevent the snacks from turning rancid.

18. Explain the chemical reaction that occurs during the "Rusting of Iron."

Condition: Rusting requires the simultaneous presence of both moisture (water) and oxygen (air) to occur.
Electrochemical Process: Rusting is not a simple reaction but an electrochemical one that takes place at different spots on the iron surface.
Anodic Reaction: At one spot (anode), iron atoms lose electrons: $Fe \rightarrow Fe^{2+} + 2e^-$.
Cathodic Reaction: At another spot (cathode), oxygen is reduced in the presence of water to form hydroxyl ions.
Final Product: These ions react further to form hydrated iron(III) oxide ($Fe_2O_3 \cdot xH_2O$), which is the reddish-brown substance we call rust.

19. How do you identify a chemical reaction has taken place in a laboratory?

Evolution of Gas: If bubbles or effervescence appear when two substances are mixed, it often indicates the formation of a gaseous product.
Change in Color: A distinct change in the color of the solution (like copper sulphate turning from blue to green) is a strong sign of a chemical change.
Formation of Precipitate: The appearance of an insoluble solid settling at the bottom of a test tube indicates a double displacement or precipitation reaction.
Temperature Change: If the test tube becomes hot or cold to the touch, it indicates an exothermic or endothermic energy exchange.
Change in State: Sometimes, a reaction between two liquids results in a solid product, indicating a fundamental molecular change.

20. What is the difference between "Balanced" and "Unbalanced" equations?

Atomic Equality: A balanced equation has the same number of atoms for every element on both the reactant and product sides.
Skeletal Equation: An unbalanced equation, also called a skeletal equation, merely shows the chemical formulas without adjusting the quantities.
Scientific Validity: Only balanced equations are used for chemical calculations as they respect the Law of Conservation of Mass.
Coefficients: Balancing is achieved by adding "coefficients" (numbers in front of formulas), whereas changing "subscripts" is strictly forbidden.
Example: $H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$ is unbalanced; $2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O$ is the correct balanced form.

21. Explain the reaction between quicklime and water and its thermal nature.

The Reaction: When water is added to quicklime (calcium oxide), it reacts vigorously to form slaked lime (calcium hydroxide).
Chemical Equation: $CaO + H_2O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2 + \text{Heat}$.
Exothermic Nature: This is a highly exothermic reaction; the energy released is so intense that the water can actually reach boiling temperatures.
Classification: It is a classic example of a "Combination Reaction" because two simple reactants form one single product.
Observation: One can hear a hissing sound during the process, and steam is often seen rising from the mixture due to the released heat.

22. What happens when lead nitrate is heated? Explain the observation.

Decomposition: Heating lead nitrate crystals is a "Thermal Decomposition" reaction where a single substance breaks down due to heat.
The Products: It decomposes into lead monoxide, nitrogen dioxide, and oxygen gas ($2Pb(NO_3)_2 \rightarrow 2PbO + 4NO_2 + O_2$).
Observation (Gas): A characteristic brown-colored gas (nitrogen dioxide) is seen evolving from the test tube during the heating.
Observation (Solid): The white lead nitrate crystals turn into a yellow-colored solid residue of lead monoxide.
Safety: Since nitrogen dioxide is a toxic gas, this experiment must always be performed in a well-ventilated lab or under a fume hood.

23. Describe the electrolysis of water and the gases collected at each electrode.

Process: Electrolysis of water is the decomposition of water into its constituent elements using an electric current.
Electrolyte: A few drops of acid are usually added to pure water to make it a better conductor of electricity.
Cathode (Negative): Hydrogen gas is collected at the cathode; it burns with a "pop" sound when tested with a flame.
Anode (Positive): Oxygen gas is collected at the anode; it can be identified because it rekindles a glowing splinter.
Volume Ratio: The volume of hydrogen collected is exactly double the volume of oxygen, matching the chemical formula $H_2O$.

24. What is a "Precipitation Reaction" and why is it useful?

Definition: A precipitation reaction is any reaction where an insoluble solid (precipitate) is formed when two clear solutions are mixed.
Identification: It is a key tool in analytical chemistry used to identify the presence of specific ions in an unknown sample.
Water Treatment: These reactions are used in water purification to remove dissolved minerals and heavy metals by turning them into solids.
Medical Use: In medicine, precipitation tests are used to detect certain proteins or antibodies in blood or urine samples.
Symbol: In a chemical equation, the precipitate is denoted by a downward-pointing arrow ($\downarrow$) after its chemical formula.

25. Explain why copper cannot displace iron from iron sulphate solution.

Reactivity Rule: A displacement reaction can only occur if the standalone metal is more reactive than the metal already in the compound.
Position in Series: In the reactivity series, Iron ($Fe$) is placed above Copper ($Cu$), meaning Iron is the more reactive of the two.
The Logic: Because Copper is "weaker" (less reactive), it does not have the chemical "strength" to break the bond between Iron and Sulphate.
No Reaction: If you place copper wire in a green iron sulphate solution, no change occurs, and the solution remains green.
Reverse Case: However, if you place an iron nail in a blue copper sulphate solution, the iron will easily displace the copper.

26. Why do we apply paint on iron articles?

Barrier Protection: Paint acts as a physical barrier that prevents oxygen and moisture from coming into direct contact with the iron surface.
Prevention of Rust: By blocking these two essential components, the electrochemical reaction of rusting cannot take place.
Longevity: Painting significantly extends the lifespan of tools, vehicles, and industrial machinery by preventing structural decay.
Aesthetics: Besides protection, paint provides a decorative finish and can be used for color-coding pipes or gas cylinders.
Maintenance: Periodic repainting is necessary because any small crack or chip in the paint layer can allow corrosion to start and spread underneath.

27. What is the role of oil and fat in the "Rancidity" of food?

Oxidation Target: Oils and fats are chemically "unsaturated" or "sensitive" molecules that react easily with atmospheric oxygen.
Chemical Breakdown: During rancidity, the long chains of fatty acids break down into smaller, smelly molecules like aldehydes and ketones.
Energy Storage: Since fats are the primary source of calories in many snacks, their destruction through rancidity ruins the nutritional value.
Catalysts for Decay: Traces of metals or exposure to light can speed up the oxidation of these fats, making the food spoil even faster.
Packaging Strategy: This is why oily snacks are never sold in transparent or oxygen-permeable packaging.

28. Define "Neutralization Reaction" in the context of Chapter 3.

Definition: A neutralization reaction is a chemical reaction between an acid and a base to produce salt and water.
Mechanism: The $H^+$ ions from the acid combine with $OH^-$ ions from the base to form stable water molecules ($H_2O$).
Exothermic nature: This reaction releases energy, making it an exothermic process.
Example: When Hydrochloric acid reacts with Sodium hydroxide, common salt and water are formed ($HCl + NaOH \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O$).
Application: Neutralization is used in daily life to treat acidity in the stomach (using antacids) or to treat acidic soil in farming.

29. What is a "Skeletal Chemical Equation" and give an example.

Definition: A skeletal equation is an initial representation of a reaction that shows the chemical formulas but does not have equal atoms on both sides.
Purpose: It serves as the starting point for chemists to begin the balancing process.
Example: The skeletal equation for the formation of water is $H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$.
The Flaw: In this example, there are 2 oxygen atoms on the left but only 1 on the right, violating the law of mass conservation.
Transformation: A skeletal equation becomes a "Balanced Equation" once the appropriate coefficients are added (e.g., $2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O$).

30. Explain the significance of the "aq" symbol in chemical equations.

Meaning: The symbol (aq) stands for "aqueous," which means the substance is dissolved in water to form a solution.
Ionization: Many ionic compounds only react when they are in an (aq) state because water allows the ions to separate and move.
Reaction Medium: Water acts as a medium that facilitates collisions between reactant molecules, which might not react at all in their dry, solid states.
Concentration: When we see (aq), we often also consider the concentration of the solution, which affects how fast the reaction will go.
Practicality: Most reactions described in standard 10th-grade chemistry, like precipitation or displacement, take place in an aqueous environment.
