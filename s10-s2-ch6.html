Chapter 6: Animal Classification

Question 1

Why is the classification of animals necessary?

There are millions of animal species on Earth, making individual study impossible.
Classification helps in identifying animals with great accuracy and ease.
It helps in understanding the relationship between different animal groups.
It provides information about the evolution of animals from simple to complex.
It helps in the systematic study of the entire animal kingdom.
A single group study can give a general idea about all animals in that group.
Question 2

Who was the first to classify animals and what was his method?

The Greek philosopher Aristotle was the first to classify animals.
He used a "Artificial Method" of classification based on external criteria.
He classified animals based on their habitat (land, water, or air).
He also considered their body size and habits for classification.
Other scientists like Pliny and John Ray also followed similar artificial methods.
Linnaeus later introduced more refined artificial systems before the natural system.
Question 3

What is the "Natural Method" of animal classification?

This method is based on internal and objective biological criteria.
It considers the type of cells (prokaryotic or eukaryotic) and body structure.
It takes into account the chemical composition of the animal body.
Chromosomal similarities and differences are studied in this method.
It reflects the true evolutionary relationships between different organisms.
This method is widely accepted in modern biological sciences.
Question 4

Name the five kingdoms proposed by Robert Whittaker.

Kingdom Monera includes all unicellular prokaryotic organisms like bacteria.
Kingdom Protista consists of unicellular eukaryotic organisms like Amoeba.
Kingdom Fungi includes multicellular decomposers like mushrooms and molds.
Kingdom Plantae consists of multicellular autotrophic organisms (plants).
Kingdom Animalia includes all multicellular heterotrophic organisms (animals).
This system is the basis for modern hierarchical classification in biology.
Question 5

Distinguish between Non-Chordates and Chordates.

Non-chordates lack a supporting rod called the notochord in their body.
In chordates, the notochord is present at least during some stage of life.
Pharyngeal gill slits are absent in all non-chordates.
Chordates possess pharyngeal gill slits or lungs for respiration.
In non-chordates, the nerve cord is ventral, solid, and usually paired.
In chordates, the nerve cord is dorsal, hollow, and single.
Question 6

What are the traditional sub-kingdoms of Kingdom Animalia?

The kingdom is divided into Sub-kingdom Non-chordata and Sub-kingdom Chordata.
Non-chordata includes ten phyla from Porifera to Hemichordata.
Chordata includes animals that possess a notochord during development.
This division is based on the presence or absence of a supporting rod.
Non-chordates are considered more primitive in terms of evolution.
Chordates are considered more advanced with complex organ systems.
Question 7

List the ten phyla of Sub-kingdom Non-chordata.

Phylum Porifera consists of pore-bearing animals like sponges.
Phylum Coelenterata (Cnidaria) includes animals like jellyfish and hydra.
Phylum Platyhelminthes consists of flatworms like tapeworms.
Phylum Aschelminthes includes roundworms like Ascaris.
Phylum Annelida consists of segmented worms like earthworms.
Phylum Arthropoda, Mollusca, Echinodermata, Hemichordata, and Protozoa (traditional) follow.
Question 8

What is "Protoplasmic Grade" of organization?

This grade is found in unicellular organisms like Amoeba and Paramecium.
All life functions are performed within a single cell.
There is no division of labor between different cells.
Various organelles perform functions similar to organs in multicellular animals.
This is considered the simplest level of biological organization.
Organisms belonging to Kingdom Protista exhibit this grade of organization.
Question 9

Explain the "Cellular Grade" of organization.

This grade is observed in the most primitive multicellular animals.
Although many cells are present, they do not form tissues.
Division of labor exists between different types of specialized cells.
This organization is characteristic of animals in Phylum Porifera (Sponges).
Cells are loosely held together to form a functioning body unit.
It is an evolutionary step up from the protoplasmic grade of organization.
Question 10

What is "Cell-Tissue Grade" of organization?

In this grade, cells coordinate to form specific tissues.
These tissues perform various physiological functions like movement or digestion.
However, these tissues do not combine to form distinct organs.
This grade is found in animals belonging to Phylum Coelenterata (Cnidaria).
Nerve nets and muscle layers begin to appear at this stage.
It represents the beginning of complex coordination in the animal kingdom.
Question 11

Describe "Tissue-Organ Grade" of organization.

In this grade, different tissues combine to form specialized organs.
However, these organs do not work together as complete organ systems.
This level of organization is seen in Phylum Platyhelminthes (Flatworms).
For example, they have a distinct excretory system with flame cells.
This grade allows for more efficient performance of specific body functions.
It marks a transition towards the highly complex organ-system level.
Question 12

What is "Organ-System Grade" of organization?

This is the most complex level of organization in animals.
Different organs coordinate to form functional systems like digestive or circulatory.
Each system performs a major life process for the entire organism.
This grade is found in all higher phyla from Annelida to Chordata.
It allows for high levels of physical activity and metabolic efficiency.
Humans and all vertebrate animals exhibit this grade of organization.
Question 13

Explain the concept of Body Symmetry.

Body symmetry refers to the balance of body parts on opposite sides of an axis.
If an animal cannot be divided into two equal halves, it is Asymmetrical.
If it can be divided into equal halves through any plane passing through the center, it is Radial.
If it can be divided into equal halves through only one specific plane, it is Bilateral.
Symmetry is used as a major criterion for modern animal classification.
Most advanced animals exhibit bilateral symmetry for better movement.
Question 14

What are Asymmetrical animals?

These animals have no definite shape or central axis for division.
There is no plane that can divide the body into two identical halves.
This type of body plan is found in some species of sponges.
Amoeba is also considered asymmetrical at the unicellular level.
It is usually found in organisms that are sessile or have irregular growth.
Asymmetry is considered the most primitive condition of the body plan.
Question 15

Describe Radial Symmetry.

In this symmetry, the body can be divided into two equal halves by any plane.
The plane must pass through the central imaginary axis of the body.
This is commonly seen in circular or star-shaped animals.
Examples include Hydra, Jellyfish, and Starfish (adult stage).
It is advantageous for animals that receive stimuli from all directions.
Most radially symmetrical animals are aquatic and slow-moving or sessile.
Question 16

What is Bilateral Symmetry?

In this symmetry, there is only one imaginary plane of division.
This plane divides the body into identical left and right halves.
Most animals, including insects, fish, and humans, exhibit this symmetry.
It is associated with "cephalization" or the development of a distinct head.
It allows for streamlined movement and better directional sensing.
This is the most common type of symmetry in the animal kingdom.
Question 17

Explain Diploblastic animals.

These animals develop from only two embryonic germ layers.
The two layers are the outer Ectoderm and the inner Endoderm.
There is no middle Mesoderm layer present in these animals.
Instead, a non-cellular jelly-like layer called mesoglea may be present.
Animals in Phylum Coelenterata (Cnidaria) are diploblastic.
This is a simpler developmental plan compared to higher animals.
Question 18

What are Triploblastic animals?

These animals develop from three distinct embryonic germ layers.
The layers are the Ectoderm, Mesoderm (middle), and Endoderm.
The Mesoderm gives rise to muscles, bones, and the circulatory system.
All animals from Phylum Platyhelminthes to Chordata are triploblastic.
This allows for the development of complex organs and a body cavity.
Triploblasty is a requirement for the formation of a true coelom.
Question 19

Define "Coelom" (Body Cavity).

Coelom is the cavity between the body wall and the internal organs.
It is formed from the Mesoderm during embryonic development.
It provides space for the growth and movement of internal organs.
The presence or absence of a coelom is a key classification feature.
A true coelom is lined with mesodermal epithelium on both sides.
It acts as a shock absorber for the delicate internal organs.
Question 20

What are Acoelomate animals?

These animals do not possess a body cavity (coelom).
The space between the body wall and gut is filled with solid tissue.
They are usually small and have a flat or simple body structure.
Examples include animals from Phylum Porifera, Coelenterata, and Platyhelminthes.
Their internal organs are embedded directly in the surrounding tissue.
Acoelomates represent an early stage in the evolution of multicellular animals.
Question 21

Describe Pseudocoelomate animals.

In these animals, a body cavity is present but not lined by mesoderm.
The cavity is formed from the blastocoel of the embryo, not the mesoderm.
The mesoderm appears as scattered pouches between the ectoderm and endoderm.
This type of cavity is known as a "false coelom" or pseudocoelom.
This characteristic is unique to Phylum Aschelminthes (Roundworms).
It provides a hydrostatic skeleton for the animal to maintain its shape.
Question 22

What are Eucoelomate (True Coelomate) animals?

These animals possess a true body cavity lined by mesoderm.
The coelom is formed either by splitting the mesoderm or from gut pouches.
All animals from Phylum Annelida to Chordata are true coelomates.
It allows for greater complexity in the digestive and circulatory systems.
Most large and active animals belong to this category.
It is divided into schizocoelom and enterocoelom based on formation.
Question 23

What is Body Segmentation?

Segmentation is the division of the body into a series of similar units.
If the body is divided into small, linear, similar segments, it is called Metamerism.
Each segment is called a "metamere" or "somite."
This is clearly visible in animals like Earthworms (Phylum Annelida).
It allows for better movement and specialization of different body regions.
Internal organs may also be repeated in each segment in some phyla.
Question 24

List the main characteristics of Phylum Porifera.

These are the simplest multicellular animals, commonly called sponges.
Their body is asymmetrical and has a cellular grade of organization.
They have numerous pores called 'Ostia' and a large opening called 'Osculum'.
They possess special flagellated cells called 'Collar cells' or 'Choanocytes'.
They are mostly marine and always attached to a substrate (sessile).
Their body is supported by spicules or spongin fibers.
Question 25

Explain the "Canal System" in Sponges.

Water enters the sponge body through numerous small pores called Ostia.
The water circulates through a system of canals into a central cavity.
The central cavity is called the Spongocoel.
Collar cells create a current that pushes water out through the Osculum.
This water flow brings in food (plankton) and oxygen for the sponge.
It also carries away metabolic wastes and reproductive cells.
Question 26

Give examples of Phylum Porifera.

Sycon is a common marine sponge with a simple tube-like structure.
Euspongia is also known as the "Bath Sponge."
Hyalonema is popularly called the "Glass rope sponge."
Euplectella is famous as "Venus's flower basket."
Spongilla is a rare example of a freshwater sponge.
Chalina is often called the "Finger sponge" due to its shape.
Question 27

List the main characteristics of Phylum Coelenterata (Cnidaria).

Their body is radially symmetrical and diploblastic.
They exhibit two body forms: the cylindrical 'Polyp' and umbrella-like 'Medusa'.
They have a central gastrovascular cavity with a single opening (mouth).
They possess specialized stinging cells called 'Cnidoblasts' or 'Nematocysts'.
Most are marine, but some like Hydra live in freshwater.
They exhibit a cell-tissue grade of organization.
Question 28

What is the function of Cnidoblasts?

Cnidoblasts are specialized cells used for both defense and offense.
They contain a coiled, poisonous thread-like structure called a nematocyst.
When touched, the thread is discharged to inject toxin into the prey.
It helps in paralyzing the prey for easy consumption.
They are also used to anchor the animal to a substrate.
These cells are mostly concentrated on the tentacles around the mouth.
Question 29

Distinguish between Polyp and Medusa.

Polyp is a cylindrical body form that is usually sessile (attached).
Medusa is a bowl or umbrella-shaped body form that is free-swimming.
Hydra and Sea Anemone are examples of the Polyp form.
Jellyfish (Aurelia) is a classic example of the Medusa form.
Polyps usually reproduce asexually, while Medusae reproduce sexually.
Some cnidarians alternate between these two forms in their life cycle.
Question 30

Give examples of Phylum Coelenterata.

Hydra is a freshwater polyp capable of somersaulting movement.
Aurelia is the scientific name for the common Jellyfish.
Physalia is known as the "Portuguese Man-of-War."
Adamsia is the common "Sea Anemone" found on hermit crab shells.
Pennatula is known as the "Sea Pen" due to its appearance.
Meandrina is a type of coral known as "Brain Coral."
Question 31

List the main characteristics of Phylum Platyhelminthes.

These are commonly called 'Flatworms' because their body is dorso-ventrally flattened.
They are acoelomate and triploblastic animals.
Most are endoparasites living inside other animals, but some are free-living.
They show bilateral symmetry and a tissue-organ grade of organization.
They are hermaphrodites, meaning both male and female systems are in one body.
They have specialized 'Flame cells' for excretion and osmoregulation.
Question 32

What are the adaptations of parasitic Flatworms?

They have hooks and suckers to attach themselves to the host's body.
Their body is covered by a thick cuticle to protect against host enzymes.
They often lack a digestive system as they absorb pre-digested food.
Their reproductive system is highly developed to ensure survival.
They have a high anaerobic respiratory capacity to live in low-oxygen environments.
Sense organs are usually reduced as they live in a stable internal environment.
Question 33

Give examples of Phylum Platyhelminthes.

Planaria is a free-living flatworm known for its high regeneration power.
Fasciola hepatica is commonly known as the "Liver Fluke."
Taenia solium is the "Pork Tapeworm" found in the human intestine.
Schistosoma is known as the "Blood Fluke."
Echinococcus is the "Dog Tapeworm."
Dugesia is another common name for free-living planarians.
Question 34

List the main characteristics of Phylum Aschelminthes.

These are commonly called 'Roundworms' or 'Threadworms'.
Their body is long, cylindrical, and unsegmented with tapering ends.
They are triploblastic and pseudocoelomate animals.
Their body is covered by a tough, resistant, non-living cuticle.
They are usually unisexual (dioecious) with distinct male and female.
Most are parasitic and cause diseases in plants and animals.
Question 35

How can you distinguish between male and female Ascaris?

The female Ascaris is generally longer and thicker than the male.
The posterior end of the female is straight.
The posterior end of the male is curved or hooked ventrally.
Males possess a pair of penial setae for reproduction at the tail end.
Females have a separate genital pore (vulva) on the ventral side.
In males, the digestive and reproductive tracts open into a common cloaca.
Question 36

Give examples of Phylum Aschelminthes.

Ascaris lumbricoides is the common "Intestinal Roundworm."
Wuchereria bancrofti is the "Filarial worm" causing Elephantiasis.
Ancylostoma duodenale is known as the "Hookworm."
Enterobius vermicularis is the "Pinworm" or "Seatworm."
Loa loa is the "Eye worm" found in Africa.
Rhabditis is a free-living roundworm found in soil.
Question 37

List the main characteristics of Phylum Annelida.

These are called 'Segmented worms' because the body shows metameric segmentation.
They are triploblastic and true coelomate (eucoelomate) animals.
They show bilateral symmetry and organ-system grade organization.
They have specialized organs for locomotion like setae, parapodia, or suckers.
Their circulatory system is of the 'Closed' type.
They may be hermaphrodites (Earthworm) or unisexual (Nereis).
Question 38

What is the function of Nephridia in Annelids?

Nephridia are specialized coiled tubular structures for excretion.
They help in removing nitrogenous wastes from the coelomic fluid and blood.
They also play a crucial role in osmoregulation (maintaining water balance).
They open to the outside through small pores called nephridiopores.
They are usually arranged segmentally throughout the body.
This represents a more advanced excretory system than flame cells.
Question 39

Give examples of Phylum Annelida.

Pheretima posthuma is the common "Earthworm."
Hirudinaria granulosa is the "Cattle Leech."
Nereis is a marine annelid often called the "Clam worm."
Aphrodite is popularly known as the "Sea Mouse."
Tubifex is a small red worm found in polluted freshwater.
Arenicola is commonly known as the "Lugworm."
Question 40

List the main characteristics of Phylum Arthropoda.

This is the largest phylum in the animal kingdom.
They have jointed appendages (Arthros = joint, Podos = leg).
Their body is covered by a tough chitinous exoskeleton.
They show bilateral symmetry, triploblastic development, and true coelom.
Their circulatory system is of the 'Open' type with a hemocoel.
They respire through gills, book gills, book lungs, or tracheal systems.
Question 41

Why is Arthropoda the most successful phylum?

The chitinous exoskeleton provides excellent protection and prevents water loss.
Jointed appendages allow for high mobility and specialized functions.
They have occupied almost every possible habitat on Earth (land, air, water).
Many have wings, allowing them to fly and colonize new areas.
They have highly developed sense organs like compound eyes and antennae.
Their small size and high reproductive rate ensure population survival.
Question 42

Give examples of Phylum Arthropoda.

Periplaneta americana is the common "Cockroach."
Palaemon is the scientific name for "Prawn."
Apis indica is the "Honey bee."
Scolopendra is the common "Centipede."
Limulus is the "King Crab," often called a "living fossil."
Butterfly, Mosquito, Scorpion, and Spider are other common examples.
Question 43

List the main characteristics of Phylum Mollusca.

This is the second-largest phylum of animals.
Their body is soft and unsegmented (Mollis = soft).
The body is divided into three parts: Head, Visceral mass, and Foot.
The visceral mass is covered by a fleshy fold called the 'Mantle'.
The mantle usually secretes a hard calcareous shell for protection.
They possess a file-like rasping organ in the mouth called 'Radula'.
Question 44

What is the function of the 'Foot' in Molluscs?

The foot is a muscular organ used primarily for locomotion.
In snails, it helps in crawling on surfaces using mucus.
In bivalves (clams), it is wedge-shaped and used for burrowing in sand.
In cephalopods (Octopus), the foot is modified into tentacles around the head.
It can also be used for capturing prey or anchoring to rocks.
The shape and function of the foot vary greatly across different classes.
Question 45

Give examples of Phylum Mollusca.

Pila globosa is the common "Apple Snail."
Octopus is known as the "Devil fish" and has eight arms.
Unio is a freshwater "Mussel."
Sepia is commonly known as "Cuttlefish."
Loligo is the "Squid."
Aplysia is known as the "Sea Hare."
Question 46

List the main characteristics of Phylum Echinodermata.

[Image of a starfish water vascular system]
These are 'Spiny-skinned' animals (Echinos = spiny, Derma = skin).
Their skeleton is made of calcareous ossicles or spines.
Adults show radial symmetry, but larvae show bilateral symmetry.
They possess a unique 'Water Vascular System' for locomotion and feeding.
They are exclusively marine animals found on the sea floor.
They have a high power of regeneration of lost body parts.
Question 47

Explain "Water Vascular System" in Echinoderms.

It is a system of canals filled with seawater.
Water enters through a perforated plate called the 'Madreporite'.
It flows through various canals to reach the 'Tube feet'.
Tube feet are used for walking, capturing food, and respiration.
The hydraulic pressure in the system allows the tube feet to extend or retract.
This system compensates for the lack of a complex circulatory system.
Question 48

Give examples of Phylum Echinodermata.

Asterias is the common "Starfish" or "Sea Star."
Echinus is known as the "Sea Urchin."
Antedon is popularly called the "Sea Lily."
Cucumaria is the "Sea Cucumber."
Ophiothrix is the "Brittle Star."
Holothuria is another genus of Sea Cucumber.
Question 49

List the main characteristics of Phylum Hemichordata.

These are small, marine, worm-like animals.
The body is divided into three parts: Proboscis, Collar, and Trunk.
They were previously considered chordates because of the 'Stomochord'.
The Stomochord is a rod-like structure present in the proboscis region.
They have pharyngeal gill slits for respiration.
They are considered a 'Connecting Link' between non-chordates and chordates.
Question 50

Why is Balanoglossus called a connecting link?

It shows non-chordate characters like a ventral nerve cord.
It shows chordate characters like pharyngeal gill slits.
The stomochord was initially mistaken for a notochord.
Its larval stage (Tornaria) resembles the larvae of echinoderms.
It helps scientists understand how vertebrates evolved from invertebrates.
It represents a transition in body plan and physiological systems.
Question 51

List the main characteristics of Phylum Chordata.

They possess a supporting rod called the Notochord at some stage.
They have a dorsal, hollow, single nerve cord.
Pharyngeal gill slits are present for respiration (at least in embryos).
A post-anal tail is usually present.
The heart is located on the ventral side of the body.
They have a closed circulatory system.
Question 52

Name the three sub-phyla of Phylum Chordata.

Sub-phylum Urochordata (Tunicata) - Notochord only in the larval tail.
Sub-phylum Cephalochordata - Notochord extends from head to tail and persists.
Sub-phylum Vertebrata (Craniata) - Notochord is replaced by a vertebral column.
Urochordates and Cephalochordates are collectively called "Protochordates."
Vertebrates are the most advanced and diverse group of chordates.
This division is based on the fate and extent of the notochord.
Question 53

Characteristics of Sub-phylum Urochordata.

These are marine animals often called "Sea squirts."
Their body is covered by a tunic or test made of tunicin (cellulose-like).
The notochord is present only in the tail of the free-swimming larva.
The adult is usually sessile and lacks a notochord and nerve cord.
They show "Retrogressive Metamorphosis" where the larva is more advanced than the adult.
Examples include Herdmania, Doliolum, and Salpa.
Question 54

Characteristics of Sub-phylum Cephalochordata.

These are small, fish-like marine animals.
The notochord extends through the entire length of the body.
The notochord persists throughout their entire life.
They have a well-developed pharynx with numerous gill slits.
Muscle segments called 'Myotomes' are present along the body.
Example: Amphioxus (Branchiostoma).
Question 55

Characteristics of Sub-phylum Vertebrata.

The embryonic notochord is replaced by a cartilaginous or bony vertebral column.
The head is well-developed with a cranium (skull) protecting the brain.
They have a complex brain and highly developed sense organs.
They have a ventral muscular heart with 2, 3, or 4 chambers.
Kidneys are present for excretion and osmoregulation.
They are divided into six classes from Cyclostomata to Mammalia.
Question 56

List the six classes of Sub-phylum Vertebrata.

Class Cyclostomata includes jawless vertebrates like lampreys.
Class Pisces consists of all types of fishes.
Class Amphibia includes animals like frogs that live on land and in water.
Class Reptilia consists of crawling animals like snakes and lizards.
Class Aves includes all birds.
Class Mammalia consists of animals that suckle their young ones.
Question 57

Characteristics of Class Cyclostomata.

They are jawless vertebrates (Agnatha) with a suctorial mouth.
Their body is elongated and eel-like, lacking paired fins.
Their skin is smooth, slimy, and devoid of scales.
Their cranium and vertebral column are cartilaginous.
They are mostly ectoparasites on other fishes.
Examples: Petromyzon (Lamprey) and Myxine (Hagfish).
Question 58

Characteristics of Class Pisces (Fishes).

They are exclusively aquatic, cold-blooded (poikilothermic) animals.
Their body is spindle-shaped (streamlined) to minimize water resistance.
They have paired and unpaired fins for swimming and balance.
Respiration occurs through gills.
Their skin is covered with scales (placoid, cycloid, etc.).
The heart is two-chambered (one auricle and one ventricle).
Question 59

Distinguish between Chondrichthyes and Osteichthyes.

Chondrichthyes have a cartilaginous skeleton (e.g., Sharks).
Osteichthyes have a bony skeleton (e.g., Rohu, Pomfret).
Sharks have placoid scales; bony fishes have cycloid or ctenoid scales.
In sharks, the mouth is ventral; in bony fishes, it is usually terminal.
Sharks lack an operculum (gill cover); bony fishes possess one.
Sharks lack an air bladder; bony fishes have one for buoyancy.
Question 60

Give examples of Class Pisces.

Scoliodon is the common "Dogfish" (Shark).
Labeo rohita (Rohu) is a popular freshwater edible fish.
Exocoetus is known as the "Flying fish."
Hippocampus is the "Sea horse."
Anabas is the "Climbing perch."
Catla and Mrigal are other common Indian freshwater fishes.
Question 61

Characteristics of Class Amphibia.

They can live both on land and in water (dual life).
They require water for external fertilization and larval development.
Their skin is moist, glandular, and lacks scales.
They are cold-blooded animals.
The heart is three-chambered (two auricles and one ventricle).
Respiration occurs through gills (in larvae), lungs, or skin.
Question 62

Why do amphibians need moist skin?

Amphibians perform "Cutaneous Respiration" (breathing through skin).
The skin must remain moist for oxygen to dissolve and diffuse into the blood.
Mucus glands in the skin constantly secrete moisture.
This is especially important when the animal is underwater or hibernating.
Moist skin also helps in regulating body temperature.
If the skin dries out, the animal may suffocate and die.
Question 63

Give examples of Class Amphibia.

Rana tigrina is the common "Indian Bullfrog."
Bufo is the scientific name for "Toad."
Hyla is known as the "Tree frog."
Salamandra (Salamander) is a tailed amphibian.
Ichthyophis is a "Caecilian" (limbless amphibian).
Necturus is commonly called the "Mudpuppy."
Question 64

Characteristics of Class Reptilia.

These are the first true terrestrial vertebrates.
They show a creeping or crawling mode of locomotion.
Their skin is dry, cornified, and covered with scales or scutes.
They are cold-blooded animals.
The heart is three-chambered (but 4-chambered in crocodiles).
They lay "Cleidoic eggs" with a hard shell, so they don't need water for breeding.
Question 65

Why are crocodiles unique among reptiles?

Most reptiles have a three-chambered heart with an incomplete septum.
Crocodiles possess a completely four-chambered heart.
This makes their circulatory system more efficient.
It is an evolutionary step towards the warm-blooded nature of birds and mammals.
They also have a secondary palate to breathe while eating.
They show more advanced parental care than most other reptiles.
Question 66

Give examples of Class Reptilia.

Naja naja is the "Indian Cobra."
Hemidactylus is the common "Wall Lizard."
Chelone is the "Turtle" (marine).
Testudo is the "Tortoise" (terrestrial).
Chameleon is the "Tree lizard" famous for changing color.
Crocodilus (Crocodile) and Gavialis (Gharial) are large aquatic reptiles.
Question 67

Characteristics of Class Aves (Birds).

They are warm-blooded (homeothermic) animals.
Their forelimbs are modified into wings for flight.
Their body is covered with feathers.
Their bones are hollow (pneumatic) to reduce body weight.
Jaws are modified into a toothless beak.
The heart is four-chambered.
Question 68

What are the flight adaptations in birds?

The spindle-shaped body offers minimum air resistance.
Hollow (pneumatic) bones make the skeleton light but strong.
Large, powerful breast muscles (pectoral muscles) move the wings.
Air sacs connected to lungs provide extra oxygen and buoyancy.
The absence of a urinary bladder and teeth reduces weight.
Feathers provide insulation and surface area for flight.
Question 69

Give examples of Class Aves.

Columba livia is the "Rock Pigeon."
Pavo cristatus is the "Indian Peafowl" (National Bird).
Struthio camelus is the "Ostrich" (flightless bird).
Aptenodytes is the "Penguin."
Psittacula is the "Parrot."
Corvus is the "Crow."
Question 70

Characteristics of Class Mammalia.

Presence of mammary glands (milk-producing) is a unique feature.
They are warm-blooded animals.
Their body is covered with hair.
They have external ears called 'Pinnae'.
They possess a muscular diaphragm separating the thorax and abdomen.
Most are viviparous (give birth to live young ones).
Question 71

What are the types of mammals based on reproduction?

Monotremes (Prototheria) are egg-laying mammals like Platypus.
Marsupials (Metatheria) have a pouch to carry underdeveloped young (e.g., Kangaroo).
Eutherians are placental mammals where the fetus develops fully inside the uterus.
Humans, elephants, and whales belong to the placental group.
