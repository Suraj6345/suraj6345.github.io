Chapter 08: Metallurgy

Comprehensive Study Guide | SSC Science 1

Question 01

Define Metallurgy and its basic purpose.

Metallurgy is the science and technology used for the extraction of metals from their ores.
It involves various physical and chemical processes to purify metals for industrial use.
The primary goal is to obtain metals in their pure form from naturally occurring minerals.
It includes the study of properties of metals and the production of alloys.
Natural resources found in the earth's crust serve as the raw material for metallurgy.
The process must be economically viable and environmentally sustainable for large-scale production.
Question 02

State the physical properties of metals.

Metals generally exist in a solid state at room temperature, except for mercury and gallium.
They possess a characteristic shine known as metallic luster on their fresh surfaces.
Most metals are hard, although alkali metals like sodium can be cut with a knife.
They exhibit malleability, meaning they can be beaten into very thin sheets.
They are ductile, allowing them to be drawn into long, thin wires.
Metals are excellent conductors of both heat and electricity due to free electrons.
Question 03

State the physical properties of non-metals.

Non-metals can exist in solid, liquid, or gaseous states at room temperature.
They generally lack luster and appear dull, with the exception of iodine crystals.
They are usually brittle in their solid form and break into pieces when hit.
They are poor conductors of heat and electricity, except for graphite (carbon).
Non-metals have relatively low melting and boiling points compared to metals.
They do not possess the properties of malleability or ductility seen in metals.
Question 04

Describe the reaction of metals with oxygen.

Most metals react with oxygen when heated to form their respective metal oxides.
Sodium and potassium react vigorously even at room temperature and are stored in kerosene.
Magnesium burns in air with a dazzling white flame to form magnesium oxide.
Some metal oxides, like those of aluminum and zinc, are amphoteric in nature.
Amphoteric oxides can react with both acids and bases to form salt and water.
The reactivity of a metal determines the speed and intensity of its oxidation.
Question 05

Explain the reaction of metals with water.

Sodium and potassium react violently with cold water, releasing hydrogen gas and heat.
Calcium reacts less vigorously, and the hydrogen bubbles formed make the metal float.
Magnesium does not react with cold water but reacts with hot water to form hydroxide.
Aluminum, iron, and zinc react only with steam to form metal oxides and hydrogen.
Metals like gold, silver, and copper do not react with water at any temperature.
The reaction with water is a key indicator of a metal's position in the reactivity series.
Question 06

Explain the reaction of metals with dilute acids.

Most metals react with dilute hydrochloric or sulfuric acid to produce a salt and hydrogen gas.
The rate of evolution of hydrogen gas depends on the reactivity of the specific metal.
Magnesium reacts the fastest, followed by aluminum, zinc, and then iron.
Copper does not react with dilute hydrochloric acid as it is below hydrogen in reactivity.
With nitric acid, hydrogen gas is usually not evolved because it is a strong oxidizing agent.
Instead, nitric acid produces water and various oxides of nitrogen during the reaction.
Question 07

Define Aqua Regia.

Aqua Regia is a highly corrosive and fuming liquid mixture of concentrated acids.
It is prepared by mixing concentrated hydrochloric acid and concentrated nitric acid.
The acids are mixed specifically in a volume ratio of 3:1 respectively.
It is one of the few reagents capable of dissolving noble metals like gold and platinum.
The name "Aqua Regia" is Latin for "Royal Water," reflecting its power over noble metals.
It must be prepared fresh as it decomposes quickly and loses its potency.
Question 08

What is the Reactivity Series of metals?

It is an arrangement of metals in the decreasing order of their chemical reactivity.
Highly reactive metals like potassium and sodium are placed at the very top.
Least reactive metals like gold and platinum are placed at the very bottom.
Hydrogen is included in the series as a reference point for comparing reactivity.
Metals above hydrogen can displace it from dilute acids, while those below cannot.
This series helps predict the outcome of displacement reactions between metals and salts.
Question 09

Explain Displacement Reactions in metals.

A displacement reaction occurs when a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal.
The more reactive metal takes the place of the less reactive metal in its salt solution.
For example, iron can displace copper from a blue copper sulfate solution.
This results in the formation of a green iron sulfate solution and copper deposits.
If a less reactive metal is added to a salt of a more reactive metal, no reaction occurs.
These reactions are used to determine the relative positions of metals in the reactivity series.
Question 10

Define Minerals and Ores.

Minerals are naturally occurring chemical compounds of metals found in the earth's crust.
They are often associated with impurities like soil, sand, and rocky matter.
Ores are specific minerals from which a metal can be extracted profitably and easily.
All ores are minerals, but all minerals are not necessarily ores.
Bauxite is the primary ore used for the extraction of aluminum.
The economic value of an ore depends on the percentage of the metal it contains.
Question 11

Define Gangue.

Gangue refers to the unwanted rocky, earthy, or sandy impurities present in an ore.
It includes materials like silica, clay, and various silicates found in the earth.
The removal of gangue is the first major step in the concentration of ores.
The physical and chemical properties of gangue determine the method of its removal.
Effective removal of gangue increases the percentage of metal in the raw material.
In some cases, chemicals called fluxes are added to react with and remove gangue.
Question 12

Describe the Hydraulic Separation Method.

This is a physical method of ore concentration based on the difference in specific gravities.
It is typically used for heavy ores like tin and lead to separate them from lighter gangue.
The powdered ore is treated with a fast-flowing upward stream of water.
The lighter gangue particles are washed away by the force of the water stream.
The heavier ore particles settle down at the bottom of the separation tank.
This method is also known as levigation or gravity separation.
Question 13

Describe the Magnetic Separation Method.

[Image of magnetic separation method diagram]
This method is used when either the ore or the gangue possesses magnetic properties.
The machine consists of two rollers, one of which is magnetic, and a conveyor belt.
Powdered ore is dropped onto the moving belt from one end.
Non-magnetic particles fall straight down into a separate heap away from the roller.
Magnetic particles cling to the belt longer and fall into a separate collector near the roller.
It is commonly used for concentrating ores of iron, such as magnetite.
Question 14

Describe the Froth Floatation Method.

This method is primarily used for the concentration of sulfide ores.
Powdered ore is mixed with water and certain oils like pine oil in a large tank.
Compressed air is blown through the mixture to create a thick froth.
Sulfide ore particles are preferentially wetted by the oil and rise to the surface with the froth.
The gangue particles are wetted by water and settle down at the bottom of the tank.
The froth is then skimmed off and dried to obtain the concentrated ore.
Question 15

Explain the process of Leaching.

Leaching is a chemical method used for the concentration of certain specific ores.
The ore is soaked in a suitable chemical solution for a long period of time.
The metal in the ore reacts with the chemical and dissolves into the solution.
The insoluble impurities (gangue) remain behind and can be filtered out.
For example, bauxite is leached using a hot solution of sodium hydroxide.
This process is essential when the impurities have similar physical properties to the ore.
Question 16

Describe the extraction of Aluminum (Bayer's Process).

Bauxite ore is first ground and then heated with concentrated sodium hydroxide under pressure.
The aluminum oxide dissolves to form water-soluble sodium aluminate.
Iron oxide impurities do not dissolve and are removed by filtration.
The solution is then diluted and cooled to precipitate aluminum hydroxide.
This precipitate is filtered, washed, and then ignited at 1000°C.
The final product is pure alumina ($Al_2O_3$) ready for electrolysis.
Question 17

Explain the Electrolytic Reduction of Alumina.

This process is carried out in a steel tank with a lining of graphite (carbon).
The graphite lining acts as the cathode, while graphite rods act as the anode.
Alumina is dissolved in a mixture of molten cryolite and fluorspar to lower its melting point.
When electricity is passed, aluminum ions are reduced at the cathode to form liquid metal.
Oxygen gas is evolved at the anode and reacts with the carbon rods to form $CO_2$.
Because the anodes get oxidized and consumed, they must be replaced periodically.
Question 18

How are highly reactive metals extracted?

Metals like sodium, calcium, and magnesium are at the top of the reactivity series.
They cannot be reduced using carbon as they have a higher affinity for oxygen.
They are extracted by the electrolysis of their molten chloride salts.
The metal is deposited at the cathode (negative electrode) during the process.
Chlorine gas is evolved at the anode (positive electrode) as a byproduct.
This method ensures the production of metals with very high purity.
Question 19

How are moderately reactive metals extracted?

Metals like zinc, iron, and lead are usually found as sulfides or carbonates.
These ores are first converted into metal oxides before the reduction process.
Sulfide ores are heated strongly in the presence of air, a process called roasting.
Carbonate ores are heated strongly in limited air, a process called calcination.
The resulting metal oxides are then reduced to metal using carbon (coke).
Carbon acts as a reducing agent by removing oxygen from the metal oxide.
Question 20

Explain the Thermit Reaction.

It is a highly exothermic reaction between a metal oxide and aluminum powder.
Aluminum acts as a strong reducing agent due to its high reactivity.
For example, iron oxide reacts with aluminum to produce molten iron and alumina.
The heat released is so intense that the iron is produced in a liquid state.
This reaction is commonly used for welding broken railway tracks on-site.
It is an effective way to extract metals from oxides that are hard to reduce with carbon.
Question 21

How are less reactive metals extracted?

Metals like mercury and copper are found near the bottom of the reactivity series.
They can be extracted simply by heating their ores in the presence of air.
Cinnabar (HgS) is heated to form mercuric oxide, which then decomposes to mercury.
Copper glance ($Cu_2S$) is heated to form copper oxide, which reacts with remaining $Cu_2S$.
This self-reduction process yields copper metal without needing external reducing agents.
These metals are relatively easy to extract compared to more reactive ones.
Question 22

Explain the Refining of Metals (Electrolytic Refining).

[Image of electrolytic refining of copper]
Electrolytic refining is used to obtain highly pure metals from impure samples.
A thick block of the impure metal is made the anode in an electrolytic cell.
A thin strip of pure metal is made the cathode in the same cell.
The electrolyte is a soluble salt solution of the same metal being refined.
On passing current, pure metal from the anode dissolves and deposits onto the cathode.
Soluble impurities go into the solution, while insoluble ones settle as "anode mud."
Question 23

What is Corrosion of metals?

Corrosion is the slow degradation of metals due to reaction with environmental gases.
It occurs when a metal surface reacts with oxygen, moisture, and pollutants in the air.
The most common example is the rusting of iron, which forms a reddish-brown layer.
Silver articles turn black due to the formation of silver sulfide with $H_2S$ gas.
Copper develops a green coating of basic copper carbonate due to $CO_2$ and moisture.
Corrosion weakens the structural strength of metals and leads to significant economic loss.
Question 24

What are the conditions necessary for rusting?

Both air (oxygen) and moisture (water) must be present simultaneously for rusting.
Iron does not rust in dry air even if oxygen is plentiful.
Similarly, iron does not rust in boiled water that is covered with a layer of oil.
The presence of salts in water, like in seawater, speeds up the rusting process.
Acidic pollutants in the atmosphere also accelerate the rate of corrosion.
Rusting is an electrochemical process that occurs on the surface of iron.
Question 25

Explain Galvanization as a prevention method.

Galvanization is the process of coating iron or steel with a thin layer of zinc.
The metal is dipped into molten zinc to ensure a uniform coating.
Zinc acts as a sacrificial layer because it is more reactive than iron.
Even if the zinc coating is scratched, it continues to protect the underlying iron.
Zinc reacts with air to form a tough protective layer of zinc oxide.
It is widely used for iron pipes, roof sheets, and fencing wires.
Question 26

Explain Tinning (Kalhai).

Tinning is the process of coating a metal surface with a thin layer of molten tin.
It is traditionally used in India for copper and brass cooking utensils.
Tin is non-toxic and prevents the food from reacting with the copper or brass.
This prevents the formation of "verdigris," a poisonous green copper compound.
The process involves heating the vessel and rubbing tin over it with ammonium chloride.
Tinned vessels are safe for storing acidic food items like buttermilk or pickles.
Question 27

Explain Anodization.

Anodization is an electrolytic process used to thicken the natural oxide layer on metals.
It is most commonly performed on aluminum to increase its corrosion resistance.
The aluminum object is made the anode in an acid bath (like sulfuric acid).
During electrolysis, oxygen gas released at the anode reacts with the aluminum.
This forms a hard, uniform, and porous layer of aluminum oxide on the surface.
This layer can be dyed in various colors, making it useful for decorative purposes.
Question 28

What is Alloying?

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal.
Alloying is done to enhance the properties of the base metal, such as strength.
Stainless steel is an alloy of iron, chromium, and nickel that does not rust.
Brass is an alloy made of copper and zinc, used for making musical instruments.
Bronze is an alloy of copper and tin, used for making statues and medals.
Amalgam is a special type of alloy where one of the components is mercury.
Question 29

Why is pure Gold not used for making jewelry?

Pure gold (24 karat) is extremely soft and easily bends or breaks.
It is difficult to set gemstones securely into ornaments made of pure gold.
To make it harder, it is alloyed with small amounts of copper or silver.
Usually, 22-karat gold is used for jewelry, containing 22 parts gold and 2 parts other metals.
Alloying improves the durability and wear resistance of the gold ornaments.
It also slightly changes the color of the gold, depending on the metals added.
Question 30

Explain the role of Carbon in the reduction of metal oxides.

Carbon (in the form of coke) is a cheap and effective reducing agent for many metals.
It is used for metals that have a lower affinity for oxygen than carbon does.
During the reaction, carbon combines with oxygen from the metal oxide to form $CO$ or $CO_2$.
This leaves the metal in its free, reduced state.
It is commonly used in the smelting of iron, zinc, lead, and tin ores.
Highly reactive metals cannot be reduced this way because they bind too strongly to oxygen.
