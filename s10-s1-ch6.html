Chapter 06: Refraction of Light

Complete Point-Wise Study Guide | SSC Science 1

Question 01

Define Refraction of Light.

Light changes its direction when going from one transparent medium to another.
This change in the direction of propagation is called refraction.
It occurs because the velocity of light is different in different media.
Refraction only takes place when light enters the second medium at an angle.
If light enters a medium perpendicularly, it does not change its direction.
Refraction is the reason behind many optical illusions like the bending of a stick in water.
Question 02

State the first law of refraction.

The incident ray and the refracted ray are on opposite sides of the normal.
The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.
This plane is perpendicular to the interface of the two transparent media.
This law ensures the geometric consistency of light paths across boundaries.
It applies to all types of transparent interfaces, whether flat or curved.
The normal is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular to the point of incidence.
Question 03

State the second law of refraction (Snell's Law).

For a given pair of media, the ratio of $\sin i$ to $\sin r$ is constant.
Here, 'i' is the angle of incidence and 'r' is the angle of refraction.
This constant value is called the refractive index of the second medium.
The formula is expressed mathematically as $n = \sin i / \sin r$.
This law is not applicable for light incident normally (where $i = 0$).
It was experimentally derived by Willebrord Snell in the 17th century.
Question 04

Define Absolute Refractive Index.

The refractive index of a medium with respect to a vacuum is called absolute.
It is calculated as the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed in the medium.
The symbol used for refractive index is 'n' or '$\mu$'.
Since it is a ratio of similar quantities, it has no units.
The absolute refractive index of a vacuum is exactly 1.
For all other transparent materials, the absolute refractive index is greater than 1.
Question 05

How does the velocity of light change in different media?

Light travels at its maximum speed of $3 \times 10^8$ m/s in a vacuum.
In an optically denser medium, the velocity of light decreases.
In an optically rarer medium, the velocity of light increases.
The change in velocity is the fundamental cause of the refraction phenomenon.
Light slows down in water compared to air because water is denser.
Velocity is inversely proportional to the refractive index of the medium.
Question 06

Explain the behavior of light moving from a rarer to a denser medium.

Air is considered a rarer medium, while glass is a denser medium.
When light enters a denser medium, its speed decreases significantly.
Due to this reduction in speed, the light ray bends towards the normal.
In this case, the angle of incidence ($i$) is greater than the angle of refraction ($r$).
The refracted ray moves closer to the perpendicular line at the point of incidence.
This bending is predictable based on the refractive index of the denser medium.
Question 07

Explain the behavior of light moving from a denser to a rarer medium.

Glass is a denser medium, while air is a rarer medium for light.
When light enters a rarer medium, its speed increases.
Due to this increase in speed, the light ray bends away from the normal.
In this case, the angle of incidence ($i$) is smaller than the angle of refraction ($r$).
The light ray moves further from the perpendicular line at the interface.
This is the reason objects inside water appear closer to the surface.
Question 08

What happens when a light ray is incident normally?

Normal incidence means the light ray hits the surface at an angle of 90 degrees.
In this case, the angle of incidence ($i$) is exactly 0 degrees.
The light ray does not change its direction as it enters the second medium.
Therefore, the angle of refraction ($r$) is also 0 degrees.
Although the direction remains the same, the velocity of light still changes.
Snell's law cannot be used for calculation in this specific scenario.
Question 09

Why does a pencil appear bent when dipped in water?

Light rays coming from the submerged part of the pencil travel from water to air.
Water is a denser medium, and air is a rarer medium.
As the rays exit the water, they bend away from the normal.
These refracted rays appear to come from a point slightly above the actual position.
This shift creates a virtual image of the pencil that is higher than reality.
The combination of the real part and the virtual part creates the "bent" look.
Question 10

Explain the phenomenon of 'Apparent Depth'.

Objects underwater appear to be at a shallower depth than they actually are.
This is caused by the refraction of light at the water-air interface.
Light rays from the object bend away from the normal upon entering the air.
To our eyes, these rays seem to originate from a point closer to the surface.
The depth we see is called the apparent depth, while the true depth is real depth.
Refractive index can be calculated as: $\text{Real Depth} / \text{Apparent Depth}$.
Question 11

Why do stars twinkle?

[Image showing atmospheric refraction causing stars to twinkle]
Stars are point sources of light located very far away from the Earth.
Their light passes through various layers of the atmosphere with different densities.
The refractive index of these layers keeps changing due to air motion and temperature.
This causes the light to refract continuously and change its path.
The apparent position of the star fluctuates slightly and its brightness varies.
This constant fluctuation in position and intensity is perceived as twinkling.
Question 12

Why do planets not twinkle?

Planets are much closer to the Earth compared to stars.
They do not appear as point sources but as a collection of many point sources.
Light from different points on a planet undergoes atmospheric refraction.
Some points may appear brighter while others appear dimmer at the same time.
The average brightness and position of the planet remain steady.
Therefore, the twinkling effect is nullified and they appear to shine steadily.
Question 13

Explain Advanced Sunrise and Delayed Sunset.

We can see the sun slightly before it actually rises above the horizon.
This is due to atmospheric refraction of sunlight as it enters Earth's atmosphere.
The air layers near the Earth are denser than those higher up.
Light from the sun below the horizon bends towards the normal as it reaches our eyes.
This makes the sun appear to be above the horizon when it is still below.
A similar effect occurs at sunset, making the day about 4 minutes longer.
Question 14

What is Dispersion of Light?

Dispersion is the process of splitting a beam of white light into its component colors.
This happens when white light passes through a transparent medium like a glass prism.
White light is composed of seven colors: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, and Violet.
Different colors have different wavelengths and travel at different speeds in glass.
Each color bends at a different angle depending on its specific refractive index.
Violet light bends the most, while Red light bends the least during dispersion.
Question 15

Describe the spectrum of white light.

The band of seven colors obtained after dispersion is called a spectrum.
The order of colors from the bottom to the top is VIBGYOR.
Violet (V) is at the bottom as it undergoes maximum refraction.
Red (R) is at the top as it undergoes minimum refraction.
Each color in the spectrum corresponds to a specific range of wavelengths.
Sir Isaac Newton was the first to use a prism to obtain the spectrum.
Question 16

Why does Violet light bend more than Red light in a prism?

The refractive index of glass is different for different colors of light.
It is maximum for violet light and minimum for red light.
Velocity of light is inversely proportional to the refractive index.
Violet light travels slower in glass, so it deviates or bends the most.
Red light travels faster in glass, so it deviates or bends the least.
This difference in deviation is what causes the separation of colors.
Question 17

What is Partial Reflection?

When light travels from a denser to a rarer medium, part of it enters the second medium.
This part follows the laws of refraction and is called refracted light.
However, a small portion of the incident light turns back into the first medium.
This turning back of light into the original medium is called partial reflection.
This happens even if the medium is transparent, like water or glass.
The intensity of reflected light increases as the angle of incidence increases.
Question 18

Define Total Internal Reflection.

[Image showing total internal reflection at angles greater than the critical angle]
This occurs only when light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium.
As the angle of incidence increases, the angle of refraction also increases.
At a certain angle, the refracted ray travels along the interface ($r = 90^{\circ}$).
If the incidence angle is increased further, light does not enter the rarer medium.
Instead, the entire light ray is reflected back into the denser medium.
This complete reflection of light is known as total internal reflection.
Question 19

Define the Critical Angle.

The critical angle is a specific angle of incidence in a denser medium.
For this angle, the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is exactly 90 degrees.
At the critical angle, the refracted ray grazes the surface separating the two media.
The value of the critical angle depends on the refractive indices of both media.
If the angle of incidence is less than the critical angle, refraction occurs.
If the angle of incidence is greater than the critical angle, total internal reflection occurs.
Question 20

Explain the formation of a Rainbow.

A rainbow is a natural spectrum appearing in the sky after rain.
It is produced by the combined effect of dispersion, refraction, and internal reflection.
Tiny water droplets in the air act like small glass prisms.
Sunlight enters the droplet and undergoes refraction and dispersion first.
Then, it undergoes total internal reflection inside the back of the droplet.
Finally, light is refracted again as it exits the droplet toward our eyes.
Question 21

What is a Mirage?

A mirage is an optical illusion often seen on hot roads or in deserts.
It happens because the air layer near the hot ground is hotter and less dense.
The refractive index of air increases with height above the ground.
Light rays from a distant object (like a tree) bend upwards due to refraction.
This creates a virtual, inverted image of the object on the ground.
It appears as if there is a pool of water reflecting the object.
Question 22

How is refractive index related to the speed of light?

The refractive index ($n$) is a measure of how much light slows down in a medium.
The formula is: $n = v_1 / v_2$, where $v_1$ is speed in medium 1 and $v_2$ in medium 2.
For absolute refractive index, $v_1$ is the speed of light in vacuum ($c$).
The higher the refractive index, the lower the speed of light in that medium.
Light travels at $2.25 \times 10^8$ m/s in water, which has a refractive index of 1.33.
Light travels at $2 \times 10^8$ m/s in glass, which has a refractive index of 1.5.
Question 23

Define wavelength and its relation to color.

Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive peaks of a light wave.
Our eyes perceive different wavelengths of light as different colors.
The visible spectrum ranges from about 400 nm to 700 nm.
Red light has the longest wavelength, which is approximately 700 nm.
Violet light has the shortest wavelength, which is approximately 400 nm.
$1 \text{ nm}$ (nanometer) is equal to $10^{-9}$ meters.
Question 24

What are the conditions for Total Internal Reflection?

The light ray must travel from an optically denser medium to a rarer medium.
The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle for that pair of media.
The interface between the two media must be transparent.
Total internal reflection cannot happen if light travels from rarer to denser.
The intensity of the reflected ray is 100%, which is better than silvered mirrors.
This principle is used in optical fibers for high-speed data transmission.
Question 25

Explain the concept of 'Optical Density'.

Optical density is the ability of a medium to refract or bend light.
It is not the same as physical mass density.
A medium with a higher refractive index is said to be optically denser.
A medium with a lower refractive index is said to be optically rarer.
For example, kerosene has a higher refractive index than water but lower mass density.
Optical density determines the velocity of light within that material.
Question 26

Why do we see a spectrum when using a CD or a soap bubble?

These effects are often due to the interference or dispersion of light.
In a soap bubble, light reflects from both the inner and outer surfaces.
In a CD, the fine grooves act like a diffraction grating.
These processes separate white light into its component wavelengths.
The result is a rainbow-like pattern of colors visible on the surface.
This proves that white light is a mixture of multiple colors.
Question 27

What is the relation between frequency and refraction?

The frequency of a light wave is determined by its source.
When light moves from one medium to another, its frequency remains constant.
However, the wavelength and velocity of the light change.
The formula connecting them is $v = f \times \lambda$, where $f$ is frequency.
Since $f$ is constant, velocity ($v$) is directly proportional to wavelength ($\lambda$).
This constant frequency is why the color of light does not change during refraction.
Question 28

Why does the sun appear oval during sunrise and sunset?

This is caused by atmospheric refraction near the horizon.
Light rays from the lower edge of the sun bend more than rays from the upper edge.
This is because the air is denser closer to the Earth's surface.
The vertical diameter of the sun appears shortened due to this uneven bending.
The horizontal diameter remains relatively unchanged.
This results in the sun appearing flattened or oval instead of perfectly circular.
Question 29

Explain how refractive index depends on temperature.

As the temperature of air increases, its density decreases.
Hotter air has a lower refractive index compared to cooler air.
This creates a gradient of refractive index in the atmosphere.
This temperature variation is the primary cause of mirages.
It also contributes to the "shimmering" effect seen above a campfire.
Light rays curve as they pass through layers of air with different temperatures.
Question 30

What is a prism and its role in refraction?

A prism is a transparent object with at least two inclined polished surfaces.
It is typically made of glass and has a triangular cross-section.
When light enters a prism, it refracts at the first surface (air to glass).
It then travels through the glass and refracts again at the second surface (glass to air).
The total deviation of the light ray depends on the angle of the prism.
It is the primary tool used to study dispersion and the spectrum of light.
