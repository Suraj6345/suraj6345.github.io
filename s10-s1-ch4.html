1. What is the "Heating Effect of Electric Current" and how is it defined?

1
When an electric current flows through a conductor having high resistance, electrical energy is converted into heat energy.
2
The flowing electrons collide with the atoms of the conductor.
3
During these collisions, kinetic energy is transferred to the atoms.
4
This causes the atoms to vibrate and heat up.
5
This effect is mathematically expressed by Joule’s Law $H = I^2Rt$.
6
Heat depends on the square of the current, resistance, and time.
7
This principle is the foundation for household appliances like irons, heaters, and toasters.
8
This effect is undesirable in devices like computers or motors as it causes energy loss and damage.

2. Explain the construction and working of an electric bulb (incandescent).

1
A thin, coiled filament made of tungsten is used due to its high melting point (3410°C).
2
When current passes through the filament, it becomes white-hot.
3
The glowing filament emits visible light.
4
The bulb is filled with inert gases like argon or nitrogen.
5
These gases prevent oxidation and evaporation of the filament.
6
Most electrical energy converts into heat.
7
Only a small portion converts into light.
8
Hence incandescent bulbs are less efficient than LEDs.

3. What is a "Fuse" and why is it essential for domestic safety?

1
A fuse is a safety component made of a lead-tin alloy wire.
2
It has a low melting point.
3
It is connected in series with the live wire.
4
Excess current heats the fuse wire rapidly.
5
The fuse melts and breaks the circuit.
6
This stops the flow of electricity instantly.
7
It prevents appliance damage and electrical fires.
8
A blown fuse must be replaced with correct rating.

1

2

3

4

5
Intense heat is produced.
6
Overloading occurs when many high-power appliances are connected.
7

8

9

5. Define "Electric Power" and state its unit and mathematical formula.

1
Electric power is the rate of consumption of electrical energy.
2
It is calculated using $P = V \times I$.
3
SI unit of power is Watt (W).
4
One Watt means 1A current at 1V.
5
Using Ohm’s law, power can be written as $P = I^2R$.
6
It can also be written as $P = V^2 / R$.
7
Domestic power is measured in kilowatts.
8
1 kW = 1000 W.
