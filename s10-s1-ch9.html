Chapter 09: Carbon Compounds

Full Study Guide | SSC Science 1

Question 01

Why is carbon called a tetravalent element?

Carbon has an atomic number of 6, meaning it has 6 protons and 6 electrons.
Its electronic configuration is (2, 4), indicating 4 electrons in its outermost shell.
To achieve a stable octet, it needs to share 4 more electrons with other atoms.
It cannot easily lose or gain 4 electrons due to high energy requirements.
Therefore, it always forms four covalent bonds with other atoms.
This property of having four valency electrons is known as tetravalency.
Question 02

Explain the concept of Catenation in carbon.

[Image of carbon catenation straight branched and ring chains]
Carbon has a unique ability to form strong covalent bonds with other carbon atoms.
This leads to the formation of long chains, branched chains, or closed ring structures.
The carbon-carbon bond is exceptionally strong and stable compared to other elements.
This property of self-linking is what allows for the existence of millions of organic compounds.
No other element exhibits catenation to the same extent as carbon.
This property is the primary reason for the vast diversity of carbon compounds.
Question 03

Define Covalent Bonds.

A covalent bond is formed by the mutual sharing of valence electrons between two atoms.
It occurs when atoms need to achieve a stable electronic configuration (noble gas state).
Carbon forms covalent bonds because it cannot form ionic bonds effectively.
Single, double, and triple bonds can be formed depending on the number of pairs shared.
Covalent compounds generally have low melting and boiling points.
They are usually poor conductors of electricity as they do not form ions.
Question 04

What are Hydrocarbons?

Hydrocarbons are the simplest organic compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen.
They serve as the parent compounds from which other organic molecules are derived.
They are divided into two main categories: saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons.
Alkanes are saturated, while alkenes and alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons.
Most fuels used in daily life, like LPG and petrol, are mixtures of hydrocarbons.
They are generally insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents.
Question 05

Define Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes).

Alkanes are hydrocarbons where carbon atoms are linked only by single covalent bonds.
The general formula for the alkane series is CnH2n+2.
Methane (CH4) is the simplest member of the alkane family.
They are relatively less reactive and are often called paraffins.
In these molecules, the valency of every carbon atom is fully satisfied by single bonds.
They burn in air with a clean blue flame, indicating complete combustion.
Question 06

Define Unsaturated Hydrocarbons (Alkenes and Alkynes).

These contain at least one double or triple bond between carbon atoms.
Alkenes contain a carbon-carbon double bond and have the formula CnH2n.
Alkynes contain a carbon-carbon triple bond and have the formula CnH2n-2.
They are much more reactive than saturated hydrocarbons due to the multiple bonds.
They undergo addition reactions to become saturated compounds.
They usually burn with a sooty yellow flame due to incomplete combustion.
Question 07

Explain Structural Isomerism.

Isomerism occurs when compounds have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements.
For example, Butane (C4H10) has two isomers: n-butane and isobutane.
These isomers have different physical and chemical properties despite having the same atoms.
As the number of carbon atoms increases, the number of possible isomers grows rapidly.
Isomers can have straight chains or branched chains.
This phenomenon further contributes to the enormous number of carbon compounds.
Question 08

What are Functional Groups?

A functional group is an atom or a group of atoms that determines the chemical properties of a compound.
The reactivity of an organic molecule is centered around its functional group.
Common examples include -OH (alcohol), -CHO (aldehyde), and -COOH (carboxylic acid).
Compounds with the same functional group show similar chemical reactions.
The carbon chain length affects physical properties but not the basic chemical nature.
They are used to classify organic compounds into different families.
Question 09

Define Homologous Series.

It is a series of compounds with the same functional group and similar chemical properties.
Successive members in the series differ by a -CH2- (methylene) group.
The molecular mass of successive members differs by 14 units.
All members of a series can be represented by a single general formula.
There is a gradual change in physical properties like boiling point as mass increases.
Examples include the alkane series, alcohol series, and carboxylic acid series.
Question 10

Explain IUPAC Nomenclature.

IUPAC stands for International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry.
It provides a systematic way to name organic compounds based on their structure.
The name consists of a parent name (number of carbons) and a suffix (functional group).
Numbers are used to indicate the position of functional groups or branches.
It ensures that every unique compound has a unique and universally accepted name.
This system eliminated the confusion caused by common or "trivial" names.
Question 11

Describe the Alcohol Functional Group.

Alcohols contain the hydroxyl (-OH) group attached to a carbon atom.
Their names end with the suffix "-ol" (e.g., Methanol, Ethanol).
They are generally soluble in water due to the ability to form hydrogen bonds.
Ethanol is the most common alcohol used in beverages and as a fuel additive.
Higher alcohols are solids, while lower alcohols are volatile liquids.
They react with sodium metal to release hydrogen gas.
Question 12

Describe the Aldehyde and Ketone Groups.

Both contain the carbonyl group (C=O), but in different positions.
In aldehydes (-CHO), the carbonyl group is at the end of the chain.
In ketones (C=O), the carbonyl group is in the middle, between two carbons.
Aldehydes end in "-al" (Ethanal) and ketones end in "-one" (Propanone).
Formaldehyde (Methanal) is used as a preservative for biological specimens.
Acetone (Propanone) is a common solvent used in nail polish removers.
Question 13

Describe the Carboxylic Acid Group.

These compounds contain the carboxyl group (-COOH).
Their IUPAC names end with the suffix "-oic acid" (e.g., Ethanoic acid).
They are weak acids and turn blue litmus paper red.
Ethanoic acid (Acetic acid) is the main component of vinegar.
They react with bases to form salt and water (neutralization).
When they react with alcohols, they form pleasant-smelling compounds called esters.
Question 14

Explain Combustion of carbon compounds.

Combustion is the process of burning a substance in the presence of oxygen.
Carbon and its compounds burn to release heat, light, CO2, and water vapor.
Saturated hydrocarbons undergo complete combustion with a clean blue flame.
Unsaturated hydrocarbons undergo incomplete combustion, producing a sooty flame.
If air supply is limited, even saturated hydrocarbons will produce soot.
Combustion is highly exothermic, which is why hydrocarbons are excellent fuels.
Question 15

Explain Oxidation reactions.

Oxidation in organic chemistry involves adding oxygen or removing hydrogen.
Alcohols can be oxidized to aldehydes and further into carboxylic acids.
Alkaline potassium permanganate (KMnO4) is a commonly used oxidizing agent.
Acidified potassium dichromate (K2Cr2O7) is another powerful oxidizer.
These reactions are used to convert ethanol into ethanoic acid on an industrial scale.
The color change of the oxidizing agent indicates that the reaction has occurred.
Question 16

Explain Addition Reactions.

Addition reactions are characteristic of unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes and alkynes).
Molecules like hydrogen or halogens are added across the double or triple bond.
This process converts an unsaturated compound into a saturated one.
Hydrogenation of vegetable oils into vanaspati ghee is a common example.
This reaction usually requires a catalyst like Nickel (Ni) or Platinum (Pt).
It is used to test for unsaturation using the bromine water test.
Question 17

Explain Substitution Reactions.

These are characteristic of saturated hydrocarbons (alkanes).
One or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by other atoms like chlorine.
The reaction with chlorine (chlorination) occurs in the presence of sunlight.
It is a chain reaction where all hydrogens can eventually be replaced.
For example, methane reacts with chlorine to form methyl chloride and HCl.
Since alkanes are stable, they do not react easily without an energy source like UV light.
Question 18

What are the properties of Ethanol?

Ethanol (C2H5OH) is a colorless, flammable liquid with a pleasant odor.
It is soluble in water in all proportions and has a boiling point of 78°C.
It reacts with sodium to produce sodium ethoxide and hydrogen gas.
Dehydration of ethanol with hot concentrated H2SO4 produces ethene.
It is used as a solvent in medicines like tincture of iodine and cough syrups.
Consuming even small quantities of pure ethanol (absolute alcohol) can be lethal.
Question 19

What is Denatured Alcohol?

To prevent the misuse of industrial ethanol, it is made unfit for drinking.
Poisonous substances like methanol or pyridine are added to it.
Often, a blue dye (copper sulfate) is added to make it easily identifiable.
This mixture is known as denatured alcohol or methylated spirit.
It is used primarily as a solvent and as fuel in spirit lamps.
Denaturation allows industries to get ethanol without paying the heavy taxes on liquor.
Question 20

What are the properties of Ethanoic Acid?

Ethanoic acid (CH3COOH) is a colorless liquid with a sour taste and vinegar-like smell.
Its melting point is 17°C, so it often freezes in cold climates (Glacial acetic acid).
A 5-8% solution of ethanoic acid in water is called vinegar.
It reacts with sodium bicarbonate to release CO2 gas (effervescence).
It is a weak acid but reacts with bases to form salts like sodium ethanoate.
It is used as a food preservative and in the manufacture of polymers.
Question 21

Explain Esterification.

Esterification is the reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol.
This reaction occurs in the presence of an acid catalyst like concentrated H2SO4.
The product formed is an ester and water.
Esters are characterized by their pleasant, fruity smells.
For example, ethanoic acid and ethanol react to form ethyl ethanoate.
Esters are used in making perfumes, flavorings, and synthetic fibers.
Question 22

Explain Saponification.

Saponification is the process of breaking down an ester using an alkali like NaOH.
The reaction produces an alcohol and the sodium salt of the carboxylic acid.
This sodium salt is what we commonly know as soap.
It is the reverse process of esterification.
In industry, fats and oils (which are natural esters) are treated with NaOH.
This reaction is fundamental to the large-scale manufacturing of soaps.
Question 23

What is the structure of a Soap molecule?

A soap molecule has a long hydrocarbon "tail" and a short ionic "head".
The hydrocarbon tail is hydrophobic (water-fearing) and oil-soluble.
The ionic head (-COONa+) is hydrophilic (water-loving) and water-soluble.
When put in water, these molecules arrange themselves into clusters called micelles.
The tails point inward towards the center, while the heads point outward into the water.
This structure is essential for the cleaning action of soap on greasy surfaces.
Question 24

Explain the Cleaning Action of Soap.

Dirt on clothes is usually oily or greasy and does not dissolve in water.
When soap is added, the hydrophobic tails of the micelles attach to the oil.
The hydrophilic heads stay in the water, pulling the oil away from the cloth.
Agitation helps the oil droplets break free and become suspended in the water.
These suspended oil droplets are then easily washed away with the water.
Soap works by acting as a bridge between the water and the oil.
Question 25

Why doesn't soap work well in Hard Water?

Hard water contains dissolved salts of calcium and magnesium.
Soap reacts with these ions to form an insoluble, sticky precipitate called "scum".
This scum wastes soap and sticks to the clothes, making them look dull.
Because the soap is used up in forming scum, it cannot create lather easily.
This makes cleaning very difficult and inefficient in hard water areas.
Detergents were developed specifically to overcome this problem.
Question 26

What are Detergents?

Detergents are ammonium or sulfonate salts of long-chain carboxylic acids.
Unlike soap, their charged ends do not form insoluble precipitates with calcium or magnesium.
This allows them to remain effective even in hard water.
They are generally stronger cleaning agents than soaps.
Most detergents are synthetic and derived from petroleum products.
They are used in shampoos, dishwashing liquids, and laundry powders.
Question 27

Define Polymers and Polymerization.

A polymer is a giant molecule formed by the repeated linking of small units.
These small individual units are called monomers.
The chemical process of joining monomers together is called polymerization.
Polymers can be natural (like cellulose, silk) or synthetic (like plastic, nylon).
Polyethene is formed by the polymerization of thousands of ethene molecules.
Polymers have high molecular masses and very different properties from their monomers.
Question 28

What is Polyethene (Polythene)?

It is a synthetic polymer made from the monomer ethene (CH2=CH2).
It is formed through an addition polymerization reaction under high pressure.
It is lightweight, flexible, and resistant to chemicals and moisture.
There are two main types: Low-Density (LDPE) and High-Density (HDPE).
It is used for making plastic bags, milk pouches, and squeeze bottles.
Its non-biodegradable nature poses a significant environmental challenge.
Question 29

Explain the importance of Macromolecules.

Macromolecules are very large molecules containing thousands of atoms.
They are essential for all life processes (e.g., DNA, proteins, carbohydrates).
Proteins are macromolecules made of amino acid monomers.
Polysaccharides like starch and cellulose are macromolecules made of glucose units.
Synthetic macromolecules include plastics, fibers, and synthetic rubber.
The study of carbon is central to understanding these complex structures.
Question 30

What are the environmental impacts of Plastics?

Most synthetic polymers/plastics are non-biodegradable, meaning they don't rot.
They accumulate in landfills and oceans, harming wildlife and ecosystems.
Burning plastic releases toxic gases and contributes to air pollution.
Microplastics enter the food chain, posing health risks to humans and animals.
Improper disposal leads to the clogging of drains and urban flooding.
Reducing use, recycling, and developing biodegradable alternatives are necessary solutions.
